## Post 1: Founder Story (Launch Announcement)

```
I watched a PhD student spend an entire weekend reading 30 papers
for her literature review.

By Sunday night, she could summarize each paper individually.
But when I asked "so what does the evidence actually say?" she
paused for 30 seconds and said: "I honestly don't know."

That moment stuck with me.

The gap between "I've read everything" and "I understand what the
evidence says" is the hardest step in research. And nothing is
designed to help with it.

Elicit helps you find papers. Zotero helps you organize them.
NotebookLM helps you ask questions about them. But none of them
help you synthesize across papers with structure and traceability.

So I built Lumen — an AI research workspace for evidence-grounded
synthesis.

Here's what it does differently:

- Every claim traces to a specific source, page, and direct quote
- Contradictions between sources are surfaced explicitly
- Evidence is tagged by confidence level (high/medium/low)
- You get structured outputs: claims map, gaps analysis, next steps
- A workspace agent helps you refine and challenge your synthesis

I'm not launching publicly yet. Instead, I'm looking for 30-50
researchers to be "Founding Researchers" — people who will use Lumen
for real research and help shape the product.

If you're working on a literature review, systematic review, or
evidence synthesis of any kind, this might be relevant to you.

Link to apply in the first comment.

#AcademicResearch #EvidenceSynthesis #ResearchTools
```

## Post 2: Problem Insight (Pre-Recruitment)

```
There's a dirty secret in academic research:

Most literature reviews bury contradictions instead of surfacing them.

Not intentionally. It's just that when you're reading 40 papers and
synthesizing by hand, it's nearly impossible to track every
disagreement. The human brain naturally smooths over conflicts to
create a coherent narrative.

But those contradictions are often the most important finding.

They reveal:
- Where the evidence is genuinely uncertain
- Where methodology differences drive different conclusions
- Where your argument is vulnerable to peer review criticism

I've been thinking about this problem for months while building a
research tool. The more I talk to researchers, the more I hear the
same thing: "I found out about a contradicting paper from a reviewer,
not from my own process."

We need better systems for evidence synthesis — not just better
search engines for papers.

Curious: for those of you who do evidence synthesis, how do you
currently track contradictions across sources?

#ResearchMethodology #SystematicReview
```
