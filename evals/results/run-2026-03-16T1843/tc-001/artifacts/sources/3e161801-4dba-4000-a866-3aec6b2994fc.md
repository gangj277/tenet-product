# Statistical Modeling: An Excursion Through 14 Topics

**Authors:** Reinhard Furrer and the Applied Statistics Group
**Year/Venue:** Version September 11, 2023

This document serves as a pedagogical resource for a lecture series on statistical modeling, offering a broad overview of 14 distinct topics. It aims to equip learners with a structured understanding of various statistical modeling techniques, from foundational concepts to advanced methods like neural networks and spatial statistics. The document emphasizes that models are useful approximations rather than perfect representations of reality and demonstrates the application of these techniques through numerous examples and R code.

## Key Claims & Findings

*   Statistical modeling is fundamentally about describing phenomena and predicting future states, with the understanding that "essentially all models are wrong, but some are useful."
*   Resampling techniques such as cross-validation and bootstrap are essential for validating models and assessing the variability of estimators.
*   Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is a valuable tool for dimensionality reduction by identifying directions of maximum data variability.
*   Clustering methods, including hierarchical clustering and k-means, group observations into homogeneous classes.
*   Generalized Linear Models (GLMs) extend linear models to accommodate non-Gaussian response variables, with logistic and Poisson regression being prominent examples.
*   Survival analysis is designed to model time-to-event data, accounting for censoring, utilizing tools like Kaplan-Meier curves and Cox proportional hazards models.
*   Time series analysis addresses autocorrelated data, with ARMA models being central to capturing temporal dependencies.
*   Spatial statistics deals with data exhibiting spatial correlation, employing geostatistical methods like kriging for prediction.
*   Extreme Value Theory (EVT) provides methods for modeling rare and extreme events.
*   Neural networks and deep learning offer powerful approaches for complex tasks, particularly with large datasets.

## Contribution to Research Frame

This source contributes to the research frame by providing a comprehensive overview of the foundational principles and diverse methodologies within statistical modeling. It implicitly supports the idea that model collapse, if it occurs in generative AI, is a deviation from ideal statistical approximation. The document's emphasis on model validation, the inherent "wrongness" of models, and the trade-offs in model selection (e.g., simplicity vs. accuracy) aligns with the frame's focus on the technical boundaries of model performance. The detailed exploration of various modeling techniques, including those that handle complex data distributions and temporal/spatial dependencies, offers a rich context for understanding the statistical underpinnings that might be affected by recursive synthetic training. The document's explicit acknowledgment of limitations, such as the difficulty in selecting the number of relevant PCs or the non-trivial choice of blocks in EVT, highlights areas where statistical approximation can be challenging, potentially informing the understanding of why model collapse might occur.
