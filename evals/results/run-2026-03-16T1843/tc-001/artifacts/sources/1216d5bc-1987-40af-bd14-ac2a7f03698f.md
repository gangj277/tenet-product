# Collapse or Thrive? Perils and Promises of Synthetic Data in a Self-Generating World

**Authors:** Joshua Kazdan, Rylan Schaeffer, Apratim Dey, Matthias Gerstgrasser, Rafael Rafailov, David Donoho, Sanmi Koyejo
**Year/Venue:** 2024 (arXiv preprint)

## Summary

This paper investigates the phenomenon of "model collapse" in generative models, where performance degrades over successive generations of synthetic data. The authors empirically demonstrate that a "replace" training workflow, which discards all previous data and trains solely on newly generated synthetic data, consistently leads to model collapse across multivariate Gaussian modeling, kernel density estimation, and supervised fine-tuning of language models. In contrast, an "accumulate" workflow, which retains all real and synthetic data, effectively avoids collapse. A hybrid "accumulate-subsample" approach shows intermediate degradation. The study also finds that the benefit of synthetic data is contingent on the amount of real data available, with synthetic data being detrimental when real data is abundant.

## Key Claims

*   The "replace" training workflow, where only newly generated synthetic data is used for training at each iteration, consistently induces model collapse across various task settings.
*   The "accumulate" training workflow, which retains all real and synthetic data for training, effectively avoids model collapse.
*   An "accumulate-subsample" workflow, simulating fixed compute budgets, results in slower degradation than "replace" but faster degradation than "accumulate."
*   The utility of synthetic data in reducing test loss is dependent on the quantity of real data; it can be beneficial when real data is scarce but detrimental when real data is ample.
*   The findings confirm and extend prior work on model collapse, suggesting that the "replace" and "accumulate" paradigms are broadly applicable.

## Relevant Evidence & Quotes

*   **On "replace" workflow causing collapse:** "we confirm that the training-workflow of replacing all real data by successive generations of purely synthetic data indeed suffers model collapse in all task-settings studied" (Abstract). Evidence includes diverging test losses (MGM, KDE, SFT) and diverging Wasserstein-2 distance.
*   **On "accumulate" workflow avoiding collapse:** "we consider the training-workflow of accumulating all prior data - both real and synthetic - and find that on all three task-settings, collapse does not occur: the test loss on real data stays bounded at reasonable levels." (Abstract). Evidence includes stable test losses and stabilized fitted means/covariances.
*   **On "accumulate-subsample":** "we observe slow and gradual rather than explosive degradation of test loss performance across generations." (Abstract). Figure 4 shows test losses are between "replace" and "accumulate."
*   **On the value of synthetic data:** "when real data are scarce, adding a small amount of synthetic data can reduce test loss, whereas when real data are ample, any synthetic data increases the test loss calculated on real data." (Abstract). Figure 5 shows that with 1024 real data points, adding synthetic data improves test loss, but with 4096 real data points, adding synthetic data degrades performance.

## Contribution to Research Frame

This source directly supports the inferred research frame that "Model collapse is a function of statistical approximation errors and variance loss that can be mathematically modeled and empirically delayed through strategic data hygiene and hybrid training regimes."

*   **Supports:** The paper provides strong empirical evidence for how different data evolution paradigms ("replace" vs. "accumulate") directly impact model performance, demonstrating that the *management* of data (hygiene) is key to avoiding collapse. The "replace" workflow exemplifies the accumulation of approximation errors, leading to collapse, while "accumulate" mitigates this. The findings on the value of synthetic data also align with the idea of statistical approximation errors and variance loss, suggesting that an imbalance can lead to degradation.
*   **Challenges:** While the paper strongly supports the idea that collapse can be delayed, it doesn't explicitly detail the mathematical drivers of "statistical approximation error" beyond the MGM case. It also focuses more on data management strategies than specific architectural interventions, though the "accumulate-subsample" method touches upon hybrid training regimes within compute constraints. The paper's conclusion that collapse "seems unlikely" under realistic accumulation scenarios challenges a more deterministic view of collapse as an inevitable mathematical certainty.
