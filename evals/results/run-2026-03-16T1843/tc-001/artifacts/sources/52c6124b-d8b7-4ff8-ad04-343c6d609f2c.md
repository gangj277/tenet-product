# Is Model Collapse Inevitable? Breaking the Curse of Recursion by Accumulating Real and Synthetic Data

**Publication:** Arxiv 2024

## Summary

This paper investigates the phenomenon of "model collapse," where generative models trained on their own synthetic outputs degrade in performance and diversity over time. Contrary to prior concerns about a "curse of recursion" leading to catastrophic feedback loops, the authors demonstrate that model collapse is not inevitable. They show that if synthetic data *accumulates* across training iterations, rather than replacing the original data, collapse can be avoided. This holds true across various model types, including transformers, VAEs, and diffusion models, and is supported by analytical results in a simplified linear model setting.

## Key Claims

*   Prior work suggested that training generative models on their own outputs leads to a "curse of recursion" and inevitable collapse.
*   This collapse occurs when training data is *replaced* at each model-fitting iteration with data generated by the preceding model.
*   However, if synthetic data *accumulates* across training iterations (i.e., is added to the existing dataset), model collapse is avoided.
*   This finding holds qualitatively for transformer-based language models (trained on TinyStories), VAEs (trained on images), and diffusion models (trained on molecular conformations).
*   Analytical results in a tractable setting of linear models confirm that data replacement leads to increasing test loss, while data accumulation results in a bounded test loss.
*   The findings suggest that accumulating synthetic data may prevent model collapse even in scenarios where synthetic data is heavily integrated into future training datasets.

## Relevant Evidence/Quotes

*   "Many prior works consider training models solely on data generated by the preceding model i.e. data are replaced at each model-fitting iteration. Replacing data leads to collapse, but isn’t done in practice."
*   "What happens if data instead accumulate across each iteration?"
*   "If data are replaced at each model-fitting iteration, then the models worsen over time - collapse! But if data accumulate, collapse is avoided!"
*   "The same results qualitatively hold for VAEs trained on images and diffusion models trained on molecular conformation generation. Replacing data leads to collapse, but accumulating data avoids collapse!"

## Contribution to/Challenge of Research Frame

This source directly challenges the inferred research frame's premise that "model collapse is a function of statistical approximation errors and variance loss that can be mathematically modeled and empirically delayed through strategic data hygiene and hybrid training regimes."

While the paper does acknowledge that *replacing* data leads to collapse (which aligns with the idea of approximation errors and variance loss), its core finding is that *accumulating* data avoids collapse. This suggests that the "curse of recursion" might be less about an inherent mathematical inevitability driven by approximation errors and more about the specific *method* of data handling during training. The paper's emphasis on data accumulation as a mitigation strategy offers a potentially simpler and more robust solution than the "strategic data hygiene and hybrid training regimes" implied by the research frame. It shifts the focus from complex data curation to a fundamental aspect of the training loop itself. The paper's findings suggest that the "curse" might be broken by a simple change in data management rather than requiring intricate interventions.
