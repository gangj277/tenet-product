# The Curse of Recursion: Training on Generated Data Makes Models Forget

**Authors:** Ilia Shumailov, Zakhar Shumaylov, Yiren Zhao, Yarin Gal, Nicolas Papernot, Ross Anderson
**Year/Venue:** arXiv:2305.17493v3 [cs.LG] 14 Apr 2024

This paper introduces and defines the phenomenon of "model collapse," where generative models, particularly large language models (LLMs), suffer irreversible degradation when trained on data generated by previous model generations. This collapse results in the disappearance of the "tails" of the original data distribution, leading to models that misinterpret reality by reinforcing their own beliefs rather than accurately modeling the true underlying distribution. The authors demonstrate this phenomenon across various model types, including VAEs, GMMs, and LLMs, and posit that access to genuine human-generated content is crucial to prevent this decay.

## Key Claims

*   Training on model-generated content causes irreversible defects in resulting models, leading to the disappearance of tails of the original content distribution, a phenomenon termed "model collapse."
*   Model collapse is a ubiquitous phenomenon across all learned generative models.
*   Access to genuine human-generated content is essential to avoid model collapse.
*   Model collapse is distinct from catastrophic forgetting; it involves models misinterpreting reality by reinforcing their own beliefs rather than forgetting previously learned data.
*   Statistical approximation error and functional approximation error are the two main causes of model collapse.
*   For discrete distributions with perfect functional approximation, model collapse arises solely from statistical errors, leading to a delta function distribution.
*   For a single-dimensional Gaussian, both functional approximation error and sampling error compound to cause model collapse.
*   Fine-tuning language models does not curb the effects of model collapse.

## Relevant Evidence & Quotes

*   "We find that use of model-generated content in training causes irreversible defects in the resulting models, where tails of the original content distribution disappear."
*   "We build theoretical intuition behind the phenomenon and portray its ubiquity amongst all learned generative models."
*   "Indeed, the value of data collected about genuine human interactions with systems will be increasingly valuable in the presence of content generated by LLMs in data crawled from the Internet."
*   "Note that this process is different from the process of catastrophic forgetting in that we are considering multiple models over time, in which our models do not forget previously learned data, but rather start misinterpreting what they believe to be real, by reinforcing their own beliefs."
*   "There are two main causes for model collapse, one primary and one secondary, which we describe now."
*   Experiments with OPT-125m models show degraded performance and increased perplexity when trained on generated data, even with some original data preserved (Figures 10 and 11).
*   GMM fitting on generated data leads to misperception of the underlying distribution, with convergence to a state of very little variance by generation 2000 (Figure 7).
*   Latent reconstructions from VAEs show generated data becoming unimodal over generations, entangling different modes of the original distribution (Figure 9).

## Contribution to Research Frame

This source is foundational to the research frame. It directly addresses the core concern of "model collapse" by providing a theoretical and empirical basis for its existence and mechanisms. The paper's claims about the ubiquity of the phenomenon, its distinction from other forms of degradation, and the identification of statistical and functional approximation errors as root causes directly inform the frame's understanding of "inherent mathematical certainty." The evidence presented, particularly the mathematical derivations and experimental results showing performance decay and loss of distribution tails, supports the frame's hypothesis that recursive synthetic training can be catastrophic. The paper's emphasis on the need for "genuine human-generated content" also aligns with the frame's interest in "sustainable model training" and "data hygiene." This work provides the critical "why" and "how" behind the potential for model collapse, setting the stage for investigating mitigation strategies.
