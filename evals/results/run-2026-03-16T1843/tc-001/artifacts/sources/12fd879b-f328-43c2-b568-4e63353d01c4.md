# Knowledge Collapse in LLMs: When Fluency Survives but Facts Fail under Recursive Synthetic Training

**Publication:** NeurIPS 2025 Workshop on Evaluating the Evolving LLM Lifecycle: Benchmarks, Emergent Abilities, and Scaling

**Authors:** Figarri Keisha, Zekun Wu, Ze Wang, Adriano Koshiyama, Philip Treleaven

## Summary

This paper defines and investigates "knowledge collapse" in Large Language Models (LLMs) trained recursively on synthetic data. Knowledge collapse is characterized as a three-stage phenomenon where factual accuracy deteriorates while surface-level fluency is maintained, leading to "confidently wrong" outputs. The authors demonstrate through controlled experiments that the trajectory and timing of this collapse are critically dependent on the instruction format used during training. They propose domain-specific synthetic training as a mitigation strategy, showing it significantly improves collapse resistance. The research also introduces an evaluation framework to detect these degradation phases, offering practical guidance for sustainable AI training in accuracy-critical domains.

## Key Claims

*   Recursive training on synthetic data can lead to "knowledge collapse," a process where factual accuracy degrades while fluency persists.
*   Knowledge collapse is a distinct three-stage phenomenon: Knowledge Preservation, Knowledge Collapse (the "confidently wrong" transition), and Instruction-following Collapse.
*   The collapse trajectory is conditional on the instruction format, unlike traditional prompt-agnostic model collapse.
*   Domain-specific synthetic training is an effective mitigation strategy, significantly delaying accuracy decay.
*   An evaluation framework combining model-centric and task-centric metrics can detect distinct phases of epistemic deterioration.

## Contribution to Research Frame

This source directly supports the inferred research frame by providing empirical evidence and a theoretical definition for model collapse as a function of recursive synthetic training. It quantifies the "statistical approximation errors" through the observed degradation of factual accuracy and the loss of "tails" of the data distribution. The paper explicitly models the "variance loss" by showing how models converge to repetitive outputs and lose diversity. Furthermore, it validates mitigation strategies like "data mixing" (implied by the varying synthetic fractions) and proposes "domain-specific synthetic training" as a method to delay collapse, aligning with the idea of strategic data hygiene and hybrid training regimes. The conditional nature of collapse based on instruction format adds a nuanced layer to the understanding of these boundary conditions.
