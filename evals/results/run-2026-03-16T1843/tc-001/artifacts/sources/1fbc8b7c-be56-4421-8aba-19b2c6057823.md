# Full error analysis for the training of deep neural networks

**Authors:** Christian Beck, Arnulf Jentzen, and Benno Kuckuck
**Year/Venue:** January 31, 2020 (arXiv preprint)

This paper provides a theoretical framework for analyzing the total error in deep learning algorithms by decomposing it into approximation, generalization, and optimization errors. The authors aim to rigorously combine these components to establish an overall error estimate and convergence speed. While they successfully develop this framework, they acknowledge that their derived convergence speed is suboptimal, slow, and suffers from the curse of dimensionality, indicating limitations in their current analytical approach.

## Key Claims

*   There is a lack of a complete, rigorous mathematical error and convergence analysis for deep learning algorithms.
*   The total error in deep learning can be decomposed into approximation error, generalization error, and optimization error.
*   The paper presents a method to estimate each of these error components and merge them into a single overall error estimate with a convergence speed.
*   The established convergence speed analysis is noted as being far from optimal, slow, and significantly affected by the curse of dimensionality.
*   The primary contribution is the development of a comprehensive framework for analyzing all three sources of error in deep learning algorithms.

## Relevant Evidence & Quotes

*   "Nevertheless, there is no completely rigorous mathematical error and convergence analysis which explains the success of deep learning algorithms." (Motivation for the paper)
*   "The error of a deep learning algorithm can in many situations be decomposed into three parts, the approximation error, the generalization error, and the optimization error." (Foundational concept)
*   "In this work we estimate the approximation error, the generalization error, as well as the optimization error and we also combine these three errors to establish convergence with a suitable convergence speed for the deep learning algorithm under consideration." (Core contribution)
*   "Our convergence speed analysis is far from optimal and the convergence speed that we establish is rather slow, increases exponentially in the dimensions, and, in particular, suffers from the curse of dimensionality." (Acknowledged limitation)
*   Theorem 1.1 provides a specific quantitative error estimate, showing a probabilistic upper bound on the L¹-distance between the learned and target functions. The bound's dependence on dimension 'd' is exponential.

## Contribution to Research Frame

This source directly addresses the "statistical approximation errors" aspect of the research frame by providing a mathematical decomposition and analysis of errors in deep learning. It highlights that approximation error is one of three key components contributing to the overall error. The paper's findings, particularly the acknowledgment of the "curse of dimensionality" in their convergence analysis, suggest that while statistical approximation errors can be modeled, achieving optimal or efficient convergence in high-dimensional spaces remains a significant challenge. This aligns with the frame's focus on understanding the "technical boundary conditions" and the potential for "catastrophic" versus "sustainable" training, as the limitations identified could contribute to performance decay if not managed. The paper's theoretical approach to error decomposition provides a foundation for understanding the mathematical drivers of potential performance degradation in complex models.
