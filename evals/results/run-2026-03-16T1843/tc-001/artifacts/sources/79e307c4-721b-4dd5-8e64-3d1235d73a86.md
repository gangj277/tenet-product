# Collapse or Thrive? Perils and Promises of Synthetic Data in a Self-Generating World

**Authors:** Joshua Kazdan, Rylan Schaeffer, Apratim Dey, Matthias Gerstgrasser, Rafael Rafailov, David Donoho, Sanmi Koyejo
**Year:** 2024 (arXiv preprint)

## Summary

This paper investigates the phenomenon of "model collapse" in generative models trained on synthetic data. The authors empirically demonstrate that a "replace" training workflow, where previous data is discarded and replaced with newly generated synthetic data at each iteration, consistently leads to model collapse across various tasks (Multivariate Gaussian Modeling, Kernel Density Estimation, Supervised Fine-Tuning of Language Models). In contrast, an "accumulate" workflow, which retains all real and synthetic data, effectively avoids collapse. A hybrid "accumulate-subsample" approach shows intermediate degradation. The study also finds that the benefit of synthetic data is contingent on the amount of real data available, with synthetic data being detrimental when real data is abundant.

## Key Claims

*   The "replace" training workflow, where only newly generated synthetic data is used in each iteration, consistently induces model collapse across multivariate Gaussian modeling (MGM), kernel density estimation (KDE), and supervised fine-tuning (SFT) of language models.
*   The "accumulate" training workflow, which retains all real and synthetic data for training at each iteration, successfully avoids model collapse in all studied task settings.
*   An "accumulate-subsample" workflow, simulating fixed compute budgets by subsampling from accumulated data, results in slower degradation than "replace" but faster degradation than "accumulate," with test losses typically plateauing.
*   The utility of synthetic data for reducing test loss is dependent on the quantity of real data; synthetic data can improve performance when real data is scarce but degrades it when real data is ample.
*   The paper confirms and extends prior work by Gerstgrasser et al. (2024), showing that model collapse is primarily linked to the "replace" workflow and avoided in the "accumulate" workflow across a broader range of settings.

## Relevant Evidence & Quotes

*   "we confirm that the training-workflow of replacing all real data by successive generations of purely synthetic data indeed suffers model collapse in all task-settings studied" (Abstract)
*   Figures 1, 2, and 3 show diverging test losses (or negative log-likelihoods) for the "replace" setting across MGM, KDE, and SFT, with evidence of Wasserstein-2 distance divergence, rapid NLL increase, and cross-entropy increase, respectively.
*   "we consider the training-workflow of accumulating all prior data - both real and synthetic - and find that on all three task-settings, collapse does not occur: the test loss on real data stays bounded at reasonable levels." (Abstract)
*   Figures 1, 2, and 3 show stable or plateauing test losses for the "accumulate" setting.
*   Figure 4 illustrates that "accumulate-subsample" test losses fall between "replace" and "accumulate," with plateauing rather than diverging losses.
*   "when real data are scarce, adding a small amount of synthetic data can reduce test loss, whereas when real data are ample, any synthetic data increases the test loss calculated on real data." (Abstract)
*   Figure 5 (SFT, Gemma 2B) shows that with 1024 real data points, adding synthetic data improves test loss, but with over 1024 real data points, more synthetic data degrades performance.

## Contribution to Research Frame

This source directly supports the inferred research frame by providing strong empirical evidence that **model collapse is a function of statistical approximation errors and variance loss that can be mathematically modeled and empirically delayed through strategic data hygiene and hybrid training regimes.**

*   **Supports "statistical approximation errors and variance loss":** The "replace" workflow demonstrably leads to divergence in key statistical measures (e.g., Wasserstein-2 distance, NLL, cross-entropy), indicating accumulated approximation errors and loss of variance. The "accumulate" workflow, by contrast, shows stable metrics, suggesting these errors are managed.
*   **Supports "mathematically modeled":** The paper includes theoretical analysis for MGM, formally proving divergence for the "replace" scenario and convergence for the "accumulate" scenario, directly linking the data workflow to mathematical outcomes.
*   **Supports "empirically delayed through strategic data hygiene and hybrid training regimes":** The core contribution is the empirical validation of different data management strategies. The "accumulate" workflow is presented as a successful "strategic data hygiene" and "hybrid training regime" (combining real and synthetic data) that prevents collapse. The "accumulate-subsample" workflow further explores a hybrid regime under compute constraints, showing delayed degradation. The findings on the interplay between real and synthetic data quantity also highlight strategic considerations.

This source challenges the notion that model collapse is an inherent, unavoidable mathematical certainty, instead framing it as a consequence of specific, potentially suboptimal, data management practices. It provides concrete evidence for the efficacy of the "accumulate" strategy as a mitigation technique.
