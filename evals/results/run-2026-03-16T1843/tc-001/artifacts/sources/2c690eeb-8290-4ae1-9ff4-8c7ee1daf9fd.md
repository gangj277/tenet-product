# A Tale of Tails: Model Collapse as a Change of Scaling Laws

**Authors:** Elvis Dohmatob, Yunzhen Feng, Pu Yang, Francois Charton, Julia Kempe
**Year/Venue:** PMLR 235, 2024 (Proceedings of the 41st International Conference on Machine Learning)

## Summary

This paper introduces a theoretical framework and empirical validation for "model collapse," a phenomenon where models trained on increasingly synthetic data cease to improve or even degrade in performance. The authors argue that synthetic data generation processes, such as top-p sampling, tend to narrow or "cut" the tail of data distributions, which are crucial for scaling laws and emergent abilities. This leads to a breakdown in traditional scaling laws, characterized by loss of scaling, shifted scaling with generations, and "un-learning" of skills. The paper proposes a "double scaling law" and a "triplet scaling law" to describe this behavior and demonstrates that mixing a small amount of clean data can mitigate collapse and induce a "grokking" phenomenon.

## Key Claims

*   Synthetic data can lead to "model collapse," where model performance deteriorates and stops improving with increased data.
*   Model collapse manifests as a loss of scaling, shifted scaling with generations, un-learning of skills, and grokking.
*   The "tail" of data distributions is critical for scaling laws; synthetic data generation often narrows this tail, causing collapse.
*   A "double scaling law" ($E_{test} = T^{-(\\beta-1)/\\beta} + k^{-(\\beta-1)}$) describes the error of models trained on AI-generated data, incorporating sample size (T) and tail cutoff (k).
*   Iterative generation of AI data leads to multiplicative degradation of scaling laws.
*   Mixing clean data with synthetic data can mitigate model collapse and induce "grokking."
*   Acquiring data from the original distribution's tail can help, but overshooting is ineffective.
*   A "Triplet Scaling Law" ($E_{test} \\sim T^{-(1-1/\\beta)} + d^{-c_q} + k^{-(\\beta-1)}$) accounts for sample size, embedding dimension, and tail cutoff.
*   The theoretical framework and findings are applicable to complex models like Llama2.

## Relevant Evidence & Quotes

*   **Theoretical Framework:** The paper develops theoretical models, including the Hutter LLM and Hutter++, to derive scaling laws for AI-generated data.
*   **Double Scaling Law:** Theorem 2.1 states: $E_{test} = T^{-(\\beta-1)/\\beta} + k^{-(\\beta-1)} = \\min(T, k^\\beta)^{-(\\beta-1)/\\beta}$. Figure 3 (Left) provides empirical confirmation.
*   **Multi-generational Collapse:** Theorem 2.4 suggests collapse occurs when $n \\gg (T_0/T)$, and Equation (13) shows $E_{test} = nT^{-c}$. Figure 3 (Middle) illustrates this degradation over generations.
*   **Grokking:** Theorem 3.2 describes grokking with tail cutting, with early-stage dynamics $E_{test} = T^{-(\\beta-1)/\\beta} + k^{-(\\beta-1)}$ and later-stage dynamics $E_{test} = (\\pi T)^{-(1-1/\\beta)}$. Figure 3 (Right) shows this predicted behavior, and Figure 7 and 34 demonstrate it with Llama data.
*   **GCD Experiments:** Figure 28 and 29 show that models trained on clean data continue to learn, while those trained on AI data plateau, indicating collapse. Table 2 and 3 highlight performance differences between AI-generated and correct data for GCD tasks.
*   **Llama2 Experiments:** Figure 33 shows loss decreasing but tapering off when finetuning Llama2 with generated data, and Figure 34 demonstrates grokking with mixed data.

## Contribution to Research Frame

This source directly supports the inferred research frame by providing a mathematical and empirical explanation for how model collapse occurs as a function of statistical approximation errors (specifically, tail narrowing) in recursive training loops. It quantifies this phenomenon through novel scaling laws (double and triplet scaling) and identifies critical thresholds related to data distribution tails. The paper also validates the effectiveness of specific interventions, such as data mixing, in delaying or mitigating collapse, aligning with the frame's focus on "strategic data hygiene and hybrid training regimes." The findings challenge the notion that scaling alone is sufficient, suggesting that the *quality* and *distribution* of data, especially the preservation of data tails, are paramount for sustainable model training. It directly addresses the subquestion about the mathematical drivers of approximation error and the critical thresholds for performance decay.
