# DATA VALUE IN THE AGE OF SCALING: UNDERSTANDING LLM SCALING DYNAMICS UNDER REAL-SYNTHETIC DATA MIXTURES

**Authors:** Haohui Wang, Lifu Huang, Edward Bowen, Alison Hu, Jingyuan Qi, Lecheng Zheng, Kevin Choi, Balaji Veeramani, Jianpeng Chen, Tyler Cody, Jun Wu, Dawei Zhou
**Year/Venue:** November 18, 2025 (arXiv preprint)

## Summary

This paper investigates the scaling dynamics of Large Language Models (LLMs) trained on mixtures of real and synthetic data. The authors identify a novel three-phase scaling behavior characterized by two breakpoints, reflecting transitions in how models learn head and tail knowledge. They propose a theoretical framework based on a generalization bound that reveals factors influencing performance in real-synthetic mixtures. From this framework, they develop an efficient data valuation method that accurately estimates data subset contributions without retraining, demonstrating superior correlation with ground truth performance compared to existing baselines and a unique ability to balance the valuation of head and tail classes.

## Key Claims

*   LLMs trained on real and synthetic data mixtures exhibit a three-phase scaling behavior with two breakpoints, corresponding to learning head and tail knowledge.
*   A novel theoretical framework, derived from an LLM generalization bound, can reveal key factors governing generalization performance in real-synthetic mixtures.
*   An effective and efficient data valuation method, derived from the theoretical framework, can scale to large datasets without requiring retraining.
*   The proposed data valuation method achieves higher correlation with ground-truth performance than existing baselines.
*   The proposed data valuation method is computationally efficient.
*   Relative valuation scores derived from the proposed method are stable under subsampling.
*   The proposed method uniquely balances the valuation of head and tail classes in data, unlike baselines that focus on one or the other.

## Relevant Evidence & Quotes

*   **Three-phase scaling behavior:** "LLMs trained on real and synthetic data mixtures exhibit a three-phase scaling behavior characterized by two breakpoints, reflecting transitions in model behavior across learning head and tail knowledge." (Empirically validated on CIFAR-100 with simulated long-tail distribution, Figures 3, 4, 5, 6).
*   **Data Valuation Performance:** "For example, on sentiment classification with Qwen3-1.7B, the proposed method achieves a Spearman correlation of 0.81, while the next best is 0.32." (Table 1).
*   **Computational Efficiency:** "Figure 8 shows runtimes for data valuation methods on image classification, with the proposed method taking an average of 8 seconds, significantly faster than Retrain (627 seconds), Deviation (553 seconds), and TRAK (166 seconds)."
*   **Balanced Valuation:** "Figure 9 visualizes top-ranked contributors for image classification, showing that the proposed method selects a mix of head and tail classes, while LOGRA, DAVINZ, and Deviation focus on head classes, and TracIn and TRAK prioritize tail classes."

## Contribution to Research Frame

This source directly contributes to the research frame by providing a technical understanding of how to manage and evaluate data mixtures in LLM training. The identified "three-phase scaling behavior" and the proposed data valuation framework offer insights into the *statistical approximation errors* and *variance loss* that can occur when mixing real and synthetic data, particularly concerning the "long-tail" of knowledge.

The paper's proposed data valuation method, which balances head and tail classes and is computationally efficient, aligns with the frame's goal of finding *sustainable* training regimes. It suggests that a nuanced approach to data valuation, rather than simply maximizing data quantity, is key to mitigating performance degradation. The findings on the stability of valuation scores under subsampling and the balanced selection of contributors offer empirical support for the idea that these issues can be *managed through strategic data hygiene and hybrid training regimes*.

While the paper doesn't explicitly discuss "model collapse" in the context of recursive synthetic training, its findings on the challenges of real-synthetic mixtures and the importance of valuing data subsets are highly relevant. The identified breakpoints in scaling behavior could be interpreted as early indicators of potential performance degradation if not managed correctly, echoing the concerns about *statistical approximation errors and variance loss* in the research frame. The paper's emphasis on a theoretically grounded, efficient valuation method provides a concrete tool for addressing these challenges, supporting the frame's objective of developing a *technical roadmap for sustainable model training*.
