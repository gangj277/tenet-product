## Is Model Collapse Inevitable? Breaking the Curse of Recursion by Accumulating Real and Synthetic Data

**Publication Info:** Matthias Gerstgrasser, Rylan Schaeffer, Apratim Dey, Rafael Rafailov, Dhruv Pai, Henry Sleight, John Hughes, Tomasz Korbak, Rajashree Agrawal, Andrey Gromov, Daniel A. Roberts, Diyi Yang, David L. Donoho, & Sanmi Koyejo. (n.d.). *Is Model Collapse Inevitable? Breaking the Curse of Recursion by Accumulating Real and Synthetic Data*. Stanford University.

**Summary:**
This paper investigates the phenomenon of "model collapse," where generative models trained on their own outputs progressively degrade in performance. The authors differentiate between two scenarios: data replacement, where new synthetic data replaces old data, and data accumulation, where new synthetic data is added to the existing dataset. Through both theoretical analysis of linear models and empirical studies with language models, diffusion models, and VAEs, they demonstrate that accumulating data effectively prevents model collapse. This is contrasted with the data replacement setting, which, as shown in prior work, leads to a linear increase in test error. The findings suggest that data accumulation is a robust strategy for mitigating model collapse in generative AI.

**Key Claims:**

*   Training generative models on their own outputs can lead to "model collapse," a progressive degradation of performance.
*   Previous studies on model collapse often assumed data replacement, not accumulation.
*   Accumulating data (adding synthetic data to existing real and synthetic data) prevents model collapse.
*   In a linear model setting, data replacement leads to linearly increasing test error, while data accumulation results in a finite upper bound for test error.
*   Empirical results across language models, diffusion models (for molecule generation), and VAEs (for image generation) confirm that data accumulation prevents model collapse.

**Contribution to or Challenge of the Research Frame:**

This source strongly supports the inferred research frame that model collapse is a conditional failure mode. Specifically, it provides direct evidence that the *method of data handling* (accumulation vs. replacement) is a critical factor determining whether collapse occurs. The paper's findings align with the frame's assumption that collapse is not inevitable and can be managed through engineering strategies, with data accumulation being a key one. It directly addresses the "mitigation feasibility" aspect by offering a concrete and empirically validated solution. The emphasis on the "ratio of real-to-synthetic data" is implicitly supported, as accumulating data inherently means the proportion of original data decreases over time, yet collapse is prevented, suggesting that the *process* of accumulation itself is beneficial, perhaps by maintaining a more stable overall distribution compared to strict replacement.
