# Optimizing Real-to-Synthetic Data Ratios for Enhanced Remaining Useful Life Prediction

**Publication Info:** Master Degree Project in Informatics with a specialization in Data Science, 2024/2025, Student: Marek Lukl, Supervisor: Alexander Karlsson, Examiner: Göran Falkman.

## Summary

This thesis investigates the optimal ratio of real to synthetic data for improving Remaining Useful Life (RUL) prediction models. Due to the scarcity of real run-to-failure data, synthetic data augmentation is explored as a solution. Experiments were conducted using LSTM-based models on the NASA C-MAPSS turbofan engine datasets (FD001 and FD004), employing Gaussian Noise Injection and Conditional Generative Adversarial Networks (cGANs) for data augmentation. The findings suggest that the optimal data mix is dataset-dependent, with simpler datasets benefiting from a higher proportion of synthetic data (40-45%) and more complex datasets showing a wider optimal range (10-50%). The augmentation method itself had minimal impact on this optimal range.

## Key Claims

*   Accurate RUL prediction is crucial for maintenance planning and cost-effective operations, but is often hindered by the scarcity of real run-to-failure data.
*   Synthetic data augmentation can enrich training sets by creating realistic failure examples, potentially improving RUL model performance.
*   The optimal ratio of real-to-synthetic data for RUL prediction models is dependent on the complexity of the dataset.
*   For simpler datasets (like FD001), a synthetic data proportion of 40-45% yielded the lowest prediction error.
*   For more complex datasets (like FD004), a wider range of 10-50% synthetic data showed optimal performance, with no single ratio clearly dominating.
*   The choice of augmentation technique (Gaussian Noise Injection vs. cGAN) had little effect on the optimal data ratio.

## Contribution to Research Frame

This source directly addresses the core of the research frame by investigating the "tipping point" where synthetic data quality might cause model degradation. It empirically demonstrates that the "ratio of real-to-synthetic data" is a critical factor influencing model performance, aligning with the frame's assumption that this ratio is significant. The findings that optimal ratios vary with "dataset complexity" and that augmentation method has less impact than the ratio itself contribute to understanding the "conditional" nature of model collapse, suggesting that it's not an inevitable outcome but rather influenced by data characteristics. The study provides empirical evidence for the "preservation of original data distributions" being a key concern, as different ratios yield different results. This work offers concrete data points for the "empirical studies showing decreased perplexity or loss of diversity in models trained on >50% synthetic data" and helps define the "critical threshold ratio of real-to-synthetic data required to maintain distributional stability."
