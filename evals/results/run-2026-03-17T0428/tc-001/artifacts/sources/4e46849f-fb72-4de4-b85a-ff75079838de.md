# How Bad is Training on Synthetic Data? A Statistical Analysis of Language Model Collapse

## Abstract

This paper investigates the phenomenon of "model collapse," where models trained on synthetic data generated by previous models experience performance degradation. The authors propose a statistical model to analyze this phenomenon, particularly focusing on language models. They demonstrate that training solely on synthetic data inevitably leads to model collapse. However, when real and synthetic data are mixed, they provide an estimate for the maximum amount of synthetic data that can be used before collapse becomes unavoidable. These theoretical findings are supported by empirical validation.

## Key Claims

*   Model collapse, defined as performance deterioration from recursive training on synthetic data, is a significant concern for future language models.
*   Training exclusively on synthetic data guarantees model collapse, leading to a loss of information about the original data distribution, particularly its "tails."
*   When real and synthetic data are mixed, model collapse can be avoided if the proportion of synthetic data remains below a certain threshold.
*   The paper introduces a statistical framework using a simplified language model (Softmax classifier for next-token prediction) to rigorously analyze the "fully synthetic" and "partially synthetic" training scenarios.
*   In the "fully synthetic" setting, total collapse (convergence to a Dirac mass) is unavoidable and occurs at a rate dependent on the number of generations, the synthetic sample size, and the initial distribution's diversity.
*   In the "partially synthetic" setting, the amount of real data injected can mitigate model collapse, with theoretical bounds provided for the deviation from the original distribution.
*   Empirical experiments using both the statistical model and realistic GPT2-style models validate the theoretical conclusions, showing that mixing real data stabilizes performance and prevents divergence.

*   "Specifically, we demonstrate that model collapse cannot be avoided when training solely on synthetic data."

*   **Definition 1 (Total Collapse):** "We say that total collapse occurs in the recursive training process if $p^{(m)}$ converges to some Dirac mass $\\delta_i$ for some token $i \\in [s]$ as $m$ grows."
*   **Theorem 1 (Control on Total Collapse):** States that in the Fully Synthetic setting, $S_m$ (sum of squared probabilities) and $P_m$ (probability of collapse) tend to 1 as $m$ increases, and the expected time to collapse is dependent on $n$ (synthetic sample size) and $S_0$ (initial distribution diversity).
*   **Theorem 3 (Model Deviation):** Provides an upper bound on the deviation $E||p^{(m)} – p^{(1)}||_1$ in the Partially Synthetic setting, showing that the amount of synthetic data ($n$) should be exponentially smaller than real data ($N$) to ensure $p^{(m)}$ stays close to $p^{(1)}$.
*   Experimental results show that in the Fully Synthetic setting, $||p^{(m)} – p^{(1)}||_1$ diverges over generations, while mixing with real data ensures stability.

## Contribution to Research Frame

This source directly addresses the core concerns of the research frame by providing a rigorous statistical and empirical analysis of **model collapse**, a key technical phenomenon related to recursive training loops.

*   **Mechanism of Distribution Shift:** The paper explicitly models and analyzes how training on synthetic data leads to a loss of statistical diversity, particularly the disappearance of "tails" of the original distribution. This aligns with the frame's focus on identifying specific mechanisms of distribution shift.
*   **Quantifying Collapse:** It offers theoretical frameworks (Theorems 1 & 3) and statistical measures ($S_m$, $||p^{(m)}||_\infty$, $||p^{(m)} - p^{(1)}||_1$) to quantify the extent and rate of collapse, moving beyond qualitative descriptions.
*   **Mitigation Strategies:** The research directly investigates the efficacy of a primary mitigation strategy: **data mixing** (the "partially synthetic" setting). It provides theoretical bounds on the ratio of real-to-synthetic data required to avoid collapse, directly addressing the "tipping point" concept.
*   **Conditional vs. Inevitable:** The findings strongly support the frame's assumption that collapse is **conditional rather than inevitable**. While collapse is guaranteed in the fully synthetic case, it is shown to be manageable and avoidable with sufficient real data in the partially synthetic case.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The paper bridges theory and practice by validating its statistical models with experiments on realistic language models, strengthening the evidence for the proposed mechanisms and mitigation effectiveness.

In essence, this paper provides foundational theoretical and empirical evidence for the existence, mechanisms, and manageability of model collapse, directly informing the research frame's investigation into the technical boundaries and feasibility of recursive training.
