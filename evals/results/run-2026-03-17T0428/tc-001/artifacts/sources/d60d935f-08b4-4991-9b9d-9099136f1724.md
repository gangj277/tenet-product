# Approximation Power of Deep Neural Networks: An Explanatory Mathematical Survey

## Summary

This survey provides a mathematical exploration of the approximation capabilities of deep neural networks, focusing on feed-forward and residual architectures. It examines how effectively these networks can approximate target functions and under what conditions they surpass traditional approximation methods. The survey formalizes neural network tasks as optimization problems, discusses the training process using stochastic gradient descent and backpropagation, and delves into practical considerations like activation functions and regularization. It also analyzes the density of neural networks within the space of continuous functions and presents an error-complexity analysis for ReLU and Fourier-type networks.

## Key Claims

*   Deep neural networks possess significant approximation power, capable of learning complex functions.
*   The nonlinear, compositional structure of deep networks is key to their expressiveness.
*   Neural network training can be formalized as an optimization problem, often solved using gradient descent and backpropagation.
*   Activation functions are crucial for introducing nonlinearity and controlling network output, with ReLU being a popular choice due to its efficiency and avoidance of vanishing gradients.
*   Deep ReLU networks have been shown to be dense in the space of continuous functions, indicating their ability to approximate any continuous function to arbitrary accuracy.
*   Error-complexity analysis provides insights into the trade-offs between approximation accuracy and computational cost for different network architectures and activation functions.

## Contribution to Research Frame

This source directly contributes to the research frame by providing a foundational mathematical understanding of how neural networks approximate functions. It offers theoretical underpinnings for the capabilities and limitations of deep learning models, which is essential for understanding phenomena like model collapse. Specifically, the discussion on density in function spaces and error-complexity analysis can help quantify the potential for distribution shift and the degradation of approximation quality when models are trained recursively on synthetic data. The detailed explanation of training mechanisms like backpropagation and the role of activation functions also provides context for how errors might propagate and amplify in recursive training loops.
