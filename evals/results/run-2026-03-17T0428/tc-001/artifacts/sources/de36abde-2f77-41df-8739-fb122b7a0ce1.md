# Approximation Error and Approximation Theory

**Publication Info:** MIT OpenCourseWare, Lecture Notes

**Summary:**
This document outlines the concepts of approximation error and approximation theory within the context of machine learning and function approximation. It decomposes generalization error into estimation error and approximation error, highlighting that approximation error is related to the choice of hypothesis space and the inherent complexity of the target function. The document explores how the rate of convergence of approximation error depends on the smoothness of the function and the dimensionality of the input space, introducing the concept of "n-widths" as a measure of approximation capability. It also discusses how certain neural network architectures can achieve dimension-independent convergence rates under specific conditions, effectively mitigating the "curse of dimensionality."

**Key Claims:**

*   Generalization error can be decomposed into estimation error and approximation error.
*   Approximation error is determined by the difference between the best possible approximation in a given hypothesis space and the true target function.
*   The rate of convergence of approximation error is influenced by the smoothness of the target function and the dimensionality of the input space.
*   For functions in $d$ dimensions with smoothness $s$, the approximation error often scales as $O(n^{-s/d})$, where $n$ is the number of parameters.
*   The "curse of dimensionality" refers to the slower convergence rates in higher dimensions, while the "blessing of smoothness" refers to faster rates with increased smoothness.
*   Certain neural network architectures (e.g., those studied by Barron, Girosi, and Anzellotti) can achieve dimension-independent convergence rates (e.g., $O(1/\sqrt{n})$) under specific conditions on the function space.
*   N-widths (Kolmogorov widths) provide a theoretical framework to measure the intrinsic complexity of function classes and the best possible approximation rates achievable by linear methods.

**Relevant Evidence/Quotes:**

*   "Generalization error = estimation error + approximation error"
*   "$\epsilon_n < \frac{\|f\|_{W_{s,2}}^2}{n^{2s}}$" (for 1D approximation of smooth functions)
*   "$\epsilon_n < \frac{\|f\|_{W_{s,2}}^2}{2n^{2s/d}}$" (for d-dimensional approximation)
*   "More smoothness $s \Rightarrow$ faster rate of convergence. Higher dimension $d \Rightarrow$ slower rate of convergence"
*   "The curse of dimensionality is the $d$ factor; the blessing of smoothness is the $s$ factor;"
*   Theorem (Barron, 1991): "we can find a neural network with $n$ coefficients... such that $\|f - \sum_{i=1}^n c_i \sigma(\mathbf{x} \cdot \mathbf{w}_i + \theta_i)\|_{L_2(\Omega)}^2 < \frac{c}{n}$. The rate of convergence is independent of the dimension $d$."
*   "We cannot expect to find an approximation technique that 'beats the curse of dimensionality', unless we let the smoothness $s$ increase with the dimension $d$."

**Contribution to Research Frame:**

This source directly addresses the core concepts of approximation error and its relationship to model complexity and data dimensionality, which are central to understanding the potential for model collapse. It provides a theoretical framework for analyzing how the choice of model architecture (hypothesis space) and the inherent properties of the data (smoothness, dimensionality) affect the achievable approximation quality. The document's discussion on dimension-independent convergence rates for certain neural networks offers a potential avenue for mitigating the "curse of dimensionality," which is a key factor in the degradation observed in recursive training. It frames the problem in terms of a trade-off between dimensionality and smoothness, suggesting that model collapse might be related to situations where the effective dimensionality of the learned distribution increases relative to its smoothness, or where the approximation scheme is insufficient. The mathematical definitions and theorems provide concrete tools to analyze the "tipping point" where approximation error becomes unacceptably large, potentially leading to collapse.
