# Per-Source Summary: The Curse of Recursion

**Document Metadata**
*   **Title:** THE CURSE OF RECURSION: TRAINING ON GENERATED DATA MAKES MODELS FORGET
*   **Authors:** Ilia Shumailov, Zakhar Shumaylov, Yiren Zhao, Yarin Gal, Nicolas Papernot, Ross Anderson
*   **Publication Info:** arXiv:2305.17493v3 [cs.LG], April 14, 2024 (Original version May 2023)
*   **Institutions:** University of Oxford, University of Cambridge, Imperial College London, University of Toronto, Vector Institute, University of Edinburgh.

**Summary**
This foundational paper introduces and defines "Model Collapse," a degenerative process where generative models trained on data produced by previous models lose track of the true underlying data distribution. The authors demonstrate that this collapse is characterized by the progressive disappearance of the "tails" (low-probability events) of a distribution, eventually leading to a state where the model produces outputs with little variance and no resemblance to the original human-generated data. Through mathematical proofs and experiments with GMMs, VAEs, and LLMs (specifically OPT-125m), the study concludes that model collapse is an inevitable consequence of recursive training unless models have continuous access to genuine human-produced data.

**Key Claims**
*   **Definition of Model Collapse:** A process where "generated data end up polluting the training set of the next generation," leading to a misperception of reality.
*   **Two Stages of Collapse:** 
    *   *Early Stage:* The model loses information about the tails (rare events) of the distribution.
    *   *Late Stage:* The model entangles different modes and converges to a distribution with very small variance (often a delta function).
*   **Primary Cause (Statistical Approximation Error):** Arises because the number of samples is finite; at each generation, there is a non-zero probability that rare information is not re-sampled and is thus lost forever.
*   **Secondary Cause (Functional Approximation Error):** Arises from the limitations of the model architecture (e.g., a neural network being insufficiently expressive or incorrectly extrapolating in low-density regions).
*   **Inevitability:** The authors argue that without a superlinear increase in the amount of data collected at each generation, the distance from the true distribution will diverge.
*   **Value of Human Data:** As AI-generated content saturates the internet, "genuine human-produced data" becomes increasingly valuable for sustaining model quality.

**Relevant Evidence and Quotes**
*   **The "Delta Function" Convergence:** "We are guaranteed to end up in a constant state, having lost all the information of the original distribution when the chain is absorbed."
*   **LLM Degradation Example:** In an experiment with OPT-125m, by Generation 9, a model prompted about English church architecture began outputting nonsensical lists of "black-tailed jackrabbits, white-tailed jackrabbits..."
*   **Mathematical Risk:** The authors derive that the variance of the estimated distribution in a 1D Gaussian random walk diverges linearly: $\text{Var}(X^n) = \sigma^2 (1 + \frac{n}{M})$.
*   **Data Mixing:** Even when mixing data from previous generations ($\alpha, \beta, \gamma$ parameters), the authors note that "changes are inevitable, although attenuated," unless the original data ($\gamma$) is heavily prioritized.

**Contribution to Research Frame**
*   **Technical Validity:** This source provides the core mathematical framework for the "inevitability" of collapse, supporting the frame's focus on statistical and functional approximation errors.
*   **Refutation of "Self-Correction":** The paper explicitly challenges the idea that models can "self-improve" in a vacuum, arguing that they instead "reinforce their own beliefs" and drift away from reality.
*   **Critical Ratios:** It suggests that to prevent the distance from the true distribution from becoming infinite, the sampling rate ($M_i$) must increase superlinearly (e.g., quadratically) over generations, which is likely unsustainable in real-world web-scraping scenarios.
*   **Distinction from Catastrophic Forgetting:** It clarifies that model collapse is a multi-generational population-level problem, whereas catastrophic forgetting is a single-model sequential task problem.
