# AI models collapse when trained on recursively generated data

**Publication Info:** *Nature*, July 2024 (Updated/Corrected March 2025).  
**Authors:** Ilia Shumailov, Zakhar Shumaylov, Yiren Zhao, Yarin Gal, Nicolas Papernot, and Ross Anderson.

### Summary
This foundational study introduces and defines "model collapse," a degenerative process where generative models trained on data produced by previous AI generations lose the ability to represent the true underlying data distribution. The authors demonstrate that recursive training leads to the disappearance of "tails" (low-probability events) and an eventual convergence to a distribution with unnaturally low variance that bears little resemblance to reality. Through mathematical proofs and experiments with Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs), Variational Autoencoders (VAEs), and Large Language Models (LLMs), the research concludes that without a steady influx of original human-generated data, the performance and diversity of AI models will inevitably degrade.

### Key Claims
*   **Definition of Model Collapse:** A process where models "misperceive reality" because their training sets are polluted by the synthetic outputs of predecessors.
*   **Two Stages of Decay:** 
    * *Early Model Collapse:* The model loses information about the "tails" or rare/marginalized data points.
    * *Late Model Collapse:* The model converges to a delta function (a single point estimate) with nearly zero variance.
*   **Three Sources of Error:** Collapse is driven by *statistical approximation error* (finite sample sizes), *functional expressivity error* (model capacity limits), and *functional approximation error* (limitations in learning procedures/algorithms).
*   **Inevitability:** The authors argue that model collapse is a universal phenomenon across all learned generative models when trained recursively on their own data.
*   **Value of Human Data:** As AI-generated content saturates the internet, "genuine human interactions" and pre-AI data archives become increasingly valuable "clean" resources.

### Relevant Evidence and Quotes
*   **Mathematical Proof:** Theorem 3.1 (Gaussian model collapse) proves that as generations ($n$) increase, the variance ($\Sigma_n$) converges to zero with probability 1, and the distance from the true distribution diverges to infinity.
*   **LLM Performance Decay:** In experiments with OPT-125m, models trained on 100% synthetic data saw perplexity increase from 20 to 28 over generations, accompanied by a "long tail" of errors and repetitive gibberish.
*   **The "Tail" Problem:** "Preserving the ability of LLMs to model low-probability events is essential to the fairness of their predictions: such events are often relevant to marginalized groups."
*   **Data Mixing Mitigation:** The study found that preserving even **10% of the original human data** significantly slowed degradation, leading to "only minor degradation of performance" compared to total collapse.

### Contribution to Research Frame: "Managed Degradation"
This source provides the primary theoretical and empirical baseline for the "Managed Degradation" frame by identifying the **critical variables** that determine the speed of collapse:
*   **Validation of the Hypothesis:** It confirms that collapse is a technical reality driven by specific mathematical errors (statistical and functional).
*   **The "Critical Ratio":** The findings suggest that collapse is not an immediate "cliff" but a slope that can be managed. Specifically, the experiment showing that **10% real data** maintains stability supports the frame's focus on "data-to-noise ratios" as a mitigation strategy.
*   **Mechanism Identification:** By distinguishing between "early" and "late" collapse, the source allows researchers to look for early warning signs (loss of tail diversity) before total model failure occurs.
*   **Challenge to Mitigation:** The authors warn that "more approximation power can even be a double-edged sword," suggesting that simply making models larger or more expressive might actually accelerate the compounding of noise rather than preventing it.
