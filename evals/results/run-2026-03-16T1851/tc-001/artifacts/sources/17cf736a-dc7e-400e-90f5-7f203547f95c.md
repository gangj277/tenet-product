# DATA VALUE IN THE AGE OF SCALING: UNDERSTANDING LLM SCALING DYNAMICS UNDER REAL-SYNTHETIC DATA MIXTURES

**Authors:** Haohui Wang, Virginia Tech; Jingyuan Qi, Virginia Tech; Jianpeng Chen, Virginia Tech; Jun Wu, Michigan State University; Lifu Huang, University of California, Davis; Edward Bowen, Deloitte; Lecheng Zheng, Virginia Tech; Kevin Choi, Deloitte; Balaji Veeramani, Deloitte; Alison Hu, Deloitte; Tyler Cody, Virginia Tech; Dawei Zhou, Virginia Tech

**Date:** November 18, 2025

## Summary

This paper addresses the challenges of training Large Language Models (LLMs) on datasets composed of both real and synthetic data. The authors identify that synthetic data, while scalable, often underrepresents "long-tail" knowledge due to generation biases, leading to performance degradation. They propose a three-phase scaling behavior model (Rapid-Learning, Plateau, Tail-Learning) characterized by two breakpoints, which explains how LLMs learn head and tail knowledge. Furthermore, they derive a novel LLM generalization bound for real-synthetic mixtures and introduce a computationally efficient data valuation method based on this bound. Experiments across four tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of their method in identifying valuable data subsets and surpassing existing baselines.

## Key Claims

*   LLMs trained on real-synthetic data mixtures exhibit a three-phase scaling behavior: Rapid-Learning, Plateau, and Tail-Learning, marked by two breakpoints.
*   Synthetic data generation methods often truncate the long-tail distribution of real-world knowledge, exacerbating imbalances.
*   A novel LLM generalization bound for real-synthetic mixtures is derived, highlighting the influence of empirical losses, distributional discrepancies, the Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK), and data composition.
*   A scalable, retraining-free data valuation framework is proposed, directly derived from the theoretical generalization bound.
*   This proposed data valuation method outperforms state-of-the-art baselines in identifying high-value data subsets with significantly lower computational cost.

## Contribution to Research Frame

This source directly addresses the core of the research frame by investigating the technical challenges and potential solutions for training LLMs with mixed real and synthetic data. It provides a theoretical framework (three-phase scaling, generalization bound) and a practical method (data valuation) to manage the risks associated with synthetic data, particularly the underrepresentation of long-tail knowledge.

*   **Supports the hypothesis that model collapse is a manageable risk:** The paper's focus on understanding scaling dynamics and developing data valuation methods implies that the issues arising from synthetic data are not insurmountable but rather require careful technical management. The three-phase model suggests that performance can be sustained or improved even with synthetic data, provided the training process is guided appropriately.
*   **Highlights the importance of data-mixing ratios and sampling methods:** The theoretical analysis and the derived generalization bound explicitly incorporate the proportion of real data ($\pi$) and distributional discrepancies, underscoring the significance of these factors in mitigating performance degradation. The identification of breakpoints related to sample size and $\pi$ directly relates to finding critical thresholds.
*   **Distinguishes theoretical proofs from empirical findings:** The paper presents both a theoretical generalization bound and empirical validation across multiple tasks, demonstrating the practical applicability of their findings. This aligns with the research frame's goal of distinguishing theoretical inevitability from empirical manageability.
*   **Provides a technical roadmap:** The proposed data valuation method offers a concrete tool for researchers and practitioners to guide training processes, identify valuable data subsets, and potentially improve training efficiency, contributing to a technical roadmap for sustainable recursive training.

The source challenges the notion of inevitable model collapse by offering a nuanced understanding of scaling dynamics and providing a method to actively manage the quality of training data, suggesting that with the right techniques, LLMs can continue to scale effectively even with significant synthetic data components.
