# A new approach to generalisation error of machine learning algorithms: Estimates and convergence

**Authors:** Michail Loulakis, Charalambos G. Makridakis
**Affiliation:** National Technical University of Athens, IACM-FORTH, University of Crete, University of Sussex
**Contact:** LOULAKIS@MAIL.NTUA.GR, C.G.MAKRIDAKIS@IACM.FORTH.GR

## Summary

This paper introduces a novel approach to estimating and analyzing the generalization error and convergence of deep neural network (DNN) interpolants. The authors consider a model problem where a function is learned from point values, and the DNN interpolant is obtained through an exact optimization step. Their key contributions include providing error estimates without structural assumptions on the neural networks, relying only on mild regularity assumptions for the target function. They also establish convergence of the approximations to the target function, requiring only that the neural network spaces possess adequate approximation capabilities. The analysis leverages optimal transport theory and Wasserstein distances for error estimation and adapts concepts from $\Gamma$-convergence for convergence analysis, offering insights into the robustness of these learning algorithms.

## Key Claims

*   A new approach to estimating generalization error in DNNs is proposed, utilizing empirical measures and Wasserstein distances.
*   Generalization error estimates are provided without structural assumptions on the neural networks, under mild regularity assumptions on the target function $f$.
*   Convergence of DNN approximations to the target function $f$ is shown, requiring only that the neural network spaces have appropriate approximation capabilities.
*   The analysis decouples the influence of approximation capacity of the DNN space and the richness of training data.
*   The Wasserstein distance framework is used to bound the generalization error, showing a dependence on $N^{-p/d}$ where $N$ is the number of samples and $d$ is the dimension.
*   Convergence of the interpolants to the target function is demonstrated using a $\Gamma$-convergence-inspired framework, even for local minimizers, under minimal assumptions.
*   The authors emphasize that their results hold for generic neural network algorithms, demonstrating robustness.

## Contribution to/Challenge of Research Frame

This source directly addresses the research frame by providing a rigorous mathematical framework for understanding the generalization error and convergence of DNNs. It offers theoretical underpinnings for why these models can learn functions and under what conditions their approximations are reliable.

*   **Contribution:** The paper contributes by offering a new perspective on generalization error that does not rely on specific network architectures (like Rademacher complexity often does) but rather on the approximation capabilities of the function space and the statistical properties of the data sampling. The use of optimal transport and Wasserstein distances provides a powerful tool for quantifying this error. The convergence analysis under minimal assumptions supports the idea that these algorithms have inherent robustness.
*   **Challenge:** While not directly challenging the premise that model collapse is a risk, this paper provides tools to *quantify* and *understand* the errors involved in learning, which are the fundamental building blocks that could lead to collapse if not managed. By showing convergence under weak assumptions, it suggests that the learning process itself is stable, implying that issues like collapse might arise from specific data distributions or extreme parameter regimes rather than inherent flaws in the learning mechanism itself. The paper's focus on *interpolation* rather than *generation* means it doesn't directly model the recursive feedback loop of model collapse, but its findings on error bounds and convergence are foundational for analyzing such phenomena.
