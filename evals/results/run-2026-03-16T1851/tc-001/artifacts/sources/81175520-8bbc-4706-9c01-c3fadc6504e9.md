# Is Model Collapse Inevitable? Breaking the Curse of Recursion by Accumulating Real and Synthetic Data

**Authors:** Matthias Gerstgrasser, Rylan Schaeffer, Apratim Dey, Rafael Rafailov, Tomasz Korbak, Rajashree Agrawal, Henry Sleight, John Hughes, Dhruv Pai, Andrey Gromov, Daniel A. Roberts, Diyi Yang, David L. Donoho, Sanmi Koyejo

**Venue:** Arxiv 2024

## Summary

This paper investigates the phenomenon of "model collapse" in generative models, which occurs when models are trained on their own outputs. Prior research suggested this could lead to a catastrophic feedback loop. However, this work demonstrates that model collapse is not inevitable if data *accumulates* across training iterations, rather than being *replaced*. The authors show through experiments with transformer-based language models, VAEs, and diffusion models that accumulating data, both real and synthetic, avoids collapse, while replacing data leads to it. They extend this finding to an analytically tractable setting of linear models, where accumulating data bounds the test loss, whereas replacing data causes it to climb linearly.

## Key Claims

*   Training generative models solely on data generated by the preceding model (i.e., replacing data at each iteration) leads to model collapse.
*   If data instead *accumulates* across each training iteration, model collapse is avoided.
*   This phenomenon holds for transformer-based language models (trained on TinyStories), VAEs trained on images, and diffusion models trained on molecular conformation generation.
*   In an analytically tractable setting of linear models, replacing data causes test loss to climb linearly with iterations, while accumulating data bounds the test loss by a small constant.
*   The authors suggest that model collapse may be avoidable even in a future where synthetic data is abundant, provided data accumulates.
*   The authors explicitly state that accumulating synthetic data does not imply that synthetic data is broadly good for AI systems.

## Relevant Evidence & Quotes

*   "Many prior works consider training models solely on data generated by the preceding model i.e. data are replaced at each model-fitting iteration. Replacing data leads to collapse, but isn’t done in practice."
*   "What happens if data instead accumulate across each iteration?"
*   "If data are replaced at each model-fitting iteration, then the models worsen over time - collapse! But if data accumulate, collapse is avoided!"

## Contribution to Research Frame

This source directly supports the inferred research frame that "Model collapse is a manageable risk rather than a fundamental law of generative AI, contingent upon the ratio of 'fresh' real-world data to synthetic data and the specific sampling methods used."

Specifically, it provides strong empirical and analytical evidence that the *method of data handling* (accumulation vs. replacement) is a critical factor in preventing collapse, rather than an inherent inevitability of recursive training. The findings suggest that the "curse of recursion" can be broken by simply ensuring that previous data is not discarded. This challenges the notion that collapse is solely driven by distribution drift from synthetic data itself, highlighting the importance of the training *process* and data lifecycle. The paper's distinction between replacing and accumulating data offers a concrete strategy that aligns with the frame's emphasis on "data-mixing ratios" and "sampling methods" (implicitly, as accumulation is a form of sampling over time).
