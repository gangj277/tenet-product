# Collapse or Thrive? Perils and Promises of Synthetic Data in a Self-Generating World

**Authors:** Joshua Kazdan, Rylan Schaeffer, Apratim Dey, Matthias Gerstgrasser, Rafael Rafailov, David Donoho, Sanmi Koyejo
**Affiliations:** Stanford University (Statistics and Computer Science)
**Publication Info:** Not explicitly stated, but presented as a research paper with an abstract and introduction.

## Summary

This paper investigates the phenomenon of "model collapse" in generative AI, where models trained on data generated by previous models can degrade in performance over successive iterations. The authors explore three different data training workflows: replacing all data with synthetic data, accumulating all real and synthetic data, and a more realistic "fixed compute" workflow that subsamples from an accumulating dataset. Through experiments in multivariate Gaussian estimation, kernel density estimation, and language model fine-tuning, they confirm that the "replace" workflow leads to collapse. However, the "accumulate" workflow, and even the "fixed compute" workflow, demonstrate containment of model collapse, suggesting that model collapse is not an inevitable outcome. The study also finds that the value of synthetic data depends on the scarcity of real data: it can improve performance when real data is limited but degrade it when real data is abundant.

## Key Claims

*   The "replace" training workflow, where all previous data is discarded and replaced with newly generated synthetic data at each iteration, leads to model collapse across various generative modeling tasks.
*   The "accumulate" training workflow, where all real and synthetic data are retained and used for subsequent training, effectively prevents model collapse, with test losses remaining bounded.
*   A "fixed compute" workflow, which involves accumulating data but subsampling a fixed-size dataset for each training iteration, also contains model collapse, though performance may plateau at a higher loss than the "accumulate" workflow.
*   The effectiveness of synthetic data in improving model performance is context-dependent: it can be beneficial when real data is scarce but detrimental when real data is abundant.
*   Both the cardinality (number) and proportion of real data significantly impact test loss, with a complex interaction between real and synthetic data.

## Relevant Evidence/Quotes

*   "we confirm that the training-workflow of replacing all real data by successive generations of purely synthetic data indeed suffers model collapse in all task-settings studied"
*   "we consider the training-workflow of accumulating synthetic data alongside real data and training on all data combined and confirming that, although the proportion of real data eventually becomes zero, models remain stable and their test losses do not diverge under this training-workflow"
*   "In this workflow, we observe slow and gradual rather than explosive degradation of test loss performance across generations." (referring to the fixed compute workflow)
*   "when real data are scarce, adding a small amount of synthetic data can reduce test loss, whereas when real data are ample, any synthetic data increases the test loss calculated on real data."

## Contribution to Research Frame

This source directly supports the inferred research frame that "Model collapse is a manageable risk rather than a fundamental law of generative AI, contingent upon the ratio of 'fresh' real-world data to synthetic data and the specific sampling methods used."

*   **Supports:** The paper provides strong empirical evidence for the manageability of model collapse by demonstrating that different data handling strategies ("accumulate" and "fixed compute" workflows) successfully prevent it, contrary to the dire predictions of the "replace" workflow. It explicitly shows that the *way* data is used and accumulated is key, aligning with the frame's emphasis on data ratios and sampling methods. The findings regarding the context-dependent value of synthetic data further reinforce the idea that collapse is not absolute but contingent on specific conditions.
*   **Challenges:** While the source doesn't fundamentally challenge the existence of model collapse (it confirms it under specific, unrealistic conditions), it challenges the notion that it is an *inevitable* or *unavoidable* outcome in realistic scenarios. It shifts the focus from an inherent flaw of generative models to a consequence of specific, potentially avoidable, training practices.
