# The Curse of Recursion: Training on Generated Data Makes Models Forget

**Publication Info:** arXiv:2305.17493v3 [cs.LG] 14 Apr 2024
**Authors:** Ilia Shumailov, Zakhar Shumaylov, Yiren Zhao, Yarin Gal, Nicolas Papernot, Ross Anderson

## Summary

This paper introduces and investigates the phenomenon of "model collapse," a degenerative process where generative models, when trained on data produced by previous generations of models, irreversibly lose information about the original data distribution. This occurs due to compounding statistical and functional approximation errors, leading to the disappearance of data distribution tails and eventual convergence to a low-variance point estimate. The authors demonstrate this effect across various model types, including Gaussian Mixture Models, Variational Autoencoders, and Large Language Models. They argue that to sustain the benefits of large-scale web-scraped data, access to genuine human-produced data is crucial, as data from human interactions with AI systems will become increasingly valuable.

## Key Claims

*   Training generative models on data produced by previous generations of models leads to "model collapse."
*   Model collapse is a degenerative process where models forget the true underlying data distribution, characterized by the disappearance of data distribution tails and convergence to a low-variance state.
*   This phenomenon is universal across different types of learned generative models, including GMMs, VAEs, and LLMs.
*   The primary cause of model collapse is statistical approximation error, arising from finite sample sizes, which is exacerbated by functional approximation error from model limitations.
*   Access to the original, genuine human-generated data distribution is essential to prevent or mitigate model collapse.
*   Data generated by LLMs will increasingly pollute the internet, making data about human interactions with AI systems more valuable for future model training.
*   Even fine-tuning LLMs with generated data does not prevent model collapse; models become vulnerable to producing more probable sequences from the original data while introducing their own errors.

## Relevant Evidence/Quotes

*   "We find that use of model-generated content in training causes irreversible defects in the resulting models, where tails of the original content distribution disappear. We refer to this effect as model collapse."
*   "We discover that learning from data produced by other models causes model collapse – a degenerative process whereby, over time, models forget the true underlying data distribution, even in the absence of a shift in the distribution over time."
*   "Statistical approximation error: this is the primary type of error, which arises due to the number of samples being finite, and disappears as the number of samples tends to infinity."
*   "Functional approximation error: this is a secondary type of error, which stems from our function approximators being insufficiently expressive (or sometimes too expressive outside of the original distribution support)."
*   "Indeed, the value of data collected about genuine human interactions with systems will be increasingly valuable in the presence of content generated by LLMs in data crawled from the Internet."
*   Regarding LLMs: "We find that over the generations models tend to produce more probable sequences from the original data and start introducing their own improbable sequences i.e. errors."
*   "To make sure that learning is sustained over a long time period, one needs to make sure that access to the original data source is preserved and that additional data not generated by LLMs remain available over time."

## Contribution to Research Frame

This source directly supports the inferred research frame by providing a technical explanation and empirical evidence for the phenomenon of model collapse. It establishes that recursive training on synthetic data *can* lead to degradation, thus posing a risk to model quality. However, it also suggests that this is not an inevitable "law" but rather a consequence of specific error compounding mechanisms. The paper's emphasis on the importance of preserving access to original data and the potential value of human-AI interaction data aligns with the idea that model collapse is a "manageable risk" contingent on data composition strategies. The distinction between statistical and functional approximation errors, and the observation that fine-tuning LLMs doesn't prevent collapse, offer specific technical insights into *how* and *why* collapse occurs, which can inform the investigation into "technical thresholds and data composition strategies." The paper challenges the notion that simply scaling up LLMs and training on more web data is a sustainable path without addressing this recursive feedback loop.
