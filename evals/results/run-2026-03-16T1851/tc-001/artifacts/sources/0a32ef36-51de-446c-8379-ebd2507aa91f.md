# A Tale of Tails: Model Collapse as a Change of Scaling Laws

**Publication:** Proceedings of the 41st International Conference on Machine Learning, Vienna, Austria. PMLR 235, 2024.

## Summary

This paper investigates the phenomenon of "model collapse" in the context of generative AI, where models are increasingly trained on synthetic data generated by other AI models. The authors propose a theoretical framework to understand how the presence of synthetic data alters established neural scaling laws. They demonstrate that synthetic data generation, through mechanisms like truncated sampling, tends to "cut off" or "narrow" the tails of data distributions. This shift in distribution leads to a decay in scaling laws, a loss of learned skills, and potentially complete model collapse. The theory is validated through experiments with a transformer model on an arithmetic task and a large language model (Llama2).

## Key Claims

*   The widespread use of AI models leads to the generation of synthetic data that increasingly populates training corpora.
*   Theoretical derivations of scaling laws assume heavy-tailed data distributions (e.g., power-law/Zipf).
*   AI models trained on heavy-tailed data tend to generate data with truncated or narrowed tails.
*   Mechanisms like truncated next-token prediction (top-p, top-k, temperature) in LLMs contribute to this tail modification.
*   This alteration of data distribution leads to a decay in scaling laws, loss of skills, and potential model collapse.
*   The authors develop a theoretical framework and validate it with experiments on arithmetic tasks and LLMs (Llama2).

## Relevant Evidence/Quotes

*   "Theoretical derivations of scaling laws always assume a heavy-tailed distribution (power-law, aka Zipf) on the input features (“heavy tail in, power scaling law out”)."
*   "Regenerating heavy-tailed data affects the distribution in two possible ways: (1) 'Cutting off' the tail of the distribution and/or (2) 'Narrowing' the tail."
*   "The mechanisms leading to this, apart from finite sampling bias... stem from deliberate choices in the generation algorithms of the models: in LLMs via truncated next token prediction at inference (e.g. selecting more likely tokens via top-p inf or top-k inf truncation, concentrating the probability distribution by lowering the temperature τ)."
*   "We discover a wide range of decay phenomena, analyzing loss of scaling, shifted scaling with number of generations, the 'un-learning' of skills, and grokking when mixing human and synthesized data."
*   Figure 2: Shows empirical evidence of truncated distributions for AI-generated data (Llama2-7B and GCD transformer) compared to original data.

## Contribution to Research Frame

This source directly addresses the core concern of the research frame: **model collapse as a manageable risk rather than an inevitable outcome**.

*   **Supports the frame:** The paper provides a theoretical and empirical explanation for *why* model collapse occurs, linking it to the degradation of data distributions and the subsequent breakdown of scaling laws. This aligns with the frame's understanding that collapse is a technical phenomenon.
*   **Provides nuance:** While confirming the risk of collapse, the paper's focus on *how* synthetic data generation modifies distributions (truncation, narrowing) and the mechanisms involved (sampling strategies) suggests that these are technical levers that could potentially be manipulated. The mention of "mixing human and synthesized data" hints at the possibility of managing the ratio of real to synthetic data, a key aspect of the frame's hypothesis about sustainable recursive training.
*   **Challenges/Refines the frame:** The paper emphasizes the *change of scaling laws* as the fundamental mechanism. This provides a more precise technical explanation for the degradation than a general statement about "statistical approximation errors." It also highlights that the *way* synthetic data is generated (e.g., sampling choices) is crucial, implying that not all synthetic data generation is equally prone to causing collapse. The frame's emphasis on "data-mixing ratios" is supported by the paper's findings on tail modification, suggesting that controlling this ratio is directly tied to preserving the data distribution's essential characteristics.
