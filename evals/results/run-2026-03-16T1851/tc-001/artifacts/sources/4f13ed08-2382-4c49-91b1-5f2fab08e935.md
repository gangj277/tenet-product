# How Bad is Training on Synthetic Data? A Statistical Analysis of Language Model Collapse

**Authors:** Mohamed El Amine Seddik, Suei-Wen Chen, Soufiane Hayou, Pierre Youssef, Merouane Debbah

## Summary

This paper investigates the phenomenon of "model collapse," where models trained on synthetic data generated by previous models degrade in performance and lose information about the original data distribution. The authors propose a statistical model for language models to analyze two recursive training scenarios: fully synthetic and partially synthetic (mixing real and synthetic data). They theoretically demonstrate that model collapse is unavoidable when training solely on synthetic data, but can be mitigated or avoided if a sufficient amount of real data is included in the training mixture. Empirical validations using both the statistical model and realistic language models support these theoretical conclusions.

## Key Claims

*   Model collapse, characterized by the disappearance of the tails of the original distribution and repetitive outputs, occurs when models are recursively trained on synthetic data.
*   Training *solely* on synthetic data inevitably leads to model collapse.
*   When training on a *mixture* of real and synthetic data, there exists a maximal amount of synthetic data below which model collapse can be avoided.
*   The rate of collapse in the fully synthetic setting is exponential with respect to the number of generations and polynomial with respect to the synthetic sample size.
*   In the partially synthetic setting, the deviation of the model's distribution from the initial generation ($p^{(1)}$) can be bounded, indicating that collapse can be attenuated by incorporating real data.
*   Empirical results with transformer-based models confirm that fully synthetic training leads to divergence, while mixing with real data ensures stability.

*   "we demonstrate that model collapse cannot be avoided when training solely on synthetic data."

## Contribution to Research Frame

This source directly supports the inferred research frame that "Model collapse is a manageable risk rather than a fundamental law of generative AI, contingent upon the ratio of 'fresh' real-world data to synthetic data and the specific sampling methods used."

*   **Supports the hypothesis:** The paper provides strong theoretical and empirical evidence that model collapse is *not* inevitable, particularly in the partially synthetic setting. The existence of a "maximal amount of synthetic data" below which collapse can be avoided directly addresses the contingency on data ratios.
*   **Technical Thresholds:** The paper quantifies the rates of collapse and provides bounds on deviations, offering technical insights into the thresholds. Theorem 3, for instance, suggests an exponential relationship between the amount of real data and the acceptable amount of synthetic data to maintain stability.
*   **Distinguishes theoretical vs. empirical:** It bridges theoretical mathematical proofs (Theorems 1 & 3) with empirical findings from realistic language models, showing that the theoretical predictions hold in practice.
*   **Data Composition:** The distinction between "Fully Synthetic" and "Partially Synthetic" training directly investigates the impact of data composition on model collapse.

This source is crucial for understanding the technical underpinnings of model collapse and provides quantitative measures for managing this risk, aligning perfectly with the research project's goal.
