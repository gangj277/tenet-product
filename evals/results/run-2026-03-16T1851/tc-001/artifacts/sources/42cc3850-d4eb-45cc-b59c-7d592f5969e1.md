# COLLAPSE OR THRIVE? PERILS AND PROMISES OF SYNTHETIC DATA IN A SELF-GENERATING WORLD

**Anonymous authors**
*Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025*

## Summary

This paper investigates the phenomenon of "model collapse," where generative models trained on their own synthetic outputs degrade over time. The authors compare three scenarios: "Replace" (models trained only on synthetic data from the previous model, leading to collapse), "Accumulate" (models trained on all real and synthetic data generated so far, avoiding collapse), and a compromise "Accumulate-Subsample" (data accumulates but is subsampled to maintain a fixed compute budget, showing intermediate results). They find that collapse is avoided in the Accumulate scenario across various generative modeling tasks. The study also explores the interaction between the quantity and proportion of real data, finding that synthetic data can be beneficial when real data is scarce but detrimental when real data is abundant.

## Key Claims

*   Model collapse occurs when models are trained on datasets where past data is deleted and only synthetic data from the previous model is used ("Replace" scenario).
*   Model collapse is avoided when new synthetic data is added to the existing real data, allowing datasets to accumulate over time ("Accumulate" scenario).
*   This contrast between collapse and avoidance holds across multivariate Gaussian modeling, kernel density estimation, and supervised finetuning of language models.
*   In a "fixed compute budget" scenario ("Accumulate-Subsample"), where data accumulates but is subsampled, model performance degrades more than in the Accumulate scenario but avoids the divergence seen in the Replace scenario.
*   The value of synthetic data in reducing test loss is not uniform; it depends on the absolute quantity of real data.
*   When real data is scarce, a small amount of synthetic data can improve test loss.
*   When real data is plentiful, adding more synthetic data often harms final model quality.

## Relevant Evidence/Quotes

*   "We find the same contrast emerges in all three settings [Multivariate Gaussian Modeling, Kernel Density Estimation, Supervised Finetuning of Language Models]."
*   "model test loss on real data is larger than in the accumulate scenario, but apparently plateaus, unlike the divergence seen with replace."
*   "Surprisingly, we find a non-trivial interaction between real and synthetic data, where the value of synthetic data for reducing test loss depends on the absolute quantity of real data."
*   "when the number of real data is 1024 or lower, we find that there is an small but non-zero amount of synthetic data that improves the test loss when it is included."
*   "when real data are plentiful, we find that more synthetic data almost always harms final model quality when the number of real data is held constant."

## Contribution to Research Frame

This source directly supports the inferred research frame that "Model collapse is a manageable risk rather than a fundamental law of generative AI, contingent upon the ratio of 'fresh' real-world data to synthetic data and the specific sampling methods used."

The paper provides strong empirical and theoretical evidence that the "Replace" scenario, which leads to collapse, is not the only or even the most likely outcome. The "Accumulate" scenario, which aligns with real-world data dynamics (not deleting old data), demonstrably avoids collapse. This directly challenges the notion of collapse as an inevitable consequence of recursive training.

Furthermore, the findings on the interaction between real and synthetic data highlight the importance of the "ratio of 'fresh' real-world data to synthetic data" in managing this risk. The nuanced results—synthetic data being beneficial only in low-real-data regimes—suggest that careful data curation and strategy are key to preventing collapse, rather than simply avoiding synthetic data altogether. The paper's exploration of the "Accumulate-Subsample" scenario also touches upon practical constraints that influence the risk, suggesting that even with limited compute, collapse might be mitigated more effectively than in the worst-case "Replace" scenario.
