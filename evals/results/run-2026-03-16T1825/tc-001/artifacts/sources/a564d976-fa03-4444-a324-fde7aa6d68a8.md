## A new approach to generalisation error of machine learning algorithms: Estimates and convergence

**Publication:** arXiv preprint arXiv:2306.13784

**Summary:**
This paper introduces a novel approach to estimating and analyzing the generalization error and convergence of deep neural network (DNN) interpolation algorithms. The authors consider a model problem where a function is learned from point values on a finite set of points using a DNN. They propose methods to estimate the generalization error without making structural assumptions on the neural networks, relying instead on mild regularity assumptions on the target function. The work also provides convergence results, showing that approximations converge to the target function if the DNN spaces have appropriate approximation capabilities. This approach utilizes concepts from optimal transport, specifically Wasserstein distances, to bound the error and analyze convergence, offering a new perspective on the robustness and behavior of these learning algorithms.

**Key Claims:**

*   A new approach to estimating generalization error in DNN interpolation is presented, requiring only mild regularity assumptions on the target function and no structural assumptions on the DNNs.
*   Convergence of DNN approximations to the target function can be shown by requiring only that the neural network spaces possess adequate approximation capabilities.
*   The generalization error can be estimated using Wasserstein distances between empirical measures and the true data distribution.
*   The proposed bounds for generalization error depend on the Lipschitz constant of the interpolant, not a uniform constant for the entire network space.
*   The asymptotic behavior of the generalization error is shown to be related to $N^{-p/d}$, where $N$ is the number of samples and $d$ is the data dimension.
*   Convergence of the interpolants to the target function can be established under minimal assumptions, specifically that the DNN spaces can approximate functions in $L_p$.
*   The analysis extends to local minimizers, showing stable behavior under certain conditions.
*   The authors leverage concepts from optimal transport and $\Gamma$-convergence from the calculus of variations to establish their results.

**Contribution to/Challenge of Research Frame:**

This source directly addresses the research frame's interest in the technical underpinnings of machine learning algorithms, specifically focusing on generalization error and convergence in DNNs. It offers a theoretical framework that moves away from structural assumptions on network architectures, which is a significant contribution. The use of optimal transport and Wasserstein distances provides a mathematical lens to analyze the error, potentially offering more robust insights than methods relying solely on Rademacher complexity or VC dimension, especially when structural assumptions are difficult to enforce. The paper's focus on convergence under minimal assumptions aligns with the goal of understanding the fundamental behavior of these algorithms. It challenges the notion that complex structural assumptions are always necessary for error analysis, suggesting that approximation capability and optimal transport metrics are key.
