# The Curse of Recursion: Training on Generated Data Makes Models Forget

**Publication:** arXiv preprint (Authors: Ilia Shumailov, Zakhar Shumaylov, Nicolas Papernot, Yiren Zhao, Yarin Gal, Ross Anderson)

## Summary

This paper introduces and defines the phenomenon of "model collapse," a degenerative process where generative models trained on data produced by previous generations of models experience irreversible performance degradation. This occurs because the models begin to forget the true underlying data distribution, with the "tails" of the distribution disappearing first, leading to a convergence towards a point estimate with very small variance. The authors demonstrate this phenomenon across various model types, including Gaussian Mixture Models, Variational Autoencoders, and Large Language Models, and provide theoretical intuition and mathematical proofs for its inevitability under recursive training conditions. They conclude that access to original, genuine human-produced data is crucial for sustaining the benefits of large-scale data training in the long term.

## Key Claims

*   **Model Collapse Defined:** A degenerative process where generative models trained on data from previous generations forget the true underlying data distribution.
*   **Mechanism:** This occurs due to compounding errors (statistical approximation error and functional approximation error) over successive generations.
*   **Ubiquity:** Model collapse affects a variety of learned generative models, including GMMs, VAEs, and LLMs.
*   **Consequences:** The "tails" of the original data distribution disappear first, leading to a convergence towards a narrow, potentially inaccurate, point estimate.
*   **Inevitability:** The process is shown to be inevitable even under near-ideal conditions for long-term learning.
*   **Mitigation:** Access to genuine, human-produced data is essential to avoid or mitigate model collapse.

## Relevant Evidence/Quotes

*   "We discover that learning from data produced by other models causes model collapse – a degenerative process whereby, over time, models forget the true underlying data distribution, even in the absence of a shift in the distribution over time."
*   "In early model collapse the model begins losing information about the tails of the distribution; in the late model collapse model entangles different modes of the original distributions and converges to a distribution that carries little resemblance to the original one, often with very small variance."
*   "Statistical approximation error – this is the primary type of error, which arises due to the number of samples being finite, and disappears as the number of samples tends to infinity. This occurs due to a non-zero probability that information can get lost at every step of re-sampling."
*   "Functional approximation error – this is a secondary type of error, which stems from our function approximators being insufficiently expressive (or sometimes too expressive outside of the original distribution support...)."
*   "The value of data collected about genuine human interactions with systems will be increasingly valuable in the presence of content generated by LLMs in data crawled from the Internet."

## Contribution to/Challenge of Research Frame

This source directly supports and elaborates on the core hypothesis of the research frame: that recursive training on synthetic data poses a significant risk to model diversity and accuracy, leading to a phenomenon akin to "model collapse." It provides the foundational theoretical and empirical evidence for *why* and *how* this collapse occurs, detailing the specific error mechanisms (statistical and functional approximation errors) that drive the degradation. The paper's emphasis on the loss of "tails" of the distribution and convergence to a point estimate aligns perfectly with the frame's concern about preserving distributional stability. Furthermore, its conclusion that "access to genuine human-produced content is essential" directly informs the frame's focus on mitigation strategies involving real-world data. This paper serves as a primary theoretical underpinning for the research, establishing the problem that the project aims to address and manage.
