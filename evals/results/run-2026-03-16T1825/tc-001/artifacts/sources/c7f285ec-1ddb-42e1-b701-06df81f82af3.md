# A Tale of Tails: Model Collapse as a Change of Scaling Laws

**Publication Info:** Proceedings of the 41st International Conference on Machine Learning, Vienna, Austria. PMLR 235, 2024.

## Summary

This paper investigates the phenomenon of "model collapse," where generative AI models trained on synthetic data generated by previous models experience performance degradation. The authors propose a theoretical framework analyzing how the introduction of synthetic data alters the scaling laws that govern model performance. They demonstrate that synthetic data generation, through mechanisms like tail-cutting and tail-narrowing, can lead to a loss of scaling, skill unlearning, and eventual collapse. The paper validates these theoretical predictions through experiments with transformer models and the Llama2 large language model, and also explores mitigation strategies such as data mixing.

## Key Claims

*   The widespread use of synthetic data in training corpora will alter established neural scaling laws.
*   Synthetic data generation often leads to "tail-cutting" (limiting rare outcomes) and "tail-narrowing" (concentrating probability distributions).
*   These alterations in data distribution cause a loss of scaling, where further increases in training data size do not improve performance.
*   Recursive generation of synthetic data across multiple generations exacerbates performance degradation, leading to potential model collapse.
*   Mixing a small fraction of original, real-world data with synthetic data can mitigate model collapse and even lead to a "grokking" phenomenon where performance eventually recovers.
*   Curating specific parts of the original data's tail can help recover lost scaling, but requires careful selection of which tail segments to include.

## Relevant Evidence/Quotes

*   "We discover a wide range of decay phenomena, analyzing loss of scaling, shifted scaling with number of generations, the 'un-learning' of skills, and grokking when mixing human and synthesized data."
*   "Theoretical derivations of scaling laws always assume a heavy-tailed distribution (power-law, aka Zipf) on the input features ('heavy tail in, power scaling law out')."
*   "Regenerating heavy-tailed data affects the distribution in two possible ways: (1) 'Cutting off' the tail of the distribution and/or (2) 'Narrowing' the tail."
*   "Theorem 2.1. ... for large k and T samples from the AI, the test error of the downstream 'LLM' scales like $E_{\\text{test}} \\propto T^{-(\\beta-1)/\\beta} + k^{-(\\beta-1)} = \\min(T^{1/\\beta}, k)^{-(\\beta-1)}$." This indicates that as $T \\gtrsim k^\\beta$, performance plateaus, and scaling is lost.
*   "Theorem 2.4 (Informal). Model collapse (as spoken of in the literature) occurs iff $n \\gg (T_0/T)^c$." This highlights the impact of multiple generations ($n$).
*   "Theorem 3.2 (Grokking with Tail Cutting). ... For later-stage dynamics ($T > Ck^\\beta/\\pi$), it holds that $E_{\\text{test}} \\propto (\\pi T)^{-(1-1/\\beta)}$." This shows recovery of scaling with a proportion $\\pi$ of original data.

## Contribution to Research Frame

This source directly supports the research frame by providing a rigorous theoretical and experimental analysis of the mechanisms behind model collapse. It quantifies how synthetic data generation alters data distributions (tail-cutting, tail-narrowing) and consequently breaks the scaling laws that underpin the success of large models. The paper's findings align with the hypothesis that model collapse is a manageable risk, offering specific mathematical conditions ($n \gg (T_0/T)^c$) and empirical evidence for its occurrence. Crucially, it also provides evidence for mitigation strategies, such as data mixing (leading to grokking) and careful tail curation, which directly addresses the research frame's interest in identifying actionable strategies. The distinction between statistical approximation error and functional approximation error is implicitly addressed through the analysis of how distribution changes affect learning. The paper's focus on scaling laws and distribution tails directly speaks to the "preservation of distribution tails" and the "ratio of real-to-synthetic data" as key factors.
