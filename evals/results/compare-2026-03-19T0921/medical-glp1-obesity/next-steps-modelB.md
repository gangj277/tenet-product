### 2) Promising hypothesis refinements (based on current evidence)

1. **Refine efficacy hypothesis: “maximal benefit is dose-dependent, but tolerability constrains realized benefit.”**
   - Current evidence supports substantial average weight loss for both drugs.
   - Next hypothesis: **tirzepatide dose escalation yields higher average weight loss**, but the **net clinical effect** depends on GI-driven discontinuation; thus, outcomes should be reframed as:
     - efficacy among completers vs intention-to-treat
     - dose–response moderated by tolerability

2. **Refine safety hypothesis: “GI adverse events are a primary barrier; biliary risk may be the second major safety differentiator.”**
   - Evidence already flags GI intolerance and biliary risk.
   - Next refinement: quantify whether **biliary events differ meaningfully** between semaglutide and tirzepatide once studies adjust for baseline gallstone prevalence and exposure duration.

3. **Refine “independent cardiometabolic benefit” hypothesis: “cardiometabolic improvements largely track weight loss, but BP/CRP may show partial independence.”**
   - Existing findings show BP and CRP changes for semaglutide.
   - Next step is to test whether the same pattern holds for tirzepatide and whether any meta-analyses provide weight-adjusted estimates.

4. **Refine long-term hypothesis: “weight regain after stopping is likely, but the magnitude may depend on follow-up duration and post-cessation behavioral support.”**
   - Current evidence claims weight regain after withdrawal (semaglutide).
   - Next hypothesis: weight regain will be **lower** in studies with structured lifestyle follow-up and **higher** in purely pharmacotherapy-withdrawal settings.
