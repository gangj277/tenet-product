# Gaps in Evidence: GLP-1 Therapies for Obesity

This document outlines the current limitations in the literature regarding GLP-1-based therapies (semaglutide and tirzepatide) for obesity management in non-diabetic adults.

## 1. Unresolved Questions
These areas represent critical knowledge voids that prevent definitive clinical guidance:
*   **Long-term Durability:** While 68–72 week data is robust, there is a lack of high-quality evidence regarding weight maintenance and safety profiles beyond 3 years. The "chronic nature" of the therapy is established, but the long-term physiological consequences of multi-year administration remain unknown.
*   **Body Composition Dynamics:** The literature lacks granular data on the ratio of lean muscle mass loss to fat mass loss in non-diabetic cohorts. It is unclear if the observed weight loss is metabolically optimal or if it risks sarcopenic obesity.
*   **Oral vs. Injectable Efficacy:** It remains unresolved whether oral semaglutide formulations can achieve the same magnitude of weight reduction as the 2.4mg injectable, particularly in populations with higher baseline BMIs.

## 2. Contradictions
*   **Pancreatitis Risk:** There is a direct disagreement between clinical trial reports (e.g., AWARD-2), which flag specific cases of pancreatitis, and large-scale Cardiovascular Outcome Trials (CVOTs), which often report no statistically significant increase in risk. This leaves the absolute risk profile for the non-diabetic population contested.
*   **Efficacy in Diabetic vs. Non-Diabetic Cohorts:** While the efficacy in non-diabetic populations is well-documented, literature suggests that insulin resistance and chronic hyperglycemia correlate with diminished efficacy. The mechanism of this "diminished response" is not fully reconciled with the drugs' primary mode of action.

## 3. Weak Evidence Areas
*   **Study Heterogeneity:** Meta-analyses for tirzepatide exhibit high heterogeneity ($I^2 > 90\%$). This suggests that the reported efficacy is highly sensitive to trial design, baseline BMI, and the intensity of concurrent lifestyle interventions, making it difficult to generalize results to diverse real-world clinical settings.
*   **Rare Adverse Events:** Evidence for severe complications (e.g., gastroparesis, biliary disease) relies on sporadic reports rather than large-scale, long-term prospective surveillance. The current evidence base is skewed toward short-term gastrointestinal tolerability rather than long-term systemic safety.

## 4. What Would Change Confidence
To move from current observations to clinical certainty, the following evidence is required:
*   **Standardized Body Composition Metrics:** Future trials must include DEXA or MRI-based body composition analysis to quantify the impact on lean muscle mass, moving beyond simple total body weight measurements.
*   **Long-term Registry Data:** Real-world evidence (RWE) registries tracking patients for 3–5 years are required to resolve the uncertainty surrounding long-term safety and the true rate of weight regain post-cessation.
*   **Head-to-Head Comparative Trials:** Current confidence in the superiority of tirzepatide over semaglutide is largely derived from network meta-analyses. Direct head-to-head trials are necessary to confirm the comparative magnitude of weight loss and the relative incidence of Grade 3+ gastrointestinal adverse events.
*   **Standardized Lifestyle Controls:** To address the high $I^2$ values in meta-analyses, future research must standardize the "lifestyle intervention" component to isolate the pharmacological efficacy of the GLP-1/GIP agonists from behavioral variables.
