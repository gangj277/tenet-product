## gaps.md — Uncertainty Map for GLP-1 / Dual GIP-GLP-1 Therapies for Adult Obesity (Non-Diabetes)

### 1) Unresolved questions (what the evidence does *not* settle)
1. **Long-term safety (≥3 years) in non-diabetic adults**
   - *Gap:* Existing claims focus heavily on ~68–72 week RCT outcomes and meta-analyses that may not fully capture sustained rare adverse events over multiple years (e.g., pancreatitis, biliary disease requiring intervention, gallbladder surgery, severe GI complications, effects on cognition/neurologic outcomes).
   - *What to look for:* Follow-up extension trials and real-world cohorts with adjudicated outcomes in **non-diabetic** populations.

2. **Long-term weight maintenance and durability after stopping**
   - *Gap:* The literature summarized supports “chronic administration is needed” and that stopping leads to weight regain, but uncertainty remains about:
     - how rapidly regain occurs across different patient subgroups (baseline BMI, age, sex, lifestyle adherence),
     - whether maintenance strategies (dose reduction, lifestyle programs) mitigate regain,
     - comparative durability between **semaglutide** and **tirzepatide**.
   - *What to look for:* Discontinuation trials, long-term extension data, and modeling studies validated against observed relapse trajectories.

3. **Cardiovascular outcomes (MACE) specifically in non-diabetic obesity populations**
   - *Gap:* The synthesis indicates cardiometabolic benefits (BP, CRP, lipids), but does not establish whether these translate into **hard CV event reduction** in people **without diabetes**.
   - *What to look for:* CVOT-like evidence, event-driven trials, or large propensity-matched real-world studies that explicitly stratify by diabetes status.

4. **Lean mass vs fat mass effects**
   - *Gap:* The project notes potential muscle/lean mass loss, but the provided consolidated findings do not quantify lean mass changes for semaglutide vs tirzepatide in non-diabetic adults.
   - *What to look for:* Trials reporting body composition (DXA/BIA) and nutritional/protein intake outcomes; meta-analyses that separate fat mass and lean mass trajectories.

5. **Comparative adverse-event profiles at clinically relevant doses**
   - *Gap:* GI intolerance is described as the primary limiter for GLP-1 therapies, but uncertainty persists about:
     - dose titration speed,
     - rates of **grade ≥3** GI events,
     - discontinuation due to adverse events,
     - comparative tolerability between semaglutide 2.4 mg and tirzepatide doses.
   - *What to look for:* Head-to-head trials (if available) or high-quality network meta-analyses using standardized AE definitions and severity grades.

6. **Oral semaglutide (vs injectable semaglutide 2.4 mg)**
   - *Gap:* The open question “Can oral semaglutide achieve comparable weight reduction?” remains unclosed in the summarized evidence.
   - *What to look for:* Direct RCT evidence in non-diabetic obesity; subgroup analyses by prior injectable exposure and adherence; meta-analyses that compare formulations with consistent endpoints.

### 2) Contradictions (direct disagreements between sources)
1. **Pancreatitis risk: “not increased” vs “cases reported—monitoring needed”**
   - *Observed conflict in the consolidated findings:*
     - One part of the synthesis suggests pancreatitis risk is not increased (referenced as “no increase based on CVOT data”).
     - Another part (via AWARD-2 referencing dulaglutide) explicitly flags pancreatitis cases requiring close monitoring.
   - *Why this matters:* Clinicians need an *absolute and comparative* risk estimate (and whether risk differs by drug, dose, and indication).
   - *What to resolve:* Compare adjudicated pancreatitis rates across:
     - CVOT populations vs obesity trials,
     - semaglutide vs dulaglutide vs tirzepatide,
     - non-diabetic vs diabetic cohorts.

2. **Magnitude of weight loss across populations (non-diabetic vs type 2 diabetes)**
   - *Not exactly a direct numerical contradiction,* but a tension:
     - Non-diabetic cohorts show ~14.9% (semaglutide 2.4) and ~15–20.9% (tirzepatide doses).
     - Another source asserts much lower weight loss in T2D and correlates it with insulin resistance/hyperglycemia.
   - *Why it’s actionable:* For clinicians treating patients who may have prediabetes or early T2D, the boundary where efficacy changes is not clearly defined.
   - *What to resolve:* Studies stratifying by baseline glycemic status (normoglycemia vs prediabetes vs early T2D) using consistent endpoints.

### 3) Weak evidence areas (thin/low-quality support in the current synthesis)
1. **Cardiometabolic benefits “independent of weight loss”**
   - *Issue:* The claim that improvements in BP/CRP occur independent of weight loss is biologically plausible but can be compromised by:
     - confounding (diet changes, concomitant meds),
     - lack of mediation analyses in many trials,
     - shorter follow-up limiting causal interpretation.
   - *Strength level in current notes:* Marked “high confidence,” but the mechanism/independence is not demonstrated in the consolidated excerpts.

2. **Comparative efficacy between semaglutide and tirzepatide**
   - *Issue:* Reported weight loss magnitudes come from different trials with different designs, titration schemes, baseline BMI distributions, and comparator placebo rates—creating risk of overconfident “ranking.”
   - *Evidence quality caveat already present:* Tirzepatide meta-analyses note substantial heterogeneity (I² > 90%).
   - *Actionable implication:* Any “tirzepatide is superior” statement should be framed as **approximate** unless network meta-analysis or head-to-head evidence uses consistent methods and patient populations.

3. **Safety for rare complications**
   - *Issue:* Claims about pancreatitis/gastroparesis/biliary disease rely on:
     - small numbers of events,
     - signal detection across different drug programs,
     - trials not powered for rare outcomes.
   - *Actionable implication:* Clinicians should treat rare-risk estimates as provisional until backed by large registry data with adjudication.

4. **Weight regain after stopping**
   - *Issue:* “Stopping leads to increased weight” is plausible and supported directionally, but the excerpt provided does not specify:
     - magnitude/time course in non-diabetics,
     - comparison between semaglutide and tirzepatide,
     - role of re-titration or maintenance dosing.
   - *Actionable implication:* The clinical counseling message is correct directionally, but quantitative counseling remains uncertain.

### 4) What would change confidence (specific evidence that would materially improve the synthesis)
1. **Long-term extension and post-marketing outcomes in non-diabetic obesity**
   - Studies that provide:
     - ≥3-year follow-up weight trajectories,
     - adjudicated serious AEs (pancreatitis, biliary disease, severe GI events),
     - discontinuation rates and reasons over time.
   - *High impact sources:* Large registry cohorts, national claims databases with adjudication, and extension RCTs.

2. **Event-driven cardiovascular outcomes stratified by diabetes status**
   - *What would change confidence:* Data demonstrating reduction (or no reduction) in MACE for **non-diabetic** adults with obesity treated with semaglutide or tirzepatide.
   - *Priority:* Trials or analyses that report results separately for normoglycemia/prediabetes vs diabetes.

3. **Mediation analyses or design features enabling “independent of weight loss” claims**
   - *What would change confidence:* Analyses quantifying whether BP/CRP improvements persist after adjusting for weight change (or using structural mediation approaches).
   - *Priority:* Meta-analyses or trials with repeated measures that allow mediation modeling.

4. **Head-to-head or well-matched network meta-analyses (semaglutide 2.4 vs tirzepatide doses)**
   - *What would change confidence:* Comparative efficacy and tolerability using consistent inclusion criteria (non-diabetic adults), standardized AE definitions, and comparable follow-up duration.
   - *Priority:* Network meta-analysis with rigorous assessment of transitivity and sensitivity analyses for baseline BMI/glycemic status.

5. **Body composition studies reporting lean mass changes**
   - *What would change confidence:* Trials using DXA/BIA that report:
     - absolute and percentage lean mass loss,
     - fat mass loss,
     - correlation with protein intake, resistance training, and weight-loss magnitude.
   - *Priority:* Comparative studies between semaglutide and tirzepatide, ideally with dietary counseling control.

6. **Clarification of pancreatitis risk through adjudicated event rates across drugs**
   - *What would change confidence:* Large pooled analyses of adjudicated pancreatitis (and biliary events) with standardized definitions, separated by:
     - drug (semaglutide vs tirzepatide vs other GLP-1 RAs),
     - indication (obesity without diabetes),
     - duration and dose,
     - baseline risk factors (history of pancreatitis, gallstones, alcohol use).
   - *Priority:* Comparative safety studies designed to address the “CVOT vs obesity trials” discrepancy.

--- 

### Researcher “next steps” (actionable checklist)
- **Separate non-diabetic obesity trials from CVOT populations** when making pancreatitis and MACE claims.
- **Seek ≥3-year extension outcomes** for both semaglutide and tirzepatide in non-diabetics.
- **Collect body composition endpoints** (lean vs fat) rather than relying only on weight % change.
- **Extract severity-graded GI AE data** (grade ≥3) and discontinuation rates by drug/dose.
- **Quantify the discontinuation/weight regain curve** and whether it differs by medication.
