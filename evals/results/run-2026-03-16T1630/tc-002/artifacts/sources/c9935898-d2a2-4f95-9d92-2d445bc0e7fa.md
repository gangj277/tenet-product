# Benchmarking Graph Neural Networks in Solving Hard Constraint Satisfaction Problems

**Metadata**
*   **Title:** Benchmarking Graph Neural Networks in Solving Hard Constraint Satisfaction Problems
*   **Authors:** G. Skenderi, L. Buffoni, F. D’Amico, D. Machado, R. Marino, M. Negri, F. Ricci-Tersenghi, C. Lucibello, M. C. Angelini.
*   **Publication Info:** arXiv:2602.18419v2 [cond-mat.dis-nn], March 11, 2026.
*   **Repository:** [RandCSPBench](https://github.com/ArtLabBocconi/RandCSPBench)

**Summary**
This study introduces a rigorous benchmarking framework for Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) applied to Constraint Satisfaction Problems (CSPs), specifically K-SAT and q-coloring. Drawing from statistical physics, the authors argue that existing GNN evaluations often use "easy" instances that do not account for phase transitions (clustering and condensation) which create structural barriers for solvers. Through a fair comparison using scaled inference times, the research finds that while GNNs perform respectably on simpler 3-SAT and 3-coloring tasks, they are significantly outperformed by classical heuristics (like Focused Metropolis Search) on harder 4-SAT and 5-coloring instances, particularly when tested on out-of-distribution (OOD) larger problem sizes.

**Key Claims**
*   **Lack of Standardization:** Current GNN research in combinatorial optimization lacks unified benchmarks, leading to inflated or incomparable performance claims.
*   **Structural Barriers:** Hardness in CSPs is dictated by phase transitions (e.g., 1-step replica symmetry breaking) that create "glassy" landscapes, which affect GNNs similarly to classical algorithms.
*   **Inference Scaling Necessity:** To maintain performance as problem size ($N$) increases, GNNs must scale their message-passing iterations (e.g., $t = 2N$); fixed-iteration models fail on larger instances.
*   **Superiority of Classical Heuristics:** Classical algorithms, specifically Focused Metropolis Search (FMS), consistently achieve higher "algorithmic thresholds" and better success rates than GNNs on hard instances.
*   **Generalization Gap:** GNN performance degrades sharply when moving from training sizes (up to $N=256$) to OOD sizes ($N=1024$), whereas classical heuristics maintain more robust scaling.

**Relevant Evidence and Quotes**
*   **Performance on Hard Problems:** "GNNs... rapidly deteriorate when applied to hard problems such as the 4-SAT and the 5-col for which classic algorithms are the most competitive ones."
*   **Algorithmic Thresholds:** In 4-SAT, the estimated algorithmic threshold for QuerySAT is $\alpha \approx 9.1$, whereas FMS reaches $\alpha \approx 9.8$ (closer to the theoretical satisfiability threshold of 9.931).
*   **Unsupervised vs. Supervised:** "The supervised NeuroSAT solver has very poor performance with respect to the unsupervised one."
*   **Time Efficiency:** While GNNs have fast inference, the authors note: "neural solvers like NeuroSAT and QuerySAT require a time for training which is much larger than the time to solve an instance once trained... Classical heuristics algorithms do not need any training."
*   **Residual Energy:** For instances where a solution isn't found, GNNs (like NeuroSAT) often achieve lower residual energy (fewer unsatisfied constraints) than message-passing algorithms, which may return random configurations upon failure.

**Contribution to Research Frame**
*   **Evidence Against Neural Superiority:** This source directly challenges the hypothesis that GNNs are general-purpose replacements for OR heuristics. It demonstrates a clear "generalization gap" where GNNs fail as $N$ increases beyond training distributions.
*   **Amortized Efficiency Critique:** The study highlights that the massive training overhead of GNNs is not justified for these hard CSPs, as classical solvers like FMS are both faster (in wall-clock time for hard instances) and more effective without any training.
*   **Refining the "Niche":** The findings suggest that GNN utility may be limited to "easy" phase regimes (3-SAT/3-col) or very small-scale problems, failing to navigate the complex solution space geometries of truly NP-hard instances.
*   **Methodological Rigor:** It provides a template for "fair" comparison by scaling test-time compute (iterations) for GNNs to match the increased computational budget given to classical iterative solvers.
