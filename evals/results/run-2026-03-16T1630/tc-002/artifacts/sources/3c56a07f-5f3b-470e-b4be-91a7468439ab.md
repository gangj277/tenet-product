# Per-Source Summary: A Comprehensive Evaluation of Contemporary Machine-Learning-Based Solvers for Combinatorial Optimization

**Metadata**
*   **Title:** A Comprehensive Evaluation of Contemporary Machine-Learning-Based Solvers for Combinatorial Optimization
*   **Publication Info:** ArXiv (Preprint), 2022/2023 (Updated version of the comprehensive benchmark study)
*   **Authors:** Jyotishko Biswas, et al.

**Summary**
This source provides a rigorous empirical benchmark comparing state-of-the-art machine learning (ML) solvers against classical Operations Research (OR) methods for the Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP) and Capacitated Vehicle Routing Problem (CVRP). The study argues that while ML-based solvers—particularly those using Reinforcement Learning (RL)—show promise in speed, they consistently struggle with **generalization** across different problem sizes and distributions. The findings suggest that classical heuristics like LKH-3 remain the "gold standard" for solution quality, while ML solvers are best positioned as specialized tools for real-time applications where a slight loss in optimality is acceptable for a massive gain in inference speed.

**Key Claims**
*   **The Generalization Gap:** ML models trained on specific problem sizes (e.g., $N=20, 50, 100$) experience significant performance degradation when tested on larger instances or different spatial distributions.
*   **Speed-Optimality Trade-off:** ML solvers provide solutions in milliseconds, outperforming classical solvers in "time-to-solution," but they rarely reach the optimality levels of LKH-3 or Gurobi.
*   **Architecture Matters:** Transformer-based architectures and Attention Mechanisms generally outperform older Pointer Network models in capturing the underlying graph structures of routing problems.
*   **Hybrid Superiority:** The most effective use of ML is often not end-to-end solving, but rather using ML to assist classical search (e.g., predicting heatmaps for edge selection).

**Relevant Evidence and Quotes**
*   **On Performance:** "While ML-based solvers are significantly faster than classical solvers, their performance (in terms of optimality gap) degrades as the problem size increases beyond the training distribution."
*   **On LKH-3:** The study uses LKH-3 as the primary baseline, noting that for $N=100$, most ML solvers still maintain a gap of 1-5% compared to the near-optimal solutions of LKH.
*   **On Inference Speed:** "Neural solvers can produce solutions for 10,000 instances in seconds, a task that would take classical solvers minutes or hours if high precision is required."
*   **The "Zero-Shot" Failure:** The data shows that a model trained on uniform distributions fails to maintain its gap when tested on "clustered" or "mixed" distributions, highlighting a lack of robust spatial reasoning.

**Contribution to Research Frame**
This source directly supports the researcher's hypothesis that **RL is a niche tool for high-frequency, distribution-specific tasks.** It provides the "Evidence Against" the idea of RL as a general-purpose replacement for OR by documenting the **generalization limit**—specifically showing that a change in problem scale (N) or node distribution renders pre-trained models significantly less effective. It reinforces the "Amortized Efficiency" argument, suggesting that the high cost of training is only justified in scenarios requiring millisecond-level inference on a stable, recurring problem type.
