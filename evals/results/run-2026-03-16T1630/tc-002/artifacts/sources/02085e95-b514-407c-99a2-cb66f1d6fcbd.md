# FrontierCO: Real-World and Large-Scale Evaluation of Machine Learning Solvers for Combinatorial Optimization

**Metadata**
*   **Authors:** Shengyu Feng, Weiwei Sun, Shanda Li, Ameet Talwalker, Yiming Yang (Carnegie Mellon University)
*   **Date:** March 9, 2026 (arXiv:2505.16952v3)
*   **Source Type:** Research Paper / Benchmark Dataset Release
*   **Key Problems:** MIS, MDS, TSP, CVRP, CFLP, CPMP, FJSP, STP.

### Summary
FrontierCO introduces a rigorous benchmark designed to evaluate Machine Learning (ML) solvers—including neural, hybrid, and LLM-based agents—against state-of-the-art (SOTA) classical solvers on real-world, large-scale combinatorial optimization (CO) problems. The authors argue that existing ML evaluations rely on small, synthetic datasets that fail to capture the structural complexity and extreme scale (e.g., TSP with 10M nodes) of industrial applications. Their evaluation of 16 representative ML solvers reveals a persistent performance gap that widens as instances become larger and structurally more irregular. While ML methods show potential in enhancing weak heuristics or synthesizing algorithms via LLMs, they generally struggle with scalability, global reasoning, and distribution shifts compared to established human-designed solvers.

### Key Claims
*   **Scale and Realism Gap:** Most ML solvers are trained on toy-scale synthetic data (e.g., $N \leq 100$), leading to severe performance degradation when faced with real-world competition-grade instances (e.g., DIMACS, TSPLib).
*   **Neural Scalability Issues:** Neural solvers face significant "out-of-memory" failures and inference bottlenecks at extreme scales (e.g., 10M nodes) due to over-parameterization and auto-regressive decoding.
*   **LLM Potential and Variance:** LLM-based agents can occasionally outperform SOTA classical solvers by synthesizing complex meta-heuristics, but they exhibit high performance variance and lack internal reasoning to verify the effectiveness of their generated algorithms.
*   **Local vs. Global Structure:** Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) effectively exploit local spatial regularities (like the triangle inequality in Euclidean TSP) but fail to capture global structures in non-Euclidean or general graph problems like Steiner Tree Problems (STP).
*   **Hybrid Utility:** Neural modules provide the most value when used to enhance relatively "weak" base heuristics (e.g., improving 2-OPT), though they still fall short of the best human-designed exact or meta-heuristic solvers.

### Relevant Evidence and Quotes
*   **The Generalization Gap:** "LEHD reports only a 0.72% gap on a standard TSP benchmark... whereas on our new benchmark the gap widens to 10% on easy TSP instances and an alarming 77% on hard instances."
*   **Inference Bottlenecks:** "The auto-regressive solver LEHD requires running a transformer model for 10M steps to produce a single solution on our largest TSP instance, failing to return any solution within the 1-hour time limit."
*   **LLM Success Example:** "Self-Refine surpasses KaMIS on the easy MIS set... [it] applies kernelization to simplify MIS instances, solves small kernels exactly... and employs ARW-style heuristics."
*   **Structural Failure:** "While the GNN quickly achieves a high F1 score in predicting Steiner points on Euclidean graphs, it fails to make any meaningful progress on non-Euclidean graphs."

### Contribution to Research Frame
This source provides strong **evidence against** the current robustness of pure RL and neural solvers for general-purpose routing and CO. It directly addresses the "generalization limit" by demonstrating that performance collapses when moving from synthetic training distributions to irregular, large-scale real-world data.

*   **Amortized Efficiency vs. Robust Optimality:** The paper challenges the "amortized efficiency" argument by showing that for very large instances, the inference time of neural models (like LEHD) can actually exceed the time limits of classical solvers.
*   **Hybridization Support:** It reinforces the "hybridization" subquestion, suggesting that ML's current best use case is guiding or enhancing existing search frameworks rather than replacing them end-to-end.
*   **Evaluation Bias:** The authors explicitly critique the "methodological flaws" of prior research that used fixed-size synthetic test sets, providing a more rigorous "Frontier" for future comparative utility studies.
