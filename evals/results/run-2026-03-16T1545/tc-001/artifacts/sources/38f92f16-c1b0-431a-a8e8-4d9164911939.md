# Research Note: Self-Consuming Generative Models with Adversarially Curated Data

**Title:** Self-Consuming Generative Models with Adversarially Curated Data  
**Authors:** Xiukun Wei, Xueru Zhang (Department of Computer Science and Engineering, The Ohio State University)  
**Publication:** Proceedings of the 42nd International Conference on Machine Learning (ICML), 2025.

---

### Summary
This paper investigates the stability of "self-consuming loops"—where generative models are trained on their own synthetic output—specifically when that data is curated based on human preferences. While previous research suggests that user-curated synthetic data can help a model converge toward human preferences, this study introduces the threat of **adversarial curation**, where malicious actors (e.g., competing platforms) manipulate the selection process to misalign the model. The authors provide a theoretical framework to identify when these loops remain robust and when they collapse, ultimately developing gradient-based and heuristic attack algorithms that demonstrate how easily a rival model’s alignment can be disrupted.

### Key Claims
*   **Conditional Robustness of Self-Consuming Loops:** Generative models can still align with true user preferences despite noisy curation, provided the adversarial reward functions remain positively correlated with genuine user rewards.
*   **Vulnerability to Negative Correlation:** If adversarial curation introduces a negative correlation with true user preferences (specifically through the covariance of the reward functions), the model's expected reward will oscillate and fail to converge, leading to misalignment.
*   **Inadequacy of Real Data Grounding:** Simply mixing real data into the training set (a common defense against model collapse) is insufficient to protect against strategic adversarial curation if the "poisoned" synthetic data is sufficiently misaligned.
*   **Feasibility of "Market Share" Attacks:** An adversarial platform can effectively disrupt a competitor by learning the competitor's reward model from public data and strategically "flipping" a limited budget of preference labels to guide malicious users.

### Relevant Evidence & Quotes
*   **The Covariance Rule:** "The convergence of the generative model toward maximizing user-expected rewards is governed by the covariance of adversarially curated synthetic data... when $e^{r(x)}$ and $e^{re_t(x)}$ are negatively correlated... the expected reward may oscillate and deviate from the maximum value."
*   **Mathematical Limit of Real Data Defense:** Lemma 3.4 proves that even with real data ($p_{data}$), if the minimum covariance ($Cov_{min}$) is negative, the model's performance can drop below the baseline of the original real data distribution: $E_{p_{t+1}}[e^{r(x)}] \geq E_{p_{data}}[e^{r(x)}] + \phi^*( \lambda + 1) Cov_{min}$.
*   **Attack Mechanism:** The authors propose a **Gradient-based attack** (Algorithm 1) that uses the implicit function theorem to compute how changing a preference label will eventually shift the model's learned parameters, allowing for highly efficient "poisoning" with a limited budget ($\kappa$).

### Contribution to Research Frame
This source significantly refines the research project's understanding of **model collapse** by shifting the focus from *passive* statistical decay to *active* manipulation.

*   **Challenges the "Data Hygiene" Solution:** It suggests that even if a researcher maintains a "specific ratio of real-world grounding data," the model can still be derailed if the synthetic portion is curated by biased or adversarial agents.
*   **Identifies a New Driver of Collapse:** It introduces "adversarial misalignment" as a technical boundary condition. It proves that model collapse is not just a result of losing the "tails" of a distribution, but can be a directed process where the model is forced to converge toward a "malicious" distribution.
*   **Refines Mitigation Strategies:** The findings imply that defending against collapse requires more than just adding real data; it requires **robust reward modeling** and mechanisms to detect negative correlations in user feedback loops.
