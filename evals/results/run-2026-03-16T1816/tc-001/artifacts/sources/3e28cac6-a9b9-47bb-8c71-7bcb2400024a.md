# Entropy Collapse: Empirical Detection and Recovery Limits in AI Systems

**Publication Info:** Preprint, Version 1, Submitted: 11 January 2026, Posted: 12 January 2026

## Summary

This paper introduces a novel, fully empirical framework for detecting "entropy collapse" in feedback-driven AI systems. Entropy collapse is defined not merely by low entropy, but by the failure of admissible interventions to restore behavioral diversity within a finite observation window. The framework relies exclusively on observable quantities such as empirical state distributions, Shannon entropy, dominance concentration, and distributional displacement. By conducting repeated intervention experiments, the system establishes a recovery-based threshold, indicating a point beyond which collapse becomes statistically irreversible. This approach offers a practical tool for early warning, evaluation, and intervention in AI systems and their societal deployments, grounding collapse in measurable irrecoverability rather than descriptive convergence.

## Key Claims

*   Contemporary AI systems increasingly utilize recursive feedback processes (self-training, preference optimization, algorithmic governance).
*   A recurring failure mode in these systems is behavioral contraction, dominance emergence, and loss of intervention effectiveness, often termed "entropy collapse."
*   Existing work lacks a general empirical criterion for declaring when a system has entered an irrecoverable state.
*   This paper defines entropy collapse operationally as the failure of admissible interventions to restore diversity within a finite observation window.
*   The proposed framework uses observable quantities: empirical state distributions, Shannon entropy, dominance concentration, distributional displacement, and second-order change in displacement.
*   A recovery-based threshold is constructed from repeated intervention experiments to identify a system-specific limit beyond which collapse is statistically irreversible.
*   The empirical indicators generalize to socio-technical systems, providing a common language for diagnosing rigidity, adaptability loss, and governance failure.

## Contribution to/Challenge of Research Frame

This source directly addresses the core of the research frame by providing a *measurable and operational definition* of "model collapse" (termed "entropy collapse" here) that moves beyond descriptive observations.

*   **Contribution:** It offers a concrete, empirically grounded methodology for detecting collapse based on *intervention failure*, which aligns with the frame's goal of identifying technical thresholds. The emphasis on observable quantities and recovery experiments provides a path to quantify the "inevitability" or "manageability" of collapse. The framework's ability to generalize to socio-technical systems also supports the broader implications of the research.
*   **Challenge:** The source's definition of collapse is *recovery-based*, meaning it's not solely about the inherent mathematical properties of the data or model but about the *efficacy of external interventions*. This might challenge a purely mathematical inevitability argument if effective interventions can consistently prevent collapse, even if mathematical indicators suggest otherwise. The research frame's focus on "mathematical certainty" might need to be nuanced to include the operational reality of intervention effectiveness as defined in this paper. The paper's focus is on *detecting* and *quantifying* the limits of recovery, rather than solely on the mathematical proofs of collapse itself.
