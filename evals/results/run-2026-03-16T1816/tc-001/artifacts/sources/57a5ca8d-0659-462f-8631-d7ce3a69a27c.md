# KL Divergence for Machine Learning

**Author:** Dibya Ghosh
**Publication:** Dibya's Blog

## Summary

This blog post provides a comprehensive introduction to the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence, explaining its definition, properties, and interpretations within the context of machine learning. The author argues that both supervised learning and reinforcement learning can be framed as minimizing KL divergence objectives. The post details the differences between minimizing forward KL (which exhibits mean-seeking behavior and is akin to Maximum Likelihood Estimation) and reverse KL (which exhibits mode-seeking behavior and is related to Maximum Entropy RL), highlighting their distinct behaviors and practical implications for model approximation.

## Key Claims

*   KL divergence measures the difference between two probability distributions.
*   KL divergence is not symmetric ($D_{KL}(P \| Q) \neq D_{KL}(Q \| P)$) and is therefore not a true distance metric.
*   Minimizing forward KL divergence ($D_{KL}(P \| Q_\theta)$) leads to "mean-seeking" behavior where the approximate distribution $Q_\theta$ tries to cover all high-probability regions of the true distribution $P$. This is equivalent to Maximum Likelihood Estimation.
*   Minimizing reverse KL divergence ($D_{KL}(Q_\theta \| P)$) leads to "mode-seeking" behavior where $Q_\theta$ tries to place probability mass only where $P$ has high probability. This is related to Maximum Entropy Reinforcement Learning.
*   Supervised learning, particularly classification with cross-entropy loss and regression with mean-squared error loss, can be viewed as minimizing the forward KL divergence.
*   Reinforcement learning can be framed as minimizing the reverse KL divergence, aligning with maximum-entropy RL objectives.

## Relevant Evidence/Quotes

*   "Put simply, the KL divergence between two probability distributions measures how different the two distributions are."
*   "Mathematically, consider two probability distributions $P,Q$ on some space $\\mathcal{X}$. The Kullback-Leibler divergence from $Q$ to $P$ (written as $D_{KL}(P | Q)$) $$D_{KL}(P \\| Q) = \\mathbb{E}_{x \\sim P}\\left[\\log \\frac{P(X)}{Q(X)}\\right]$$"
*   "The KL Divergence is not symmetric : that is $D_{KL}(P | Q) \\neq D_{KL}(Q | P)$. As a result, it is also not a distance metric."
*   "Wherever $P(\\cdot)$ has high probability, $Q(\\cdot)$ must also have high probability. We consider this mean-seeking behaviour, because the approximate distribution $Q$ must cover all the modes and regions of high probability in $P$." (Describing Forward KL)
*   "Wherever $Q(\\cdot)$ has high probability, $P(\\cdot)$ must also have high probability. We consider this mode-seeking behaviour, because any sample from the approximate distribution $Q$ must lie within a mode of $P$ (since it’s required that samples from $Q$ have high probability under $P$)." (Describing Reverse KL)
*   "Supervised Learning = Forward KL"
*   "Reinforcement Learning = Reverse KL"

## Contribution to Research Frame

This source directly supports the research frame by providing a foundational understanding of KL divergence as a measure of distribution difference, which is central to understanding distribution shift. Specifically, the distinction between forward and reverse KL divergence offers a theoretical lens to analyze how different optimization objectives (like those in supervised vs. reinforcement learning) might lead to different types of distribution degradation or approximation. The framing of supervised learning as forward KL (MLE) and RL as reverse KL (Max Entropy) helps to categorize the potential behaviors of models trained on synthetic data, depending on whether they are trying to match the overall distribution (forward) or specific modes (reverse). This aligns with the research's goal of understanding the technical mechanisms of model collapse and its mitigation.
