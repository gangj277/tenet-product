# A Tale of Tails: Model Collapse as a Change of Scaling Laws

**Publication Info:** Proceedings of the 41st International Conference on Machine Learning, Vienna, Austria. PMLR 235, 2024.

## Summary

This paper investigates the phenomenon of "model collapse," where generative models trained on synthetic data degrade in performance over successive generations. The authors propose a theoretical framework analyzing how the distribution of AI-generated data, often characterized by "tail cutting" and "tail narrowing," deviates from the heavy-tailed distributions typically assumed by scaling laws. They demonstrate that this deviation leads to a loss of scaling, where performance plateaus instead of improving with more data. The research also explores mitigation strategies, such as mixing real data with synthetic data, which can lead to a "grokking" phenomenon where performance eventually recovers.

## Key Claims

*   AI-generated data tends to exhibit "tail cutting" and "tail narrowing" compared to natural, heavy-tailed data distributions.
*   This deviation from heavy-tailed distributions causes a loss of traditional scaling laws, leading to performance plateaus.
*   Recursive generation of AI data across multiple generations exacerbates this degradation multiplicatively.
*   Mixing a small fraction of real data with synthetic data can mitigate model collapse and even lead to a "grokking" phenomenon where performance recovers.
*   The effectiveness of adding curated real data to compensate for missing tails depends critically on selecting data from the correct range of the original distribution.

## Relevant Evidence/Quotes

*   "We discover a wide range of decay phenomena, analyzing loss of scaling, shifted scaling with number of generations, the 'un-learning' of skills, and grokking when mixing human and synthesized data."
*   "Theoretical derivations of scaling laws always assume a heavy-tailed distribution (power-law, aka Zipf) on the input features ('heavy tail in, power scaling law out')."
*   "Regenerating heavy-tailed data affects the distribution in two possible ways: (1) 'Cutting off' the tail of the distribution and/or (2) 'Narrowing' the tail."
*   **Theorem 2.1:** "Consider long-tail real-world data with exponent $\\beta > 1$, and let the cutoff for AI-generated data be $k$. Then, for large $k$ and $T$ samples from the AI, the test error of the downstream 'LLM' scales like $E_{\\text{test}} \\propto T^{-(\\beta-1)/\\beta} + k^{-(\\beta-1)} = \\min(T^{1/\\beta}, k)^{-(\\beta-1)}$."
*   **Theorem 2.4 (Informal):** "Model collapse (as spoken of in the literature) occurs iff $n \\gg (T_0/T)^c$."
*   **Theorem 3.2 (Grokking with Tail Cutting):** "For $T \\ll k^\\beta/\\pi$, it holds that $E_{\\text{test}} \\propto T^{-(1-1/\\beta)} + k^{-(\\beta-1)}$. As soon as $T > Ck^\\beta/\\pi$ (where $C$ is an absolute constant), it holds that $E_{\\text{test}} \\propto (\\pi T)^{-(1-1/\\beta)}$."
*   **Theorem 5.1 (Triplet Scaling Law):** "For all the algorithms $q$ considered in (Cabannes et al., 2023), one has the following triplet scaling law w.r.t sample size $T$, embedding dimension $d$, and frequency cutoff $k$, $E_{\\text{test}} \\propto T^{-(1-1/\\beta)} + d^{-c_q} + k^{-(\\beta-1)}$."

## Contribution to Research Frame

This source directly addresses the core of the research frame by providing a mathematical and empirical explanation for *why* and *how* model collapse occurs. It moves beyond a qualitative description of degradation to a quantitative analysis of scaling law changes due to synthetic data characteristics (tail cutting/narrowing). The paper's theoretical framework and experimental validation offer concrete mechanisms for distribution shift and variance loss in recursive training. Crucially, it proposes and validates mitigation strategies like data mixing, directly contributing to the goal of identifying actionable safeguards and moving away from a deterministic "doom" narrative. The findings suggest that collapse is indeed a function of data-to-noise ratios and distribution drift, which can be modeled and mitigated.
