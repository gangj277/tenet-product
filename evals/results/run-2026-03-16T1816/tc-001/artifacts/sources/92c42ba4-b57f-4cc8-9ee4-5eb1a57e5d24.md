# Al models collapse when trained on recursively generated data

**Publication:** Nature (2024)
**Authors:** Ilia Shumailov, Zakhar Shumaylov, Yiren Zhao, Nicolas Papernot, Ross Anderson, Yarin Gal

## Summary

This paper introduces and defines the phenomenon of "model collapse," a degenerative process where generative AI models, when trained on data produced by their predecessors, irreversibly lose information about the original data distribution. This occurs because the synthetic data generated by earlier models becomes part of the training set for subsequent models, leading to a gradual "forgetting" of rare or tail-end events. The authors demonstrate this collapse across various model types, including Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs), Variational Autoencoders (VAEs), and Large Language Models (LLMs), showing that it is a universal issue for recursively trained generative models. They argue that preserving access to original, human-generated data is crucial to sustain the benefits of large-scale data training and that the value of such data will increase as LLM-generated content proliferates online.

## Key Claims

*   **Model Collapse Defined:** A degenerative process where generative models trained on recursively generated data lose information about the true underlying data distribution, particularly affecting the tails (low-probability events).
*   **Ubiquity Across Models:** Model collapse is not limited to LLMs but affects GMMs, VAEs, and other learned generative models.
*   **Causes of Collapse:** The phenomenon is driven by a compounding of statistical approximation error, functional expressivity error, and functional approximation error.
*   **Mathematical Intuition:** Theoretical analysis using discrete distributions and multidimensional Gaussians demonstrates that recursive training inevitably leads to a loss of variance and divergence from the original distribution.
*   **LLM Vulnerability:** LLMs, especially when fine-tuned on generated data, are susceptible to model collapse, leading to degraded performance and the generation of erroneous or repetitive content.
*   **Mitigation Strategy:** Preserving access to original, human-generated data is essential to counteract model collapse and maintain model quality over time.
*   **Value of Real Data:** Data about genuine human interactions will become increasingly valuable as LLM-generated content pollutes online datasets.

## Relevant Evidence/Quotes

*   "We find that indiscriminate use of model-generated content in training causes irreversible defects in the resulting models, in which tails of the original content distribution disappear. We refer to this effect as 'model collapse'..."
*   "We discover that indiscriminately learning from data produced by other models causes ‘model collapse’—a degenerative process whereby, over time, models forget the true underlying data distribution, even in the absence of a shift in the distribution over time."
*   "Model collapse is a degenerative process affecting generations of learned generative models, in which the data they generate end up polluting the training set of the next generation. Being trained on polluted data, they then mis-perceive reality."
*   "The value of data collected about genuine human interactions with systems will be increasingly valuable in the presence of LLM-generated content in data crawled from the Internet."
*   In the context of LLMs: "Both training regimes lead to degraded performance in our models, yet we do find that learning with generated data is possible and models can successfully learn (some of) the underlying task. In particular, from Fig. 1 and their 3D versions in the Supplementary Materials, we see that model collapse occurs, as the density of samples with low perplexity begins to accumulate over the generations."
*   Example 1 shows text outputs degrading over generations, with Gen 5 producing a list of languages and Gen 9 producing a list of jackrabbit types, deviating significantly from the original input about architecture.

## Contribution to/Challenge of Research Frame

This source directly supports the inferred research frame by providing a foundational understanding of model collapse as a mathematically demonstrable phenomenon, not merely an anecdotal issue. It offers empirical evidence and theoretical underpinnings for the "distribution shift and variance loss" mechanisms. The paper's emphasis on the "inevitability" of collapse under recursive training challenges the idea that it's solely an "engineering challenge" and leans towards a fundamental mathematical certainty, at least under certain conditions.

However, it also provides crucial insights for identifying "actionable architectural and data-curation safeguards." The explicit recommendation to preserve "fresh' real-world data" and the discussion on the increasing value of "data provenance" directly align with the research's goal of finding mitigation strategies. The paper's findings suggest that the "technical thresholds and data-mixing ratios" are critical, implying that while collapse is a risk, its severity and timeline can be influenced by how much real data is retained. The paper does not present evidence *against* model collapse, but rather elaborates on its causes and consequences, thereby framing the problem that the research aims to solve.
