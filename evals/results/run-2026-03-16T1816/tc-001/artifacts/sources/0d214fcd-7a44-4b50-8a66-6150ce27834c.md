# AI Models Collapse When Trained on Recursively Generated Data

**Publication:** Nature

**Summary:**
This paper introduces and defines the phenomenon of "model collapse," where generative AI models, including Large Language Models (LLMs), degrade in performance over successive generations when trained on data produced by their predecessors. This collapse is characterized by the loss of information about the original data distribution, particularly in the "tails" of the distribution, and can lead to models converging to a point estimate with very small variance. The authors demonstrate this phenomenon theoretically and empirically across various model types (GMMs, VAEs, LLMs), highlighting that indiscriminate use of model-generated content in training datasets is unsustainable for maintaining model quality and the benefits of large-scale data. They emphasize the increasing value of genuine human-generated data to counteract this degradation.

**Key Claims:**

*   Indiscriminate use of model-generated content in training datasets causes irreversible defects in AI models, a phenomenon termed "model collapse."
*   Model collapse leads to the disappearance of the "tails" of the original data distribution, causing models to forget the true underlying data distribution.
*   This collapse is a universal phenomenon affecting various learned generative models, including LLMs, VAEs, and GMMs.
*   Model collapse is driven by compounding errors: statistical approximation error, functional expressivity error, and functional approximation error.
*   In the limit, recursive training on generated data can cause models to diverge from the original distribution and collapse to a zero-variance state.
*   Fine-tuning language models on generated data does not prevent model collapse; in fact, it can exacerbate the issue.
*   Access to the original data distribution and the preservation of real human-produced data are crucial for sustaining the benefits of training from large-scale web data.

**Relevant Evidence/Quotes:**

*   "We find that indiscriminate use of model-generated content in training causes irreversible defects in the resulting models, in which tails of the original content distribution disappear."
*   "We refer to this effect as ‘model collapse’ and show that it can occur in LLMs as well as in variational autoencoders (VAEs) and Gaussian mixture models (GMMs)."
*   "We give examples of model collapse for GMMs, VAEs and LLMs. We show that, over time, models start losing information about the true distribution, which first starts with tails disappearing, and learned behaviours converge over the generations to a point estimate with very small variance."
*   "Indeed, the value of data collected about genuine human interactions with systems will be increasingly valuable in the presence of LLM-generated content in data crawled from the Internet."
*   Theorem 3.1 (Gaussian model collapse): "In words, this implies that not only does the n th generation approximation diverge arbitrarily far from the original one but it also collapses to be zero variance as the number of generations increases, with probability 1."
*   "We find that models trained on generated data are able to learn some of the original task, but with errors, as seen from the increase in perplexity."
*   "The repeating problem has been observed in nearly all text-generation models... We find that this causes the models to produce lower score continuations to avoid using repeats, which—as a result—causes the consequent models to perform even worse."

**Contribution to Research Frame:**

This source directly supports the research frame by providing a foundational definition and empirical/theoretical evidence for "model collapse." It establishes that recursive training on synthetic data *does* lead to degradation, particularly through the loss of distribution tails and variance reduction. The paper's findings suggest that model collapse is a fundamental mathematical certainty under certain conditions, rather than merely an engineering challenge, and that mitigation requires preserving access to original, real-world data. This aligns with the frame's focus on the technical validity and inevitability of collapse and the need for strategies like integrating "fresh" real-world data. The paper's emphasis on the compounding nature of errors and the specific mechanisms of distribution shift and variance loss directly addresses the research's goal of understanding these processes.
