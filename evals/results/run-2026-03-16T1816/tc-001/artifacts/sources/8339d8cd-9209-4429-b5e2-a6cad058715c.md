# AI Models Collapse When Trained on Recursively Generated Data

**Publication:** Nature
**Date:** (Article published online: 06 May 2024; Author Correction: 21 March 2025)

## Summary

This paper introduces and defines the phenomenon of "model collapse," where generative AI models, particularly Large Language Models (LLMs), degrade in performance when trained on data generated by previous versions of themselves. This recursive training process leads to a loss of information about the original data distribution, characterized by the disappearance of "tails" (low-probability events) and a convergence towards a narrow, high-variance output. The authors demonstrate this collapse across various model types, including Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs), Variational Autoencoders (VAEs), and LLMs, providing both theoretical intuition and empirical evidence. They argue that this collapse is an inevitable consequence of training on synthetic data and highlights the increasing value of genuinely human-generated data to sustain the benefits of large-scale AI.

## Key Claims

*   Indiscriminate use of model-generated content in training causes irreversible defects in generative AI models, leading to "model collapse."
*   Model collapse is a degenerative process where models forget the true underlying data distribution, with tails of the distribution disappearing first.
*   This phenomenon is universal among learned generative models that recursively train on data generated by previous generations.
*   Model collapse can occur in LLMs, VAEs, and GMMs, and is driven by compounding errors from statistical approximation, functional expressivity, and functional approximation.
*   The process is inevitable, even under ideal learning conditions, and can lead to models converging to a point estimate with very small variance.
*   Access to the original data distribution is crucial, and data about genuine human interactions will become increasingly valuable as LLM-generated content proliferates online.

## Relevant Evidence/Quotes

*   "We find that indiscriminate use of model-generated content in training causes irreversible defects in the resulting models, in which tails of the original content distribution disappear. We refer to this effect as ‘model collapse’..."
*   "Model collapse is a degenerative process affecting generations of learned generative models, in which the data they generate end up polluting the training set of the next generation. Being trained on polluted data, they then mis-perceive reality."
*   "We show that, over time, models start losing information about the true distribution, which first starts with tails disappearing, and learned behaviours converge over the generations to a point estimate with very small variance."
*   "Indeed, the value of data collected about genuine human interactions with systems will be increasingly valuable in the presence of LLM-generated content in data crawled from the Internet."
*   In the context of LLMs: "We fine-tune the OPT-125m causal language model... We find that training with generated data allows us to adapt to the underlying task, losing some performance, from 20 to 28 perplexity points." and "Both training regimes lead to degraded performance in our models, yet we do find that learning with generated data is possible and models can successfully learn (some of) the underlying task."
*   Example 1 showing text outputs degrading over generations, becoming nonsensical or repetitive.

## Contribution to Research Frame

This source directly supports the research frame by providing a foundational definition and empirical evidence for the phenomenon of "model collapse." It establishes that recursive training on synthetic data leads to a quantifiable degradation of model performance, specifically the loss of distributional diversity and the convergence to a narrow output. The paper's theoretical underpinnings and mathematical models offer a technical explanation for *why* this collapse occurs, framing it as a fundamental challenge arising from compounding errors in generative processes.

The findings align with the idea that model collapse is a "fundamental mathematical certainty" under certain conditions, challenging the notion that it's merely a manageable engineering problem without addressing the core issue of data provenance. The paper's emphasis on the loss of "tails" and the increasing value of "genuine human-generated data" directly speaks to the need for strategies that preserve distributional diversity and integrate fresh, real-world data, which are central to the research project's goal of identifying technical thresholds and mitigation strategies. It provides strong evidence for the "doom narrative" that the project aims to explore and potentially refine by identifying specific triggers and thresholds.
