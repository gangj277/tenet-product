# How Bad is Training on Synthetic Data? A Statistical Analysis of Language Model Collapse

**Authors:** Mohamed El Amine Seddik, Pierre Youssef, Suei-Wen Chen, Merouane Debbah, Soufiane Hayou

**Publication Info:** Technology Innovation Institute, NYU Abu Dhabi, Khalifa University of Science and Technology, Simons Institute

## Summary

This paper investigates the phenomenon of "model collapse," where training generative models on synthetic data produced by previous model generations leads to performance degradation and a loss of diversity. The authors develop a statistical framework to analyze this issue in language models, differentiating between "fully synthetic" training (using only generated data) and "partially synthetic" training (mixing real and generated data). They theoretically demonstrate that model collapse is unavoidable in the fully synthetic scenario, characterizing its rate. Crucially, they show that in the partially synthetic setting, there exists a maximal amount of synthetic data below which model collapse can be avoided, providing theoretical bounds and empirical validation.

## Key Claims

*   Model collapse is the deterioration of model performance when trained recursively on synthetic data, leading to the disappearance of the original data distribution's tails.
*   In a "fully synthetic" training scenario (using only data generated by the previous model), model collapse is unavoidable and can be characterized by specific rates of convergence to a Dirac mass.
*   In a "partially synthetic" training scenario (mixing real and synthetic data), model collapse can be avoided if the amount of synthetic data remains below a certain threshold.
*   The paper provides theoretical bounds and empirical evidence to estimate this threshold, suggesting that the amount of synthetic data should be significantly smaller than the real data to prevent collapse.
*   The rate of collapse is influenced by factors such as the number of generations, the size of the synthetic dataset used per generation, and the initial distribution's diversity.

## Relevant Evidence/Quotes

*   "The phenomenon of model collapse, introduced in (Shumailov et al., 2023), refers to the deterioration in performance that occurs when new models are trained on synthetic data generated from previously trained models. This recursive training loop makes the tails of the original distribution disappear, thereby making future-generation models forget about the initial (real) distribution."
*   "Specifically, we demonstrate that model collapse cannot be avoided when training solely on synthetic data. However, when mixing both real and synthetic data, we provide an estimate of a maximal amount of synthetic data below which model collapse can eventually be avoided."
*   **Theorem 1 (Control on Total Collapse)**: This theorem quantifies the rate of collapse in the fully synthetic setting, showing how expected sum of squared probabilities ($S_m$) and the probability of collapse ($P_m$) increase with generations ($m$) and decrease with synthetic sample size ($n$).
*   **Theorem 3 (Model Deviation)**: This theorem provides an upper bound on the deviation of the model's distribution from the first generation's distribution in the partially synthetic setting, allowing for the estimation of the maximum synthetic samples ($n$) to keep the distribution close to the initial one. The bound suggests that $n$ should be exponentially smaller than the real data to ensure small $L^1$ deviation.
*   Figure 1 illustrates "total collapse" in the fully synthetic setting, showing the trained conditional distributions converging towards Dirac measures over generations.
*   Figure 3 shows that in the partially synthetic setting, increasing synthetic data ($n$) leads to increased distribution drift ($||p^{(m)} – p^{(1)}||_1$), while dispersion ($\sigma_m$) remains relatively constant.

## Contribution to Research Frame

This source directly addresses the core of the research frame by providing a rigorous statistical and theoretical analysis of model collapse. It moves beyond anecdotal evidence to mathematically characterize the conditions under which collapse occurs and, importantly, identifies a critical threshold for synthetic data inclusion in partially synthetic training that can *avoid* collapse. This directly supports the frame's goal of identifying "specific technical thresholds and data-mixing ratios that trigger or prevent model collapse" and offers a "technical roadmap for sustainable synthetic data utilization" by demonstrating that collapse is not an absolute certainty but a function of data ratios and training strategies. The paper's findings on the inevitability of collapse in fully synthetic settings and its potential avoidance in mixed settings provide strong evidence for the frame's premise that model collapse is a "function of data-to-noise ratios and distribution drift that can be mathematically modeled and empirically mitigated."
