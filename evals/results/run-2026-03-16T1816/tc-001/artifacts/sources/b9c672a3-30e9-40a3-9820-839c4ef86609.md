# The Curse of Recursion: Training on Generated Data Makes Models Forget

**Publication:** arXiv:2305.17493v3 [cs.LG] 14 Apr 2024

**Authors:** Ilia Shumailov, Zakhar Shumaylov, Yiren Zhao, Yarin Gal, Nicolas Papernot, Ross Anderson

## Summary

This paper introduces and investigates the phenomenon of "model collapse," a degenerative process where generative models trained on their own outputs (or outputs from previous generations of models) progressively forget the original data distribution. The authors demonstrate this collapse across various model types, including Gaussian Mixture Models, Variational Autoencoders, and Large Language Models. They identify two primary causes: statistical approximation error (due to finite sampling) and functional approximation error (due to model expressiveness limitations). The research posits that this collapse is inevitable without access to the original, genuine data distribution, leading to a loss of information, particularly in the tails of the distribution, and a convergence towards a low-variance point estimate.

## Key Claims

*   Training generative models on data produced by previous generations of models leads to "model collapse," a degradation of performance.
*   Model collapse is characterized by the loss of information about the original data distribution, starting with the disappearance of tail-end events.
*   The primary causes of model collapse are statistical approximation error (finite samples) and functional approximation error (model limitations).
*   This process is universal across different types of generative models, including LLMs.
*   Access to genuine, human-generated data is crucial to prevent model collapse and sustain the value of large-scale datasets.

## Relevant Evidence/Quotes

*   "We discover that learning from data produced by other models causes model collapse – a degenerative process whereby, over time, models forget the true underlying data distribution, even in the absence of a shift in the distribution over time."
*   "In early model collapse the model begins losing information about the tails of the distribution; in the late model collapse model entangles different modes of the original distributions and converges to a distribution that carries little resemblance to the original one, often with very small variance."
*   "Statistical approximation error this is the primary type of error, which arises due to the number of samples being finite, and disappears as the number of samples tends to infinity."
*   "Functional approximation error this is a secondary type of error, which stems from our function approximators being insufficiently expressive (or sometimes too expressive outside of the original distribution support)."
*   "Indeed, the value of data collected about genuine human interactions with systems will be increasingly valuable in the presence of content generated by LLMs in data crawled from the Internet."

## Contribution to/Challenge of Research Frame

This source directly supports the inferred research frame by providing a foundational technical explanation and evidence for the phenomenon of model collapse. It mathematically and empirically demonstrates that recursive training on synthetic data leads to a quantifiable degradation of model performance, specifically through the loss of distributional diversity and variance. The paper's emphasis on the inevitability of this process without access to "fresh" real-world data aligns with the frame's focus on data hygiene and the critical role of real data. It offers concrete mechanisms (statistical and functional approximation errors) that contribute to the "distribution drift" and "variance loss" mentioned in the frame, suggesting that model collapse is indeed a function of data-to-noise ratios and distribution drift. The paper's findings challenge the idea that simply scaling up models or data indefinitely will lead to continuous improvement if the data itself is recursively generated, reinforcing the need for strategic data curation.
