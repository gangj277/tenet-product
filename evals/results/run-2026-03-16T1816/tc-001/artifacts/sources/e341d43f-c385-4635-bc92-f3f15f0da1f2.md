# Is Model Collapse Inevitable? Breaking the Curse of Recursion by Accumulating Real and Synthetic Data

**Publication Information:** This paper is a pre-print from researchers at Stanford University, Constellation, and the University of Maryland.

## Summary

This paper investigates the phenomenon of "model collapse," where generative models trained on their own synthetic outputs progressively degrade in performance. Previous research largely assumed that synthetic data replaces real data, leading to this collapse. However, this study proposes and empirically demonstrates that accumulating synthetic data alongside original real data, rather than replacing it, effectively avoids model collapse. This finding is supported by experiments across various generative models (language models, diffusion models for molecules, and VAEs for images) and is further substantiated by a theoretical framework using linear models, which shows that accumulating data leads to a bounded test error, unlike the linearly increasing error observed when data is replaced.

## Key Claims

*   Training generative models on their own synthetic outputs can lead to "model collapse," characterized by a progressive degradation of performance.
*   Previous studies on model collapse often assumed synthetic data replaces real data, a scenario that indeed leads to performance degradation.
*   Accumulating synthetic data with the original real data, rather than replacing it, effectively avoids model collapse.
*   This phenomenon holds true across different model architectures (transformers, diffusion models, VAEs) and data modalities (text, molecules, images).
*   A theoretical framework using linear models confirms that accumulating data results in a bounded test error, preventing collapse, while data replacement leads to unbounded error.

## Relevant Evidence/Quotes

*   "We confirm that replacing the original real data by each generation's synthetic data does indeed tend towards model collapse, then demonstrate that accumulating the successive generations of synthetic data alongside the original real data avoids model collapse; these results hold across a range of model sizes, architectures, and hyperparameters."

## Contribution to Research Frame

This source directly addresses and significantly contributes to the inferred research frame by providing strong empirical and theoretical evidence that model collapse is *not* an inevitable mathematical certainty. Instead, it demonstrates that the method of data handling—specifically, accumulation versus replacement—is a critical factor in mitigating or causing collapse. The findings suggest that "data hygiene" through accumulation, rather than strict avoidance of synthetic data, can be a viable strategy. This challenges the "doom" narrative by offering a technical solution (data accumulation) that can lead to "actionable architectural and data-curation safeguards." The paper's theoretical analysis also provides a basis for understanding the "specific mathematical indicators" of collapse and how different data strategies influence them.
