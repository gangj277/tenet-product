# Gaps in Research: Model Collapse from Recursive Synthetic Data Training

This document outlines the current state of knowledge regarding model collapse. While the field has moved beyond the initial panic of "inevitable collapse," significant gaps remain in defining the boundaries between manageable engineering challenges and fundamental mathematical limits.

### 1. Unresolved Questions
These questions represent the "known unknowns" that currently prevent a unified theory of recursive training:
*   **Scaling Validity:** Most empirical evidence is derived from small-scale models (e.g., GPT-2, OPT-125m). It is unclear if the "accumulation" mitigation strategy scales to frontier models (e.g., Llama-3, GPT-4) where the parameter-to-data ratio is vastly different.
*   **Stochasticity vs. Determinism:** The impact of generation temperature on collapse rates is under-researched. Does deterministic generation (temperature 0) accelerate the erosion of the "long tail" compared to stochastic sampling?
*   **Standardized Metrics:** There is no consensus on the mathematical threshold that separates "model specialization" (a desired outcome) from "model collapse" (a failure state). We lack a standardized metric to quantify the onset of collapse across different architectures.
*   **Teacher-Student Dynamics:** Can high-quality synthetic data generated by a "teacher" model improve a "student" model without introducing the recursive degradation observed in self-training loops?

### 2. Contradictions
The literature currently presents two conflicting frameworks for understanding the nature of model collapse:
*   **Inevitability vs. Artifact:** One school of thought posits that model collapse is a fundamental mathematical certainty driven by information entropy and the loss of tail distributions. An opposing view argues that "collapse" is primarily an artifact of poor data management (e.g., the "Replace" workflow) and is not an inherent property of recursive training.
*   **Asymptotic Divergence:** While empirical studies show that "Accumulate" workflows stabilize performance, theoretical analysis of Gaussian KDEs suggests that even with accumulation, the model will eventually diverge asymptotically. The disagreement lies in whether this divergence is "glacial" enough to be ignored in practical engineering or if it represents a hidden, long-term failure mode.

### 3. Weak Evidence Areas
Claims in these areas should be treated with caution:
*   **Threshold Generalization:** The "threshold of benefit" (e.g., the claim that synthetic data helps only when real data is below ~1024 points) is highly dependent on specific datasets like TinyStories. There is thin evidence to suggest these specific numerical thresholds hold for complex, multi-modal, or domain-specific datasets.
*   **Fixed Compute Constraints:** Evidence regarding the interaction between "Accumulate" workflows and fixed compute budgets is limited. We know that subsampling to maintain a fixed budget accelerates degradation, but we lack a clear understanding of the trade-off curve between compute efficiency and long-term model stability.

### 4. What Would Change Confidence
To move from the current state of uncertainty to actionable engineering guidelines, the following evidence is required:
*   **Large-Scale Ablations:** Empirical studies on models with >7B parameters that specifically measure the "tail erosion" rate when trained on synthetic data, comparing "Accumulate" vs. "Replace" workflows.
*   **Long-Horizon Stability Tests:** Experiments extending beyond 10 generations of recursive training to determine if the "plateau" observed in current studies is a stable equilibrium or a very slow, long-term decline.
*   **Formal Metric Definition:** A peer-reviewed proposal for a standardized "Collapse Index" that combines FID, perplexity, and entropy-based measures to provide a consistent way to monitor model health during synthetic data integration.
*   **Architectural Sensitivity Analysis:** Comparative studies between Transformer-based LLMs and Diffusion models to determine if the mechanisms of collapse are universal or architecture-specific.
