# Data Value in the Age of Scaling: Understanding LLM Scaling Dynamics Under Real-Synthetic Data Mixtures

**Authors:** Haohui Wang, Jingyuan Qi, Jianpeng Chen, Jun Wu, Lifu Huang, Edward Bowen, Lecheng Zheng, Kevin Choi, Balaji Veeramani, Alison Hu, Tyler Cody, Dawei Zhou

## Summary

This paper investigates the impact of mixing real and synthetic data on the training dynamics and generalization capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs). The authors identify a three-phase scaling behavior characterized by rapid learning, a plateau, and tail learning, linked to the long-tail distribution of knowledge in real-world data. They propose a novel LLM generalization bound for real-synthetic mixtures and derive a computationally efficient data valuation method based on this bound. Experiments across various tasks demonstrate that their method effectively identifies valuable data subsets, outperforming existing baselines with significantly lower computational cost.

## Key Claims

*   LLMs trained on mixed real and synthetic datasets exhibit a three-phase scaling behavior: Rapid-Learning, Plateau, and Tail-Learning.
*   Synthetic data generation methods often exacerbate the long-tail distribution of knowledge by underrepresenting rare (tail) knowledge.
*   A novel LLM generalization bound for real-synthetic mixtures reveals key factors influencing performance, including empirical losses, distribution discrepancies, and the neural tangent kernel (NTK).
*   A computationally efficient, retraining-free data valuation method is proposed, derived from the theoretical generalization bound.
*   This proposed data valuation method effectively identifies high-value data subsets and correlates well with ground truth across multiple tasks, with significantly lower computational cost than existing methods.

## Relevant Evidence/Quotes

*   "While synthetic data offers scalability and cost-efficiency, it often introduces systematic distributional discrepancies, particularly underrepresenting long-tail knowledge due to truncation effects from data generation mechanisms like top-p sampling, temperature scaling, and finite sampling."
*   "In particular, an initial Rapid-Learning Phase dominated by frequent (\"head\") knowledge present abundantly in both real and synthetic data; a subsequent Plateau Phase, in which additional data provides diminishing returns due to the limited coverage of rare (\"tail\") knowledge in synthetic data; and a final Tail-Learning Phase, where sufficient real data containing the tail knowledge enables further performance gains."
*   "Our proposed data valuation framework is directly derived from our LLM generalization bound, enabling computationally efficient and theoretically grounded estimation of the contributions of individual data subsets without retraining, thereby potentially improving training efficiency to guide the training process under real and synthetic mixtures."
*   "Notably, our valuation scores exhibit the highest correlation with ground-truth compared to the baseline methods, peaking at ~ 20× in the strongest case."

## Contribution to Research Frame

This source directly addresses the research frame by providing a technical analysis of how synthetic data can lead to distributional discrepancies, specifically impacting the "tail" distributions. It offers a theoretical explanation for this phenomenon through the identified three-phase scaling behavior and a derived generalization bound. Crucially, it proposes and empirically validates a practical solution (the data valuation method) to mitigate these issues by identifying valuable data subsets, thus supporting the idea that model collapse is a manageable engineering challenge rather than an inherent mathematical certainty. The findings align with the frame's emphasis on the interplay between synthetic data density and the preservation of tail distributions, and the efficacy of mitigation strategies.
