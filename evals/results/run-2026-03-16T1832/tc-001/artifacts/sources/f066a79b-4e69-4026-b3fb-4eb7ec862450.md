# The Curse of Recursion: Training on Generated Data Makes Models Forget

**Publication:** arXiv preprint (Authors affiliated with University of Oxford, University of Cambridge, Imperial College London, University of Toronto, Vector Institute)

## Summary

This paper introduces and defines "model collapse," a degenerative process where generative models, trained recursively on their own outputs, forget the original data distribution. This phenomenon occurs due to compounding statistical and functional approximation errors, leading to the disappearance of data distribution tails and eventual convergence to a low-variance point estimate. The authors demonstrate this across various model types, including Gaussian Mixture Models, Variational Autoencoders, and Large Language Models. They argue that to sustain the benefits of large-scale data, access to genuine human-generated content is crucial, as it becomes increasingly valuable in an environment polluted by model-generated data.

## Key Claims

*   Training generative models on data produced by previous generations of models leads to "model collapse."
*   Model collapse is a degenerative process where models forget the true underlying data distribution, characterized by the disappearance of distribution tails and convergence to a narrow output.
*   The primary cause of model collapse is statistical approximation error (due to finite samples), exacerbated by functional approximation error (model expressiveness limitations).
*   This phenomenon is universal across different model types, including GMMs, VAEs, and LLMs.
*   Access to genuine, human-generated data is essential to counteract model collapse and sustain long-term learning benefits.
*   Fine-tuning LLMs on generated data does not prevent model collapse; in fact, it can lead to degraded performance.

## Relevant Evidence/Quotes

*   "We discover that learning from data produced by other models causes model collapse – a degenerative process whereby, over time, models forget the true underlying data distribution, even in the absence of a shift in the distribution over time."
*   "In early model collapse the model begins losing information about the tails of the distribution; in the late model collapse model entangles different modes of the original distributions and converges to a distribution that carries little resemblance to the original one, often with very small variance."
*   "Statistical approximation error this is the primary type of error, which arises due to the number of samples being finite, and disappears as the number of samples tends to infinity."
*   "Functional approximation error this is a secondary type of error, which stems from our function approximators being insufficiently expressive..."
*   "The described process demonstrates that fine-tuning of language models does not curb the effects of Model Collapse and models that are being fine-tuned are also vulnerable."
*   "In other words, the use of LLMs at scale to publish content on the Internet will pollute the collection of data to train them: data about human interactions with LLMs will be increasingly valuable."
