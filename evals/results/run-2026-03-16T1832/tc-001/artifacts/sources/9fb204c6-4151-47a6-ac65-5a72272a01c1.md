# How Bad is Training on Synthetic Data? A Statistical Analysis of Language Model Collapse

**Authors:** Mohamed El Amine Seddik, Suei-Wen Chen, Soufiane Hayou, Pierre Youssef, Merouane Debbah

## Summary

This paper investigates the phenomenon of "model collapse," where models trained recursively on synthetic data generated by previous models degrade in performance and lose information about the original data distribution. The authors develop a statistical model for language models to analyze this phenomenon. They demonstrate that training solely on synthetic data inevitably leads to model collapse. However, when real and synthetic data are mixed, they provide an estimate of the maximum amount of synthetic data that can be used before collapse becomes unavoidable. These theoretical findings are supported by empirical validations.

## Key Claims

*   Model collapse occurs when models are trained recursively on synthetic data generated by previous models, leading to a loss of information about the original data distribution, particularly its "tails."
*   Training exclusively on synthetic data guarantees model collapse.
*   When training on a mixture of real and synthetic data, it is possible to avoid model collapse if the proportion of synthetic data remains below a certain threshold.
*   The rate of collapse in the "fully synthetic" scenario is exponential with respect to the number of generations and polynomial with respect to the synthetic sample size.
*   In the "partially synthetic" scenario, the deviation of the model from the initial distribution can be controlled by the amount of real data used, with the amount of synthetic data needing to be exponentially smaller than real data to ensure minimal deviation.

## Contribution to Research Frame

This source directly supports the research frame by providing a rigorous statistical and theoretical analysis of model collapse. It confirms that collapse is a probabilistic outcome of information entropy, specifically detailing how the loss of "tail" data occurs in recursive training. The paper quantifies the conditions under which this collapse is inevitable (fully synthetic data) and demonstrates that it can be managed or arrested through the strategic integration of "fresh" real data, aligning with the frame's assertion that collapse is not an inherent certainty. The theoretical bounds and empirical evidence presented offer a strong foundation for understanding the technical thresholds and causal mechanisms of model collapse, and the paper implicitly validates the efficacy of data mixing as a mitigation strategy.
