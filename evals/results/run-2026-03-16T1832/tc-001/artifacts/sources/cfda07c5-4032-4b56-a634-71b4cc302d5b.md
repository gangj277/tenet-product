# Collapse or Thrive? Perils and Promises of Synthetic Data in a Self-Generating World

**Publication:** arXiv preprint arXiv:2407.00616

**Authors:** Joshua Kazdan, Rylan Schaeffer, Apratim Dey, Matthias Gerstgrasser, Rafael Rafailov, David Donoho, Sanmi Koyejo

## Summary

This paper investigates the phenomenon of "model collapse," where generative models trained on their own synthetic outputs degrade in performance over successive generations. The authors explore three different training workflows: "replace" (synthetic data entirely replaces real data each generation), "accumulate" (all data, real and synthetic, is kept), and "accumulate-subsample" (data accumulates, but each training iteration uses a fixed-size subsample). Through experiments in multivariate Gaussian estimation, kernel density estimation, and language model fine-tuning, they confirm that the "replace" workflow leads to collapse. The "accumulate" workflow effectively prevents collapse, and the "accumulate-subsample" workflow shows a slower, more gradual degradation, suggesting that model collapse is not an inevitable outcome but depends heavily on data management strategies. The study also highlights that the amount of real data matters: scarce real data can benefit from synthetic augmentation, while abundant real data can be harmed by it.

## Key Claims

*   The "replace" training workflow, where all previous data is discarded and replaced by newly generated synthetic data at each iteration, consistently leads to model collapse across various generative modeling tasks.
*   The "accumulate" training workflow, which retains all real and synthetic data from previous iterations, successfully prevents model collapse, keeping model performance stable.
*   A "fixed compute" or "accumulate-subsample" workflow, where data accumulates but training uses a fixed-size subsample, results in performance degradation that is slower and more gradual than in the "replace" scenario, thus avoiding explosive divergence.
*   The quantity of real data significantly influences the impact of synthetic data: a small amount of synthetic data can improve performance when real data is scarce, but it can degrade performance when real data is abundant.
*   Model collapse is not an inherent mathematical certainty but is highly dependent on the chosen data training workflow and the management of real versus synthetic data.

## Relevant Evidence/Quotes

*   "we confirm that the training-workflow of replacing all real data by successive generations of purely synthetic data indeed suffers model collapse in all task-settings studied"
*   "we consider the training-workflow of accumulating synthetic data alongside real data and training on all data combined and confirming that, although the proportion of real data eventually becomes zero, models remain stable and their test losses do not diverge under this training-workflow"
*   "In this training-workflow, we observe slow and gradual rather than explosive degradation of test loss performance across generations." (referring to accumulate-subsample)
*   "when real data are scarce, adding a small amount of synthetic data can reduce test loss, whereas when real data are ample, any synthetic data increases the test loss calculated on real data."
*   "In our experiments, when the number of real data is 1024 or lower, we find that there is a small but non-zero amount of synthetic data that improves the test loss when it is included."
*   "On the other hand, when real data are plentiful (more than 1024 datapoints), we find that more synthetic data almost always degrades final model quality when the number of real data is held constant."

## Contribution to Research Frame

This source directly addresses the core of the research frame by empirically investigating the conditions under which "model collapse" occurs and can be mitigated. It provides strong evidence against the inevitability of collapse, demonstrating that specific data management strategies (like accumulation) can prevent it. The findings on the interplay between real and synthetic data quantity also offer crucial insights into how to manage data pipelines to sustain high-fidelity performance over multiple generations, aligning with the frame's focus on the "manageable engineering challenge" aspect and the "conditions under which high-fidelity performance can be sustained." The paper's experimental validation across multiple task settings strengthens the argument that these phenomena are generalizable.
