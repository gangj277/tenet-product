## Characterizing Model Collapse in Large Language Models Using Semantic Networks and Next-Token Probability

**Publication Info:** (Not explicitly stated in the provided text, but appears to be a research paper)

**Summary:**
This paper investigates the phenomenon of "model collapse" in Large Language Models (LLMs), which occurs when models are fine-tuned on their own generated outputs, leading to a degradation in performance and diversity. The authors propose a more comprehensive approach to characterizing model collapse than previously used, employing semantic networks to analyze text repetitiveness and diversity, and next-token probabilities to quantify loss of diversity. They also examine the impact of synthetic token proportions on collapse severity and conduct cross-dataset evaluations to understand domain-specific variations. The study aims to provide new insights for developing more robust generative AI systems by offering detailed metrics and strategies for assessing model collapse.

**Key Claims:**

*   Recursive fine-tuning of LLMs on their own outputs (autophagy) can lead to model collapse, characterized by degraded performance and reduced diversity.
*   Existing characterizations of model collapse are often simplistic and lack comprehensive evaluation.
*   Semantic networks can be used to analyze textual repetitiveness and semantic diversity, revealing structural changes in generated text during collapse.
*   Next-token probabilities, specifically metrics like the Gini coefficient and the number of "collapsed predictions" (tokens with probability > 0.999), can quantitatively measure diversity loss.
*   The proportion of synthetic tokens used in fine-tuning influences the severity of model collapse.
*   Model collapse can manifest differently across various text domains.
