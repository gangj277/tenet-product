# AI Models Collapse When Trained on Recursively Generated Data

**Publication:** arXiv preprint arXiv:2301.00109

## Summary

This paper introduces and defines the phenomenon of "model collapse," a degenerative process where generative AI models, particularly Large Language Models (LLMs), degrade over successive generations of training. This collapse occurs when models are trained on data generated by their predecessors, leading to an irreversible loss of information about the original data distribution, characterized by the disappearance of "tails" (low-probability events) and convergence towards a narrow, high-density point. The authors demonstrate this phenomenon theoretically using discrete distributions and Gaussian approximations, and empirically with LLMs, showing that even with a small percentage of original data preserved, performance degrades significantly without it. The research highlights the critical need to maintain access to original, human-generated data to sustain the benefits of large-scale AI training.

## Key Claims

*   Generative AI models can suffer from "model collapse," a process where they forget the true underlying data distribution over successive generations of training on their own outputs.
*   Model collapse is characterized by the disappearance of "tails" (low-probability events) from the data distribution and a convergence towards a point estimate with very small variance.
*   This collapse is caused by a compounding of statistical approximation error, functional expressivity error, and functional approximation error.
*   The phenomenon is universal across different types of generative models, including LLMs, Variational Autoencoders (VAEs), and Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs).
*   Even with a small percentage (10%) of original training data preserved, significant performance degradation is observed in LLMs trained recursively on generated data.
*   The value of data collected from genuine human interactions will increase as LLM-generated content pollutes the internet, making it crucial to preserve access to original data sources.

## Relevant Evidence/Quotes

*   "We find that indiscriminate use of model-generated content in training causes irreversible defects in the resulting models, in which tails of the original content distribution disappear. We refer to this effect as 'model collapse'..."
*   "We discover that indiscriminately learning from data produced by other models causes ‘model collapse’—a degenerative process whereby, over time, models forget the true underlying data distribution, even in the absence of a shift in the distribution over time."
*   "Model collapse is a degenerative process affecting generations of learned generative models, in which the data they generate end up polluting the training set of the next generation. Being trained on polluted data, they then mis-perceive reality."
*   "In early model collapse, the model begins losing information about the tails of the distribution; in late model collapse, the model converges to a distribution that carries little resemblance to the original one, often with substantially reduced variance."
*   "We find that training with generated data allows us to adapt to the underlying task, losing some performance, from 20 to 28 perplexity points." (Referring to training without original data)
*   "We find that preservation of the original data allows for better model fine-tuning and leads to only minor degradation of performance." (Referring to training with 10% original data)
*   "The value of data collected about genuine human interactions with systems will be increasingly valuable in the presence of LLM-generated content in data crawled from the Internet."

## Contribution to/Challenge of Research Frame

This source directly supports the inferred research frame by providing a technical and mathematical explanation for model collapse as a probabilistic outcome of information loss, specifically the erosion of "tail" data. It empirically demonstrates how this distribution narrowing occurs in LLMs and quantifies the performance degradation. The paper's emphasis on the inevitability of collapse without intervention and the critical need for "fresh" human-generated data aligns perfectly with the frame's assertion that collapse is not inevitable and can be arrested by strategic data integration. The research offers strong evidence for the "probabilistic outcome of information entropy" and the "loss of low-probability 'tail' data" leading to distribution convergence. It also implicitly supports the idea that "novel training objectives" or "strategic integration of 'fresh' human-generated data" are necessary mitigation strategies, as shown by the performance difference when original data is preserved.
