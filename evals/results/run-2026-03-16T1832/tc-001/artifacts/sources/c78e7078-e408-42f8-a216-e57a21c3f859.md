# Al models collapse when trained on recursively generated data

**Authors:** Ilia Shumailov¹,⁸, Zakhar Shumaylov²,⁸, Yiren Zhao³, Nicolas Papernot⁴,⁵, Ross Anderson⁶,⁷,⁹ & Yarin Gal¹

## Summary

This paper introduces and defines the phenomenon of "model collapse," a degenerative process where generative AI models, when trained on data produced by previous generations of models, irreversibly lose information about the original data distribution. This occurs because the generated data pollutes the training set for subsequent models, causing them to misperceive reality. The authors demonstrate this collapse across various model types, including Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs), Variational Autoencoders (VAEs), and Large Language Models (LLMs). They argue that this collapse is a universal issue for recursively trained generative models, driven by compounding statistical approximation, functional expressivity, and functional approximation errors. The paper highlights that preserving access to original, human-generated data is crucial to sustain the benefits of large-scale data training and prevent the degradation of AI model capabilities.

## Key Claims

*   Indiscriminate use of model-generated content in training causes irreversible defects in resulting models, leading to the disappearance of the tails of the original content distribution.
*   This phenomenon, termed "model collapse," affects LLMs, VAEs, and GMMs, and is a ubiquitous issue among learned generative models.
*   Model collapse is a degenerative process where models forget the true underlying data distribution over successive generations, even without an external distribution shift.
*   The collapse is driven by three compounding sources of error: statistical approximation error, functional expressivity error, and functional approximation error.
*   In discrete distributions with exact approximation, model collapse leads to the loss of information about low-probability events (tails) and eventual convergence to a delta function (a single point).
*   In Gaussian approximations, recursive training leads to divergence from the original distribution and a collapse towards zero variance.
*   Fine-tuning LLMs on data generated by other models also leads to model collapse, degrading performance over generations.
*   Preserving a portion of the original, human-generated training data is crucial to mitigate model collapse and maintain model performance.
*   The value of data collected about genuine human interactions will increase significantly as LLM-generated content becomes more prevalent online.

*   "We find that indiscriminate use of model-generated content in training causes irreversible defects in the resulting models, in which tails of the original content distribution disappear. We refer to this effect as 'model collapse'..."
*   "We discover that indiscriminately learning from data produced by other models causes ‘model collapse’—a degenerative process whereby, over time, models forget the true underlying data distribution, even in the absence of a shift in the distribution over time."
*   "Model collapse is a degenerative process affecting generations of learned generative models, in which the data they generate end up polluting the training set of the next generation. Being trained on polluted data, they then mis-perceive reality."
*   "The process is depicted in Fig. 1a. We separate two special cases: early model collapse and late model collapse. In early model collapse, the model begins losing information about the tails of the distribution; in late model collapse, the model converges to a distribution that carries little resemblance to the original one, often with substantially reduced variance."
*   "Indeed, the value of data collected about genuine human interactions with systems will be increasingly valuable in the presence of LLM-generated content in data crawled from the Internet."
*   "To sustain learning over a long period of time, we need to make sure that access to the original data source is preserved and that further data not generated by LLMs remain available over time."

## Contribution to Research Frame

This source directly supports the inferred research frame by providing a foundational technical explanation and empirical evidence for the phenomenon of model collapse. It mathematically and theoretically demonstrates how recursive training on synthetic data leads to distribution narrowing and the loss of "tail" data, framing it as a probabilistic outcome of information entropy. The paper's emphasis on the compounding errors and the eventual convergence to a point estimate (delta function) aligns with the idea that collapse is a "probabilistic outcome of information entropy."

Crucially, the paper also offers a key mitigation strategy: "preserving access to the original data source is crucial... and that further data not generated by LLMs remain available over time." This directly addresses the frame's assertion that collapse "can be arrested through strategic integration of 'fresh' human-generated data." The paper's findings suggest that while collapse is a strong tendency, it is not an absolute inevitability if proper data preservation strategies are employed. The research validates the empirical observation of distribution degradation and provides a theoretical underpinning for why it occurs.
