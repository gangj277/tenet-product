# Beyond the Black Box: A Survey on the Theory and Mechanism of Large Language Models

**Publication Info:** arXiv:2601.02907v2 [cs.CL] 12 Mar 2026

## Summary

This survey addresses the paradox of Large Language Models (LLMs) exhibiting immense empirical success while lacking a robust theoretical understanding, often treating them as "black boxes." The authors propose a unified lifecycle-based taxonomy, organizing LLM research into six stages: Data Preparation, Model Preparation, Training, Alignment, Inference, and Evaluation. Within this framework, they systematically review foundational theories and internal mechanisms, analyze core theoretical issues like data mixture justification and optimization dynamics, and identify frontier challenges such as the limits of synthetic data self-improvement and the origins of emergent intelligence. The goal is to transition LLM development from engineering heuristics to a principled scientific discipline.

## Key Claims

*   Despite the monumental engineering successes of LLMs, theoretical understanding remains nascent, leading to them being treated as "black boxes."
*   A unified lifecycle-based taxonomy (Data Preparation, Model Preparation, Training, Alignment, Inference, Evaluation) can organize the fragmented theoretical landscape of LLMs.
*   Fundamental theoretical questions in data preparation include how to guarantee better data utilization and how data characteristics affect model performance.
*   The efficacy of data mixtures is rooted in theories of Domain Adaptation and Multi-Task Learning, with models generalizing better when trained on heterogeneous data.
*   Data deduplication is crucial for improving generalization and reducing verbatim memorization, with theoretical understanding evolving from computational constraints to information-theoretic concepts.
*   Memorization in LLMs is a complex process beyond exact duplication, intertwined with learning and generalization, and can be quantified and potentially mitigated.
*   Frontier challenges include understanding the theoretical limits of synthetic data for recursive self-improvement and the potential for "model collapse."

## Contribution to Research Frame

This source directly contributes to the research frame by providing a comprehensive overview of the theoretical underpinnings of LLMs, explicitly addressing the "black box" problem. It offers a structured approach to understanding the various stages of LLM development, including the critical role of data preparation. The survey's discussion on synthetic data generation and its associated challenges, such as potential "model collapse," directly aligns with the project's focus on the technical thresholds and causal mechanisms of distribution degradation in recursive training. It highlights that while synthetic data is a frontier, theoretical hurdles remain, suggesting that collapse might not be an inherent certainty but rather a complex interplay of factors that could potentially be managed. The paper's emphasis on bridging empirical observations with rigorous scientific inquiry supports the project's goal of evaluating proposed solutions and determining conditions for sustained high-fidelity performance.
