# How Bad is Training on Synthetic Data? A Statistical Analysis of Language Model Collapse

**Publication:** arXiv preprint arXiv:2403.10177

## Summary

This paper investigates the phenomenon of "model collapse," where models trained recursively on synthetic data generated by previous models experience performance degradation. The authors develop a statistical model for language models to analyze this phenomenon. They demonstrate that training solely on synthetic data inevitably leads to model collapse, characterized by the disappearance of the original data distribution's tails. However, when real and synthetic data are mixed, they provide an estimate for the maximum amount of synthetic data that can be used before collapse becomes unavoidable. These theoretical findings are supported by empirical validation using both simplified statistical models and realistic GPT2-style models.

## Key Claims

*   Model collapse, defined as the deterioration of performance due to recursive training on synthetic data, is a significant concern for future language models.
*   Training exclusively on synthetic data generated by previous models inevitably leads to model collapse, where the model forgets the original data distribution.
*   When training on a mixture of real and synthetic data, it is possible to avoid model collapse if the proportion of synthetic data remains below a certain threshold.
*   The paper provides a theoretical framework and statistical quantities (e.g., $S_m$, $\|p^{(m)}\|_{\infty}$) to quantify the rate of model collapse.
*   In the "Fully Synthetic" scenario, total collapse (convergence to a Dirac mass) is unavoidable and its rate depends on the generation number, sample size, and initial distribution's diversity.
*   In the "Partially Synthetic" scenario, incorporating real data can mitigate or avoid collapse, with the maximum allowable synthetic data being dependent on the amount of real data available.
*   Empirical results with transformer-based models validate the theoretical findings, showing divergence in distribution shift in the fully synthetic setting and stability when real data is mixed.

## Contribution to Research Frame

This source directly supports the inferred research frame by providing a rigorous mathematical and empirical analysis of model collapse.

*   **"Model collapse is a probabilistic outcome of information entropy where the loss of low-probability 'tail' data leads to distribution convergence..."**: The paper explicitly models this by showing how the probability distribution $p^{(m)}$ converges to a Dirac mass (total collapse), losing information about the original distribution's tails. The quantities $S_m$ and $\|p^{(m)}\|_{\infty}$ are used to measure this convergence and loss of diversity.
*   **"...however, this process is not inevitable and can be arrested through strategic integration of 'fresh' human-generated data..."**: The core finding of the "Partially Synthetic" scenario directly addresses this. The paper demonstrates that by mixing real data with synthetic data, model collapse can be avoided, and provides theoretical bounds on the acceptable ratio of synthetic to real data.
*   **"...or novel training objectives."**: While not the primary focus, the paper's statistical model of next-token prediction and the analysis of recursive training implicitly touches upon the training process itself as a factor. The distinction between "Fully Synthetic" and "Partially Synthetic" training regimes highlights how the training data composition is key.

The paper's findings align with the frame's assertion that collapse is not an absolute certainty but depends on specific conditions, particularly the ratio of real to synthetic data. It provides the mathematical underpinnings and empirical evidence for why and how this phenomenon occurs and can be mitigated.
