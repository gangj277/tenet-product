# A Tale of Tails: Model Collapse as a Change of Scaling Laws

**Publication Info:** arXiv preprint (as of current knowledge, no formal journal publication details available)

## Summary

This paper investigates the phenomenon of "model collapse," where the performance of AI models degrades due to training on increasingly synthetic data generated by previous models. The authors propose a theoretical framework analyzing how scaling laws, which predict performance improvements with data and model size, are altered in the presence of synthetic data. They demonstrate that synthetic data generation often leads to "tail cutting" or "tail narrowing" of data distributions, causing a loss of scaling and potential model collapse. The research also explores mitigation strategies, such as mixing real and synthetic data, which can lead to a "grokking" phenomenon where performance initially plateaus before resuming improvement.

## Key Claims

*   The widespread use of generative AI models leads to an increasing proportion of synthetic data in training corpora.
*   This influx of synthetic data alters the underlying data distributions, typically by "cutting off" or "narrowing" the tails.
*   These distributional changes cause a breakdown of established neural scaling laws, leading to performance plateaus and potential model collapse.
*   Model collapse can manifest as a loss of scaling with dataset size, shifted scaling laws with the number of data generation generations, and the "un-learning" of skills.
*   Mixing a small proportion of original (human-generated) data with synthetic data can mitigate model collapse and induce a "grokking" phenomenon where performance eventually recovers.
*   Curating specific parts of the original data's tail is crucial for effective mitigation, with efforts needing to be focused around the cutoff point of the synthetic data's tail.

## Relevant Evidence/Quotes

*   "We develop a theoretical framework of model collapse through the lens of scaling laws. We discover a wide range of decay phenomena, analyzing loss of scaling, shifted scaling with number of generations, the 'un-learning' of skills, and grokking when mixing human and synthesized data."
*   "Theoretical derivations of scaling laws always assume a heavy-tailed distribution (power-law, aka Zipf) on the input features ('heavy tail in, power scaling law out')... But what distribution do AI-models generate when trained on such data? Figure 2 provides an empirical answer for a large scale LLM (Llama2-7B) and a transformer model trained on an arithmetic task. Regenerating heavy-tailed data affects the distribution in two possible ways: (1) 'Cutting off' the tail of the distribution and/or (2) 'Narrowing' the tail..."
*   **Theorem 2.1:** "Consider long-tail real-world data with exponent $\\beta > 1$, and let the cutoff for AI-generated data be $k$. Then, for large $k$ and $T$ samples from the AI, the test error of the downstream 'LLM' scales like $E_{\\text{test}} \\propto T^{-(\\beta-1)/\\beta} + k^{-(\\beta-1)} = \\min(T, k^\\beta)^{-(\\beta-1)/\\beta}$. Thus, as soon as $T \\geq k^\\beta$, the AI-generated sample size $T$ ceases to be a 'scalable' resource: collecting more AI-generated samples will not improve the performance of the downstream model, i.e performance plateaus and we lose scaling."
*   **Theorem 3.2 (Grokking with Tail Cutting):** "Consider a sample of size $T$ of which a proportion $\\pi$ comes from the true distribution $p$ and the remainder comes from a version $p'$ of $p$ with its tail chopped off at rank $k$. We have the following scaling laws for the Hutter LLM defined in (7). (A) Early-Stage Dynamics: For $T \\ll k^\\beta/\\pi$, it holds that $E_{\\text{test}} \\propto T^{-(1-1/\\beta)} + k^{-(\\beta-1)}$. (B) Later-Stage Dynamics: As soon as $T > Ck^\\beta/\\pi$ (where $C$ is an absolute constant), it holds that $E_{\\text{test}} \\propto (\\pi T)^{-(1-1/\\beta)}$. Thus, during this stage, we recover the unpolluted sample-size law scaling $T^{-(1-1/\\beta)}$, up to within a multiplicative constant $\\pi^{-(1-1/\\beta)}$..."
*   Experiments on LLMs (Llama2-7B) showed a "scaling law decay over several generations" and that mixing "a random 2% of original data with AI data across all fine-tuning phases. It significantly mitigates model collapse, with the emergence of a grokking curve."

## Contribution to Research Frame

This source directly addresses the core of the research frame by providing a theoretical and empirical investigation into the phenomenon of model collapse. It offers a mathematical explanation for *why* distribution degradation occurs (tail cutting/narrowing) and how this impacts scaling laws. Crucially, it moves beyond simply identifying the problem to proposing and validating mitigation strategies (data mixing, curated tail acquisition).

*   **Challenges:** The paper challenges the notion that scaling laws are universally applicable in the "synthetic data age." It demonstrates that the *nature* of the data distribution is paramount, not just its quantity.
*   **Supports:** It strongly supports the idea that model collapse is a probabilistic outcome tied to information entropy (loss of tail data) and that this process is not inevitable. The proposed mitigation strategies directly align with the frame's focus on arresting distribution narrowing and maintaining high-fidelity performance through strategic data integration. The "grokking" phenomenon described is a key piece of evidence for how performance can be sustained. The paper provides specific mathematical mechanisms (tail cutting/narrowing) and empirical validation (LLama2 experiments) for the phenomena discussed in the research frame.
