# Gaps in Learned Routing vs. Classical OR

This document outlines the current limitations and unresolved tensions in the literature regarding Neural Combinatorial Optimization (NCO) versus classical Operations Research (OR) methods.

## 1. Unresolved Questions
*   **Feasibility in Multi-Constraint Environments:** While NCO performs well on standard TSP/Knapsack, there is a lack of evidence regarding performance on problems with complex, hard constraints (e.g., VRP with Time Windows, heterogeneous fleets). It is unclear if neural architectures can maintain feasibility without relying on expensive classical repair heuristics.
*   **True Economic ROI:** There is no standardized framework for comparing the "total cost of ownership" of a solver. We lack studies that quantify the energy/time cost of training a neural model against the human/compute time required for hyperparameter tuning (e.g., via ParamILS) of classical metaheuristics.
*   **Integrity of Optimality Guarantees:** Can neural-hybrid solvers (e.g., learned branching policies) be formally verified to maintain the optimality guarantees inherent in classical Branch-and-Bound? Current literature focuses on performance metrics rather than formal correctness.

## 2. Contradictions
*   **Generalization vs. Active Search:** Literature is split on the necessity of training. Some sources claim NCO is inherently limited by the training distribution (generalization collapse), while others argue that "Active Search" (an inference-time optimization strategy) renders the model distribution-independent.
*   **The Value of Amortization:** There is a fundamental disagreement on the primary value proposition of ML in OR. One camp argues the value lies in *amortized construction* (fast inference for repeated tasks), while the other argues the value lies in *learned search guidance* (improving the efficiency of classical algorithms).
*   **REINFORCE Efficacy:** There is no consensus on the utility of REINFORCE. Some benchmarks exclude it as inefficient, while seminal NCO papers rely on it as a core component, suggesting the performance gap may be due to baseline implementation rather than the algorithm itself.

## 3. Weak Evidence Areas
*   **Real-World vs. Synthetic Benchmarks:** Most claims of "near-optimal performance" rely on 2D Euclidean TSP datasets. There is thin evidence regarding how these models behave on non-Euclidean, real-world logistics data where cost matrices are asymmetric and dynamic.
*   **Scaling Beyond N=100:** Evidence for NCO efficacy is heavily clustered around small-scale problems (n ≤ 100). Claims regarding scalability to large-scale industrial instances (n > 1000) are largely speculative or rely on decomposition techniques that have not been rigorously compared to state-of-the-art classical solvers like LKH3.

## 4. What Would Change Confidence
To move from experimental prototypes to production-grade confidence, the following evidence is required:

*   **Standardized "Total Cost" Benchmarks:** A study comparing the time-to-solution for a *new* problem instance, including the time required for model training/fine-tuning vs. the time required for classical solver parameter tuning.
*   **Constraint-Heavy Datasets:** Performance benchmarks on standard OR-Library instances (e.g., CVRPTW) that explicitly report the percentage of "infeasible" solutions generated by neural models before repair.
*   **Hybrid-Solver Formalism:** Research demonstrating that a neural-guided Branch-and-Bound solver can reach the same optimality gap as a standard solver while providing a formal proof of convergence or a bounded optimality gap.
*   **Out-of-Distribution (OOD) Stress Tests:** Systematic testing of models trained on uniform distributions against real-world, clustered, or adversarial distribution shifts to quantify the exact "generalization decay" rate.
