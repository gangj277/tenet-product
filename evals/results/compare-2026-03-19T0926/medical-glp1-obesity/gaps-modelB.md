# gaps.md — Uncertainty & Evidence Gaps (GLP-1 therapies for obesity; adults without diabetes)

## 1) Unresolved questions (what the evidence does *not* settle)

### A. Long-term safety (≥3 years) in *non-diabetic* adults
- **Unresolved:** What are the true incidence rates over time (not just trial-period risk) for:
  - pancreatitis
  - gallbladder/biliary events (including cholelithiasis/cholecystitis)
  - pancreatic exocrine insufficiency
  - GI complications (e.g., gastroparesis symptoms severe enough to change management)
  - serious adverse events overall and treatment-related discontinuation causes
- **Why it’s unresolved:** The strongest statements in the consolidated findings emphasize **short-term (≈68–72 week)** efficacy and **acute AEs**, while **openQuestions** explicitly include long-term (3+ years) for tirzepatide. For semaglutide, long-term data exist but are not clearly synthesized here for the *non-diabetes obesity* subgroup specifically.

### B. Weight maintenance after discontinuation and how fast regain occurs
- **Unresolved:** The evidence indicates weight increases after stopping semaglutide, but key clinician-relevant details are missing:
  - How much regain occurs at 1 year, 2 years post-withdrawal (quantified)
  - Whether the regain differs between **semaglutide vs tirzepatide**
  - Whether tapering vs abrupt discontinuation changes outcomes
  - Whether adjunct lifestyle/behavior interventions reduce regain sufficiently to alter policy
- **Why it’s unresolved:** “Chronic administration” is asserted, but the consolidated findings do not provide a standardized comparison across drugs, doses, and discontinuation protocols.

### C. Lean mass vs fat mass loss (body composition)
- **Unresolved:** What proportion of weight loss is attributable to **lean muscle mass loss** vs fat mass in non-diabetic adults, and how this changes with:
  - dose escalation
  - duration of therapy
  - concurrent resistance training or protein intake
- **Why it’s unresolved:** This is explicitly listed under **openQuestions** (“impact … on lean muscle mass loss versus fat mass loss”), suggesting the current synthesis lacks sufficient body-composition endpoints across trials/meta-analyses.

### D. Cardiovascular (CV) outcomes / MACE reduction *in people without diabetes*
- **Unresolved:** Do these therapies reduce MACE in **non-diabetic obesity** populations, and by how much?
- **Why it’s unresolved:** The consolidated findings mention cardiometabolic benefits (BP, CRP) but do not establish the presence (or absence) of **hard CV outcome evidence** specifically in non-diabetic cohorts.

### E. Oral vs injectable semaglutide equivalence (and real-world feasibility)
- **Unresolved:** Can **oral semaglutide** achieve comparable weight loss to **injectable 2.4 mg** in non-diabetic adults, and with what safety/tolerability tradeoffs?
- **Why it’s unresolved:** This appears as an open question; the consolidated findings do not synthesize oral-formulation efficacy against the obesity-population benchmark.

## 2) Contradictions (where sources directly disagree)

### A. Pancreatitis risk: “no increase” vs “cases reported requiring monitoring”
- **Conflict:** One part of the synthesis notes that **some sources suggest no increase in pancreatitis risk** based on CV outcome datasets, while other clinical/trial-era reports (example given: **AWARD-2**) flag **pancreatitis cases** for particular GLP-1 agents.
- **Actionable implication:** A clinician cannot yet rely on a single consistent absolute-risk estimate:
  - Is pancreatitis risk truly null/inconsequential, or just **rare but non-zero**, or potentially **under-detected**?
- **What’s needed to reconcile:** A drug- and indication-specific meta-analysis using consistent case definitions (confirmed pancreatitis vs suspected), longer follow-up, and careful adjudication methods.

## 3) Weak evidence areas (thin/low-quality reliance)

### A. Tirzepatide evidence synthesis may be less stable due to heterogeneity
- **Weakness indicated:** Confidence notes mention **high heterogeneity (I² > 90%)** in tirzepatide meta-analyses, attributed to baseline BMI and trial duration variability.
- **Why it matters:** Reported weight loss magnitudes (and any dose ranking) may shift materially depending on:
  - which trials are included/excluded
  - whether analyses adjust for baseline BMI and concomitant lifestyle arms
  - how estimands are defined (ITT, completers, treatment policy)

### B. Cardiometabolic claims may be surrogate-outcome–heavy
- **Weakness:** Improvements in BP and CRP are supported, but these are **surrogates**, not definitive clinical endpoints.
- **Why it matters:** Without linking to hard outcomes (MACE, renal failure, etc.) in **non-diabetic obesity**, the “clinically meaningful benefit” magnitude remains partially inferred.

### C. Safety statements are broader than the evidence supports
- **Example:** Gallbladder/biliary disease risk is asserted as higher with GLP-1 RAs, and pancreatitis is flagged as rare but serious—however, the consolidated findings do not clearly quantify:
  - absolute risk per 1,000 patient-years
  - whether risk differs by dose (e.g., semaglutide 2.4 vs lower doses; tirzepatide 5/10/15)
  - whether risk differs by obesity severity or prior gallstone history
- **Why it matters:** Relative risks without consistent absolute event rates are less usable for shared decision-making.

### D. Body composition and muscle-sparing effects are not sufficiently evidenced here
- **Weakness:** Lean vs fat mass outcomes are recognized as a gap, implying current claims may rely on indirect inference from weight loss totals rather than direct DEXA/MRI endpoints.

## 4) What would change confidence (specific evidence/studies that would materially affect the synthesis)

### A. Long-term extension trials and/or registries in non-diabetic obesity
To materially increase confidence in safety and durability, prioritize:
- **≥3–5 year** extension data for **tirzepatide** (and semaglutide where not already stratified by non-diabetes status)
- adjudicated event reporting for **pancreatitis** and **biliary events**
- treatment discontinuation follow-up cohorts (standardized regain measurements)
- preferably with obesity-defined eligibility (BMI criteria) rather than mixed diabetes/non-diabetes populations unless subgroup stratification is possible

**What to look for (study features):**
- clear denominator (patient-years)
- standardized AE definitions and adjudication
- accounting for baseline gallstone/pancreatitis risk
- reporting of GI adverse event severity (not just incidence)

### B. Head-to-head or network meta-analyses with consistent estimands (semaglutide 2.4 vs tirzepatide doses)
Confidence would rise if analyses:
- harmonize outcome definitions (e.g., percent change at week 68–72)
- adjust for baseline BMI, age, sex, and trial lifestyle intervention intensity
- quantify dose-response uncertainty (ranking validity) under heterogeneity

### C. Body composition-focused studies in non-diabetic adults
Confidence would materially change with:
- RCTs or high-quality meta-analyses reporting **DEXA/MRI-derived fat mass and lean mass changes** (including change in appendicular lean)
- interaction tests with resistance training or diet-protein targets

### D. CV outcomes (MACE) in *non-diabetic* obesity populations
To move from surrogate cardiometabolic improvements to clinical benefit:
- event-driven trials or robust observational cohort studies (with careful confounding control) in **non-diabetic obesity** using GLP-1/tirzepatide exposure
- or subgroup analyses from large CV outcome datasets that clearly report results for participants without diabetes (not merely mixed populations)

### E. Oral semaglutide equivalence trials (non-diabetic obesity)
Confidence would improve if there are:
- RCTs comparing **oral semaglutide** (doses and formulation details) vs injectable 2.4 mg
- and safety/tolerability comparisons focused on GI events leading to discontinuation
