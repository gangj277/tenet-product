# KARMA: Leveraging Multi-Agent LLMs for Automated Knowledge Graph Enrichment

**Publication:** NeurIPS 2025 San Diego Poster Session 2

## Summary

This paper introduces KARMA, a novel framework designed to automate the enrichment of knowledge graphs (KGs) using multi-agent large language models (LLMs). Recognizing the limitations of manual curation in keeping pace with scientific literature, KARMA employs nine specialized agents to discover entities, extract relations, align schemas, and resolve conflicts. These agents work collaboratively to parse documents, verify extracted information, and integrate it into existing KGs while adhering to domain-specific schemas. Experimental results on 1,200 PubMed articles demonstrate KARMA's effectiveness, showing the identification of numerous new entities, high LLM-verified correctness, and a significant reduction in conflict edges.

## Key Claims

*   Manual curation of knowledge graphs (KGs) cannot scale with the rapid growth of scientific literature.
*   Multi-agent LLMs can automate KG enrichment by analyzing unstructured text.
*   KARMA utilizes nine collaborative agents for entity discovery, relation extraction, schema alignment, and conflict resolution.
*   The KARMA framework iteratively parses documents, verifies extracted knowledge, and integrates it into KGs.
*   Experiments on PubMed articles show KARMA can identify new entities, achieve high correctness, and reduce conflict edges.

## Contribution to Research Frame

This source directly contributes to the research frame by providing a concrete example of how LLMs can be used to manage and enrich complex data structures like knowledge graphs. The multi-agent approach highlights a sophisticated method for handling the nuances of information extraction and integration, which is relevant to understanding how AI systems can manage and process vast amounts of information. While not directly about model collapse, the challenges of maintaining up-to-date and comprehensive data (KGs) mirror the challenges of maintaining distributional integrity in generative models. The success of KARMA in verifying correctness and reducing conflicts suggests that robust validation and conflict resolution mechanisms are key to managing complex AI systems, a principle that could be applied to mitigating model collapse.
