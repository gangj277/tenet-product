# Al models collapse when trained on recursively generated data

**Publication:** Nature, July 24, 2024
**Authors:** Ilia Shumailov, Zakhar Shumaylov, Yiren Zhao, Nicolas Papernot, Ross Anderson, Yarin Gal

This paper introduces and defines the phenomenon of "model collapse," where generative AI models, particularly Large Language Models (LLMs), degrade in performance over successive generations when trained on data produced by their predecessors. The authors demonstrate that this collapse is a universal issue affecting various model types (LLMs, VAEs, GMMs) and stems from compounding errors in statistical approximation, functional expressivity, and functional approximation. They show that this process leads to a loss of information about the original data distribution, particularly affecting low-probability events (the "tails"), and can result in models converging to a point estimate with very small variance, effectively forgetting the true data distribution. The research emphasizes the critical need to preserve access to original, human-generated data to sustain the benefits of large-scale training.

## Key Claims

*   Generative AI models trained recursively on their own generated data suffer from "model collapse," a degenerative process that degrades their performance and understanding of the original data distribution.
*   Model collapse is a universal phenomenon affecting various generative model architectures, including LLMs, VAEs, and GMMs.
*   The collapse is driven by three compounding error sources: statistical approximation error, functional expressivity error, and functional approximation error.
*   A key symptom of model collapse is the disappearance of the "tails" of the data distribution, meaning models forget or misrepresent low-probability events.
*   Over time, models affected by collapse converge towards a distribution with very small variance, losing information about the true underlying data.
*   This process is inevitable if models are indiscriminately trained on data produced by their predecessors.
*   Preserving access to original, human-generated data is crucial for sustaining the long-term benefits of training from large-scale web-scraped data.
*   Data about genuine human interactions will become increasingly valuable as LLM-generated content proliferates online.

## Relevant Evidence/Quotes

*   "We find that indiscriminate use of model-generated content in training causes irreversible defects in the resulting models, in which tails of the original content distribution disappear. We refer to this effect as 'model collapse'..."
*   "We discover that indiscriminately learning from data produced by other models causes ‘model collapse’—a degenerative process whereby, over time, models forget the true underlying data distribution, even in the absence of a shift in the distribution over time."
*   "Model collapse is a degenerative process affecting generations of learned generative models, in which the data they generate end up polluting the training set of the next generation. Being trained on polluted data, they then mis-perceive reality."
*   "In early model collapse, the model begins losing information about the tails of the distribution; in late model collapse, the model converges to a distribution that carries little resemblance to the original one, often with substantially reduced variance."
*   "The value of data collected about genuine human interactions with systems will be increasingly valuable in the presence of LLM-generated content in data crawled from the Internet."
*   The paper provides theoretical intuition using discrete distributions and multidimensional Gaussian approximations, showing that $E[W_2(\mathcal{N}(\mu_n, \Sigma_n), D_0)] \to \infty$ and $\Sigma_n \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$, indicating divergence from the original distribution and collapse to zero variance.
*   Experimental results with the OPT-125m language model show degraded performance (increased perplexity) when trained on generated data, with performance further degrading when less original data is preserved.

## Contribution to/Challenge of Research Frame

This source directly addresses the core of the research frame by providing a technical explanation and empirical evidence for "model collapse." It confirms that recursive training on synthetic data *can* lead to degradation, challenging the idea that advanced models can indefinitely improve by training on their own outputs without consequence.

*   **Confirms Technical Validity:** The paper rigorously defines model collapse and presents both theoretical arguments and empirical evidence (across different model types and modalities) to support its existence and inevitability under certain conditions. This directly validates the concern about the technical limitations of recursive synthetic training.
*   **Identifies Mechanisms:** It breaks down the causes into specific error types (statistical approximation, functional expressivity, functional approximation), providing a framework for understanding *why* collapse occurs. This is crucial for identifying potential mitigation points.
*   **Highlights Tail Distribution Loss:** The emphasis on the loss of "tails" (low-probability events) is a significant finding, suggesting that collapse isn't just a general performance drop but a specific form of distributional amnesia that can impact fairness and the modeling of rare but important phenomena.
*   **Challenges Indiscriminate Use:** The paper strongly argues against the indiscriminate use of model-generated content for training future models, directly challenging the assumption that simply scaling up data generation will lead to perpetual improvement.
*   **Suggests Mitigation:** While not detailing specific engineering solutions, it points towards the necessity of preserving access to original, human-generated data as a fundamental requirement for sustained learning, implying that data provenance and a mix of real/synthetic data are key to avoiding collapse.

In essence, this paper establishes model collapse as a fundamental technical challenge, not merely an engineering hurdle, that must be addressed to ensure the long-term viability of generative AI development. It provides the foundational understanding of the problem that the research frame seeks to explore and potentially overcome.
