# The Curse of Recursion: Training on Generated Data Makes Models Forget

**Publication:** arXiv:2305.17493v3 [cs.LG] 14 Apr 2024

This paper introduces and analyzes the phenomenon of "model collapse," a degenerative process where generative models trained on data produced by previous generations of models exhibit irreversible defects. The authors demonstrate that this collapse occurs across various model types, including Gaussian Mixture Models, Variational Autoencoders, and Large Language Models (LLMs). The core issue stems from the compounding of statistical approximation errors and functional approximation errors, leading to a loss of information about the original data distribution, particularly its "tails." The research posits that to sustain the benefits of large-scale web-scraped data, access to genuine human-generated content is crucial, as model-generated content will increasingly pollute training datasets.

## Key Claims

*   Training generative models on data produced by previous generations of models leads to "model collapse," a degenerative process.
*   Model collapse results in models forgetting the true underlying data distribution, with tails of the distribution disappearing first.
*   This phenomenon is observed across different model types, including VAEs, GMMs, and LLMs.
*   The primary causes of model collapse are statistical approximation error (due to finite samples) and functional approximation error (due to model expressiveness limitations).
*   Access to genuine human-generated content is essential to avoid model collapse and maintain the value of large-scale datasets.

## Relevant Evidence/Quotes

*   "We discover that learning from data produced by other models causes model collapse – a degenerative process whereby, over time, models forget the true underlying data distribution, even in the absence of a shift in the distribution over time."
*   "We give examples of model collapse for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs), Variational Autoencoders (VAE) and Large Language models (LLMs)."
*   "We show that over time we start losing information about the true distribution, which first starts with tails disappearing, and over the generations learned behaviours start converging to a point estimate with very small variance."
*   "The use of LLMs at scale to publish content on the Internet will pollute the collection of data to train them: data about human interactions with LLMs will be increasingly valuable."
*   "Statistical approximation error this is the primary type of error, which arises due to the number of samples being finite, and disappears as the number of samples tends to infinity. This occurs due to a non-zero probability that information can get lost at every step of re-sampling."
*   "Functional approximation error this is a secondary type of error, which stems from our function approximators being insufficiently expressive (or sometimes too expressive outside of the original distribution support...)."

## Contribution to/Challenge of Research Frame

This source directly supports the research frame by providing a foundational technical explanation and empirical evidence for the phenomenon of model collapse. It articulates the specific mechanisms (statistical and functional approximation errors) that drive the degradation of models trained on synthetic data. The paper's claim that model collapse is an "inevitable" process without access to genuine human data challenges the notion that recursive synthetic training can be indefinitely sustained through engineering alone. It strongly suggests that the "freshness" and authenticity of data are critical, aligning with the research's goal to understand the technical boundaries of synthetic data usage and the necessity of real-world data integration. The paper's emphasis on the loss of "tails" of the distribution is particularly relevant to the research's focus on maintaining distributional diversity.
