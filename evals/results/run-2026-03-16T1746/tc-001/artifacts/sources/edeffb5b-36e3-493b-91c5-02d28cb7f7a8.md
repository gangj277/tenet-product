# AI Models Collapse When Trained on Recursively Generated Data

**Publication:** Nature
**Date:** (Article published online: 06 May 2024; Author Correction: 21 March 2025)

## Summary

This paper introduces and defines the phenomenon of "model collapse," where generative AI models, particularly Large Language Models (LLMs), degrade in performance and lose information about the original data distribution when trained on data generated by previous versions of themselves. The authors demonstrate that this collapse is a universal issue across various generative models, including LLMs, Variational Autoencoders (VAEs), and Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs). They explain that the recursive training process, where models learn from their own outputs, leads to compounding errors that cause the models to forget rare events (tails of the distribution) and eventually converge to a narrow, inaccurate representation of reality. The research emphasizes the critical need to preserve access to original, human-generated data to sustain the benefits of large-scale training.

## Key Claims

*   **Model Collapse Defined:** Model collapse is a degenerative process where generative models trained on their own generated data lose information about the true underlying data distribution, leading to performance degradation.
*   **Universality:** Model collapse affects a wide range of generative models, including LLMs, VAEs, and GMMs, and is not limited to a specific architecture.
*   **Mechanism of Collapse:** The collapse is driven by compounding errors over generations, including statistical approximation error, functional expressivity error, and functional approximation error, which cause the disappearance of distribution tails and convergence to a narrow variance.
*   **Irreversibility:** Indiscriminate use of model-generated content in training causes irreversible defects in the resulting models.
*   **Consequences:** Models trained on recursively generated data misperceive reality, losing the ability to model low-probability events crucial for fairness and understanding complex systems.
*   **Mitigation Implication:** Access to original, human-produced data is crucial for long-term learning and preventing model collapse. The value of data about genuine human interactions will increase significantly.

## Relevant Evidence/Quotes

*   "We find that indiscriminate use of model-generated content in training causes irreversible defects in the resulting models, in which tails of the original content distribution disappear."
*   "We refer to this effect as ‘model collapse’ and show that it can occur in LLMs as well as in variational autoencoders (VAEs) and Gaussian mixture models (GMMs)."
*   "We give examples of model collapse for GMMs, VAEs and LLMs. We show that, over time, models start losing information about the true distribution, which first starts with tails disappearing, and learned behaviours converge over the generations to a point estimate with very small variance."
*   "Indeed, the value of data collected about genuine human interactions with systems will be increasingly valuable in the presence of LLM-generated content in data crawled from the Internet."

## Contribution to Research Frame

This source directly addresses the core concern of the research frame regarding the technical validity and inevitability of model collapse. It provides a formal definition and theoretical underpinnings for the phenomenon, establishing it as a fundamental mathematical certainty arising from recursive training loops. The paper's findings that model collapse causes the disappearance of "tails of the original content distribution" and leads to models "mis-perceiving reality" strongly support the hypothesis that recursive synthetic training can become unsustainable.

The paper's emphasis on compounding errors and the eventual convergence to a "point estimate with very small variance" highlights the mechanisms of distribution drift. It also implicitly challenges the idea that advanced sampling techniques alone can prevent collapse, as the phenomenon is shown to be universal across model types. The conclusion that "access to the original data source is preserved and that further data not generated by LLMs remain available over time" directly informs the research's exploration of mitigation strategies, particularly the critical role of integrating "fresh" real-world data. The paper's evidence against the long-term viability of purely synthetic training loops aligns with the research frame's goal to identify the boundary conditions for such approaches.
