# AI Models Collapse When Trained on Recursively Generated Data

**Publication:** Nature
**Date:** July 24, 2024
**DOI:** 10.1038/s41586-024-07566-y

## Summary

This paper introduces and defines the phenomenon of "model collapse," which occurs when generative AI models, particularly Large Language Models (LLMs), are trained on data that includes outputs from previous generations of models. This recursive training process leads to a degradation of the models' ability to represent the original data distribution, causing the loss of "tail" information and a convergence towards a narrower, less diverse output. The authors demonstrate this collapse across various model types (LLMs, VAEs, GMMs) and argue that it is an inevitable consequence of indiscriminate use of synthetic data, necessitating a greater value placed on genuine human-generated data for future AI development.

## Key Claims

*   Indiscriminate use of model-generated content in training causes irreversible defects in generative AI models.
*   This phenomenon, termed "model collapse," results in the disappearance of the "tails" of the original content distribution.
*   Model collapse can occur in various generative models, including LLMs, Variational Autoencoders (VAEs), and Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs).
*   The process is degenerative, leading models to forget the true underlying data distribution over time, even without external distribution shifts.
*   Learned behaviors in collapsed models converge to a point estimate with very small variance.
*   This collapse is inevitable, even under ideal learning conditions with no function estimation error.
*   Access to the original data distribution is crucial, especially for tasks where the tails of the distribution are important.
*   The increasing use of LLM-generated content online will "pollute" future training datasets, making genuine human interaction data increasingly valuable.

## Relevant Evidence/Quotes

*   "We find that indiscriminate use of model-generated content in training causes irreversible defects in the resulting models, in which tails of the original content distribution disappear."
*   "We refer to this effect as 'model collapse' and show that it can occur in LLMs as well as in variational autoencoders (VAEs) and Gaussian mixture models (GMMs)."
*   "We discover that indiscriminately learning from data produced by other models causes ‘model collapse’—a degenerative process whereby, over time, models forget the true underlying data distribution, even in the absence of a shift in the distribution over time."
*   "We show that, over time, models start losing information about the true distribution, which first starts with tails disappearing, and learned behaviours converge over the generations to a point estimate with very small variance."
*   "Furthermore, we show that this process is inevitable, even for cases with almost ideal conditions for long-term learning, that is, no function estimation error."
*   "Definition 2.1 (model collapse). Model collapse is a degenerative process affecting generations of learned generative models, in which the data they generate end up polluting the training set of the next generation. Being trained on polluted data, they then mis-perceive reality."
*   "In other words, the use of LLMs at scale to publish content on the Internet will pollute the collection of data to train their successors: data about human interactions with LLMs will be increasingly valuable."
