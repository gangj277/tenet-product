# DATA VALUE IN THE AGE OF SCALING: UNDERSTANDING LLM SCALING DYNAMICS UNDER REAL-SYNTHETIC DATA MIXTURES

**Publication Info:** Preprint, November 18, 2025

## Summary

This paper investigates the scaling dynamics of Large Language Models (LLMs) trained on mixtures of real and synthetic data. The authors identify a three-phase scaling behavior—Rapid-Learning, Plateau, and Tail-Learning—characterized by two breakpoints that signify transitions in how LLMs learn head and tail knowledge. They propose a novel LLM generalization bound tailored for real-synthetic data mixtures, highlighting key factors influencing performance. Based on this theoretical framework, they develop an efficient and scalable data valuation method that estimates data subset contributions without retraining, outperforming existing methods in experiments across various tasks.

## Key Claims

*   LLMs trained on real-synthetic data mixtures exhibit a three-phase scaling behavior: Rapid-Learning, Plateau, and Tail-Learning.
*   These phases are marked by two breakpoints related to the sample size and the proportion of real data, reflecting transitions in learning head versus tail knowledge.
*   Synthetic data generation often truncates the long-tail distribution of real-world knowledge, leading to underrepresentation of rare but important information.
*   A novel LLM generalization bound for real-synthetic mixtures reveals that generalization error depends on empirical losses, distribution discrepancies, the Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK), and data composition.
*   A scalable, retraining-free data valuation method is proposed, derived from the generalization bound, to identify valuable data subsets for LLM training.
*   The proposed data valuation method demonstrates superior performance and significantly lower computational cost compared to state-of-the-art baselines across multiple tasks.

## Relevant Evidence and Quotes

*   "While synthetic data offers scalability and cost-efficiency, it often introduces systematic distributional discrepancies, particularly underrepresenting long-tail knowledge due to truncation effects from data generation mechanisms like top-p sampling, temperature scaling, and finite sampling."
*   "In particular, an initial Rapid-Learning Phase dominated by frequent (\"head\") knowledge present abundantly in both real and synthetic data; a subsequent Plateau Phase, in which additional data provides diminishing returns due to the limited coverage of rare (\"tail\") knowledge in synthetic data; and a final Tail-Learning Phase, where sufficient real data containing the tail knowledge enables further performance gains."
*   "Our proposed data valuation framework is directly derived from our LLM generalization bound, enabling computationally efficient and theoretically grounded estimation of the contributions of individual data subsets without retraining, thereby potentially improving training efficiency to guide the training process under real and synthetic mixtures."
*   "Notably, we observe the predicted three-phase scaling behavior in an image classification task explicitly characterized by a known long-tail distribution."
*   "Furthermore, experimental results demonstrate that our valuation method outperforms existing baselines in effectively identifying high-value data subsets with a low computation cost. In particular, our valuation scores exhibit the highest correlation with ground-truth compared to the baseline methods, peaking at ~ 20× in the strongest case."

## Contribution to or Challenge of the Research Frame

This source directly addresses the research frame by providing a technical analysis of the challenges posed by using synthetic data in LLM training. It confirms the issue of distributional discrepancies and the underrepresentation of "tail knowledge," which aligns with the frame's concern about the loss of diversity. The identification of a three-phase scaling behavior and the proposed data valuation method offer concrete mechanisms and solutions for managing the balance between real and synthetic data. This work challenges the notion that model collapse is an inevitable outcome by proposing a method to actively guide training and mitigate the negative effects of synthetic data, suggesting that strategic data valuation can maintain model stability and performance.
