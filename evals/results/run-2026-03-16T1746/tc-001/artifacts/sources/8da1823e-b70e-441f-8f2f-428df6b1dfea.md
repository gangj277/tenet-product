# Is Model Collapse Inevitable? Breaking the Curse of Recursion by Accumulating Real and Synthetic Data

**Authors:** Matthias Gerstgrasser, Rylan Schaeffer, Apratim Dey, Rafael Rafailov, Dhruv Pai, Henry Sleight, John Hughes, Tomasz Korbak, Rajashree Agrawal, Andrey Gromov, Diyi Yang, David Donoho, Sanmi Koyejo

**Publication Info:** Stanford University, Constellation, University of Maryland, College Park, MIT & Sequoia Capital

## Summary

This paper investigates the phenomenon of "model collapse," where generative models trained on their own synthetic outputs progressively degrade in performance. Previous research often assumed that synthetic data replaces real data, leading to collapse. However, this study explores a more realistic scenario where synthetic data accumulates alongside real data. Through empirical experiments with language models, diffusion models for molecule generation, and variational autoencoders for image generation, the authors demonstrate that accumulating data, rather than replacing it, effectively avoids model collapse. This finding is further supported by a theoretical analysis using a tractable framework of linear models, which shows that accumulating data leads to a bounded test error, whereas replacing data results in unbounded error.

## Key Claims

*   Training generative models on their own synthetic outputs can lead to "model collapse," a progressive degradation of performance.
*   Previous studies on model collapse often assumed synthetic data *replaces* real data, a scenario that indeed tends towards collapse.
*   A more realistic scenario, where synthetic data *accumulates* with existing real data, was empirically and theoretically shown to *avoid* model collapse.
*   This avoidance of model collapse through data accumulation holds across various model types (transformers, diffusion models, VAEs) and data modalities (text, molecules, images).
*   Theoretically, accumulating data results in a finite upper bound for test error, independent of the number of training iterations, while replacing data leads to linearly increasing test error.

## Contribution to Research Frame

This source directly supports the inferred research frame that "Model collapse is not an absolute law but a function of data-to-noise ratios and training hygiene; the research tests the hypothesis that strategic integration of 'fresh' real-world data and specific sampling techniques can maintain model stability indefinitely."

Specifically, it provides strong empirical and theoretical evidence for the hypothesis that **strategic integration of 'fresh' real-world data** (by accumulating it with synthetic data) can maintain model stability. The paper demonstrates that the *mechanism* of data usage (replacement vs. accumulation) is a critical factor in preventing collapse, aligning with the idea that "training hygiene" is paramount. The theoretical analysis further quantifies how accumulation mitigates the impact of noise and errors over iterations, suggesting that it's not just about adding more data, but *how* that data is managed.

The findings challenge the notion that model collapse is an inevitable "curse of recursion," reframing it as a consequence of specific, potentially avoidable, training practices. The paper directly addresses the subquestion: "What is the critical threshold or ratio of real-to-synthetic data required to maintain distributional stability?" by showing that even a maximally pessimistic scenario of accumulating *only* synthetic data alongside an initial real dataset can avoid collapse, implying that the accumulation strategy itself is key. It also touches upon "Which mitigation strategies... show the most robust empirical success?" by highlighting data accumulation as a highly effective strategy.
