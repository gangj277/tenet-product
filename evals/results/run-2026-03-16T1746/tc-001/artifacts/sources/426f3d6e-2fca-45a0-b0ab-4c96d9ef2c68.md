# Is Model Collapse Inevitable? Breaking the Curse of Recursion by Accumulating Real and Synthetic Data

**Authors:** Matthias Gerstgrasser, Rylan Schaeffer, Apratim Dey, Rafael Rafailov, Dhruv Pai, Henry Sleight, John Hughes, Tomasz Korbak, Rajashree Agrawal, Andrey Gromov, Daniel A. Roberts, Diyi Yang, David Donoho, Sanmi Koyejo

**Publication Info:** Stanford University, University of Maryland, College Park, MIT, Sequoia Capital, Harvard University

## Summary

This paper investigates the phenomenon of "model collapse," where generative models trained on their own outputs progressively degrade in performance. Previous studies often assumed that new synthetic data replaces old data, leading to collapse. However, this research explores a more realistic scenario where data accumulates over time. Through empirical studies on language models, diffusion models for molecule generation, and variational autoencoders for image generation, the authors demonstrate that **accumulating synthetic data alongside original real data effectively avoids model collapse**. They further support this with a theoretical framework using linear models, proving that accumulating data leads to a finite upper bound on test error, thus preventing collapse, whereas data replacement results in unbounded error.

## Key Claims

*   Model collapse, characterized by performance degradation in generative models trained on their own outputs, has been a concern, particularly under data replacement scenarios.
*   Accumulating synthetic data with original real data, rather than replacing it, is shown to avoid model collapse across various generative model types (language, diffusion, VAEs) and data modalities.
*   Empirical results show that replacing data leads to increasing test error (model collapse), while accumulating data results in equal or lower test error over iterations.
*   A theoretical framework using linear models confirms that data accumulation leads to a finite, bounded test error, preventing collapse, whereas data replacement results in linearly increasing test error.
*   The study suggests that data accumulation is a robust strategy against model collapse and highlights the importance of considering real-world data dynamics in analyzing generative model training.

## Contribution to or Challenge of Research Frame

This source directly addresses the core question of the research frame regarding the inevitability of model collapse. It provides strong empirical and theoretical evidence that **model collapse is *not* inevitable**, specifically under the condition of data accumulation. This challenges the implicit assumption in some prior work that recursive training inherently leads to degradation.

The source's findings align with the inferred research frame that model collapse is a function of training dynamics rather than an absolute law. By demonstrating that data accumulation is a successful mitigation strategy, it suggests that "strategic integration of 'fresh' real-world data" (as mentioned in the frame) can indeed maintain model stability. The research specifically tests and validates the hypothesis that certain data-handling strategies can prevent the loss of diversity, directly contributing to understanding the "technical boundary conditions" and "which specific architectural or data-curation interventions effectively prevent the loss of tail-end distribution diversity."

The paper's emphasis on accumulating data as a way to avoid collapse provides a concrete answer to how "strategic integration of 'fresh' real-world data" can be conceptualized, even if the synthetic data itself is generated recursively. It suggests that the *process* of data growth, rather than just the *source* of data, is critical.
