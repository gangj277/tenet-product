# Model Collapse Demystified: The Case of Regression

**Publication Info:** arXiv preprint arXiv:2402.07712

## Summary

This paper provides a theoretical analysis of "model collapse," a phenomenon where models trained recursively on their own generated data degrade in performance. The authors study this in the context of high-dimensional regression, deriving analytic formulas that quantify the collapse. They show that iterative retraining on synthetic data leads to a multiplicative degradation in performance scaling with the number of generations. The paper also proposes adaptive regularization as a mitigation strategy and validates their theoretical findings with experiments.

## Key Claims

*   Model collapse occurs when a model is trained recursively on data generated by previous versions of itself, leading to performance degradation.
*   The paper derives analytic formulas for test error in high-dimensional regression under iterative retraining on synthetic data.
*   The test error can be expressed as $E_{\text{test}} = E_{\text{clean}} + \Delta \text{Bias} + n \times \text{Scaling}$, where the $n \times \text{Scaling}$ term quantifies the degradation due to iterative generation.
*   $\Delta \text{Bias}$ represents an increase in bias due to synthetic data generation, which can disappear if the generator is trained on sufficient data ($T_0 \ge d$).
*   Modified scaling laws are derived for power-law spectra, highlighting the negative impact of training on synthetic data.
*   Adaptive regularization can be used to mitigate model collapse, with an optimal parameter that differs from the classical theory for clean data.
*   In certain regimes, even without label noise ($\sigma_0 = 0$), model collapse can occur due to an increase in bias if the generator is under-parametrized.

## Relevant Evidence/Quotes

*   "In this work, we study this phenomenon in the setting of high-dimensional regression and obtain analytic formulae which quantitatively outline this phenomenon in a broad range of regimes."
*   "In $n$-fold iteration of data-generation, this formula writes $E_{\text{test}} = E_{\text{clean}} + \Delta \text{Bias} + n \times \text{Scaling}$."
*   "A direct consequence of (1) is that, as the number of generations $n$ becomes large, the effect of re-synthesizing will make learning impossible."
*   "We also propose a simple strategy based on adaptive regularization to mitigate model collapse."
*   "The non-negative term $\text{Scaling}$ precisely highlights the effects of all the relevant problems parameters like: the feature covariance matrix $\Sigma$, sample size $T$, label noise level in the clean data distribution $\sigma$, label noise level in the fake data distribution $\sigma_0^2$, etc."
*   "The nonnegative term $\Delta \text{Bias}$ is an increase in bias brought about by the iterative synthetic data generation process. This term disappears if each stage in the process was fitted on sufficiently many samples $T_0$ compared to the input dimension $d$ (i.e if $T_0 \ge d$)."

## Contribution to Research Frame

This source directly addresses the core research frame by providing a rigorous mathematical framework to understand the "distributional drift" and "statistical information loss" inherent in recursive training loops. It quantifies how the performance degrades ($n \times \text{Scaling}$) and identifies conditions under which this degradation is exacerbated ($\Delta \text{Bias}$). The derivation of analytic formulae allows for the identification of critical thresholds and the evaluation of mitigation strategies like adaptive regularization, directly contributing to understanding the technical boundary conditions and manageability of model collapse. The paper's focus on regression provides a foundational understanding that can inform analyses of more complex generative models.
