# DATA VALUE IN THE AGE OF SCALING: UNDERSTANDING LLM SCALING DYNAMICS UNDER REAL-SYNTHETIC DATA MIXTURES

## Summary

This paper addresses the challenges of training large language models (LLMs) on datasets that combine real and synthetic data. The authors observe that synthetic data, while scalable, often underrepresents long-tail knowledge due to generation biases, leading to performance degradation. They propose a three-phase scaling behavior (Rapid-Learning, Plateau, Tail-Learning) characterized by two breakpoints, and derive a generalization bound for real-synthetic mixtures. Based on this, they introduce a computationally efficient data valuation method that estimates data subset contributions without retraining, outperforming existing baselines across various tasks.

## Key Claims

*   LLMs trained on real-synthetic data mixtures exhibit a three-phase scaling behavior: Rapid-Learning, Plateau, and Tail-Learning, marked by two breakpoints.
*   Synthetic data generation methods often truncate long-tail knowledge, exacerbating the imbalance present in real-world data distributions.
*   A novel LLM generalization bound for real-synthetic mixtures reveals that generalization error depends on empirical losses, distribution discrepancies, the Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK), and the proportion of real data.
*   A scalable, retraining-free data valuation method is proposed, derived from the generalization bound, to identify valuable data subsets in mixed datasets.
*   The proposed data valuation method demonstrates superior performance and significantly lower computational cost compared to state-of-the-art baselines across image classification, sentiment classification, instruction following, and complex reasoning tasks.

## Relevant Evidence/Quotes

*   "While synthetic data offers scalability and cost-efficiency, it often introduces systematic distributional discrepancies, particularly underrepresenting long-tail knowledge due to truncation effects from data generation mechanisms like top-p sampling, temperature scaling, and finite sampling."
*   "In particular, an initial Rapid-Learning Phase dominated by frequent (\"head\") knowledge present abundantly in both real and synthetic data; a subsequent Plateau Phase, in which additional data provides diminishing returns due to the limited coverage of rare (\"tail\") knowledge in synthetic data; and a final Tail-Learning Phase, where sufficient real data containing the tail knowledge enables further performance gains."
*   "Theorem 1 (LLM Generalization Bound under Real and Synthetic Mixtures)... shows that the generalization error on the test distribution is bounded in terms of four key factors: (1) the empirical loss on training real samples and training synthetic samples; (2) the distribution discrepancy between test data and train data of real and synthetic mixtures; (3) the NTK-related value at initialization; and (4) the composition of the training dataset, specifically the proportion $\\pi$ of real data and the total number of samples $|S|$."
*   "Our proposed data valuation framework is directly derived from our LLM generalization bound, enabling computationally efficient and theoretically grounded estimation of the contributions of individual data subsets without retraining, thereby potentially improving training efficiency to guide the training process under real and synthetic mixtures."
*   "Comprehensive experiments across four tasks, including image classification, sentiment classification, instruction following, and complex reasoning, demonstrate that our method surpasses state-of-the-art baselines in data valuation with significantly low computational cost."

## Contribution to Research Frame

This source directly supports the inferred research frame by providing a theoretical and empirical analysis of how the mixture of real and synthetic data impacts LLM performance, specifically concerning the loss of "tail" knowledge. The identification of the three-phase scaling behavior and the derived generalization bound offer concrete mechanisms for understanding "distributional drift" and the critical role of the real data proportion ($\pi$). The proposed data valuation method offers a practical strategy to "arrest" this degradation by identifying and prioritizing valuable data subsets, aligning with the idea that maintaining a specific ratio of high-quality "anchor" (real) data is crucial. The paper empirically validates that the proposed method can effectively identify high-value data, suggesting that model collapse is indeed an "empirical challenge" that can be managed rather than an "inherent mathematical certainty."
