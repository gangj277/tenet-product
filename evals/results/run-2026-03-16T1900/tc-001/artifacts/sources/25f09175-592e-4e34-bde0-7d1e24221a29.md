# Rate of Model Collapse in Recursive Training

**Publication Info:** arXiv, December 24, 2024

## Summary

This paper theoretically analyzes the rate of model collapse in recursive training, where models are trained on data generated by previous models. The authors investigate this phenomenon for fundamental distributions, specifically discrete and Gaussian, using maximum likelihood estimation. They provide theoretical characterizations of collapse rates, finding that for discrete distributions, the forgetting of a symbol is roughly linearly dependent on its original occurrence count. For Gaussian models, the variance collapses to zero approximately at $n$ iterations, where $n$ is the number of samples per iteration. These findings suggest that model forgetting can be slow for simple distributions with a sufficient number of samples.

## Key Claims

*   Recursive training, where models learn from data generated by prior models, can lead to model quality degradation, known as model collapse.
*   For discrete distributions, the probability of a symbol not being forgotten after $k$ iterations is at least $1 - \exp(-\lambda_i k)$, where $\lambda_i$ is the count of symbol $i$ in the original dataset. This implies a slow collapse rate, approximately $1/k$.
*   For one-dimensional Gaussian models, the variance collapses to zero. The probability of the variance remaining above $\epsilon^2$ after $k$ iterations falls roughly as $e^{-k/(4n)}$, suggesting that the time to collapse is linearly dependent on the number of samples per iteration ($n$).
*   Similar collapse rates are observed for one-dimensional Gaussian mixtures.
*   The analysis suggests that model forgetting can be slow for simple distributions when a large number of samples are used per iteration.

## Contribution to or Challenge of Research Frame

This source directly addresses the core concern of the research frame regarding the technical validity and inevitability of model collapse. It provides a theoretical foundation for understanding *how* and *at what rate* model collapse occurs for fundamental distributions.

*   **Supports the frame:** The paper's findings that parameters (like variance or symbol probabilities) tend towards trivial values (zero or one) directly supports the idea of "distributional drift" and "information loss" mentioned in the frame. The characterization of collapse rates provides a quantitative aspect to this drift.
*   **Challenges/Refines the frame:** The paper's conclusion that collapse can be "slow for simple distributions with many samples" suggests that collapse is not necessarily an immediate or catastrophic event. It implies that the rate is dependent on specific parameters (distribution type, sample size), which could align with the frame's notion of identifying "specific thresholds and mitigation strategies." The focus on fundamental distributions might imply that more complex distributions or real-world data could exhibit different, potentially faster, collapse rates, which would require further investigation beyond this paper's scope. The paper does not directly discuss mixing real and synthetic data or architectural adjustments, which are key mitigation strategies in the frame, but it lays the groundwork for understanding the baseline collapse behavior that these strategies aim to counteract.
