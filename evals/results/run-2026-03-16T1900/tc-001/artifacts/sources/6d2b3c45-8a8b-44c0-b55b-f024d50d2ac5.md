# Recursive Collapse: Theory and Mitigation

**Publication Info:** Updated 7 December 2025

## Summary

This paper defines and analyzes "recursive collapse," a degenerative process in iterative systems where small errors are amplified over time, leading to a loss of diversity and accuracy. This phenomenon is observed across various domains, including generative models (language, vision), mathematical structures, and even in physical systems like MHD current sheets. The paper outlines the probabilistic foundations of recursive collapse, detailing how feedback amplification of approximation or sampling errors erodes representational fidelity. It also presents quantitative characterizations of collapse rates and explores various mitigation strategies, such as mixing real and synthetic data, loss function engineering, and importance-weighted resampling, to delay or prevent this degradation.

## Key Claims

*   Recursive collapse is a degenerative process in iterative systems that reduces diversity and accuracy through the amplification of small errors.
*   It manifests across generative models, mathematical structures, and physical systems, impacting language, vision, and topological systems with quantifiable collapse rates.
*   The phenomenon is fundamentally probabilistic, driven by the feedback amplification of approximation or sampling errors across iterations.
*   Preventing collapse requires superlinear growth of the sample schedule or a strictly non-zero contribution of genuine external data.
*   Mitigation strategies include mixing real and synthetic data, loss function engineering, importance-weighted resampling, parameter schedule optimization, and conditional domain anchoring.
