# Is Model Collapse Inevitable? Breaking the Curse of Recursion by Accumulating Real and Synthetic Data

**Authors:** Matthias Gerstgrasser, Rylan Schaeffer, Apratim Dey, Rafael Rafailov, Dhruv Pai, Henry Sleight, John Hughes, Tomasz Korbak, Rajashree Agrawal, Andrey Gromov, Diyi Yang, David Donoho, Sanmi Koyejo

**Publication Info:** Stanford University, Constellation, University of Maryland, College Park, MIT & Sequoia Capital

## Summary

This paper investigates the phenomenon of "model collapse," where generative models trained on their own outputs progressively degrade in performance. The authors challenge the common assumption that new synthetic data replaces old real data, proposing instead a more realistic scenario where data accumulates. Through empirical experiments with language models, diffusion models for molecule generation, and variational autoencoders for image generation, they demonstrate that accumulating synthetic data alongside original real data effectively avoids model collapse. This finding is further supported by a theoretical analysis using a tractable framework of linear models, which shows that accumulating data leads to a bounded test error, unlike the unbounded error observed when data is replaced. The research suggests that data accumulation is a robust strategy against model collapse, highlighting the importance of considering real-world data dynamics in generative model training.

## Key Claims

*   Training generative models on their own outputs can lead to "model collapse," a progressive degradation of performance.
*   Previous studies often assumed data replacement, which tends to cause model collapse.
*   Accumulating synthetic data with original real data, rather than replacing it, avoids model collapse across various model types (transformers, diffusion models, VAEs) and data modalities (text, molecules, images).
*   Theoretical analysis of linear models confirms that data accumulation leads to a finite upper bound on test error, preventing collapse, while data replacement results in linearly increasing test error.
*   The findings suggest that data accumulation is a viable strategy to mitigate the "curse of recursion" in generative model training.

## Contribution to Research Frame

This source directly addresses the core concern of the research frame: the technical validity and manageability of model collapse. It provides strong empirical and theoretical evidence that model collapse is *not* an inevitable mathematical certainty, particularly under the condition of data accumulation.

*   **Challenges "inherent mathematical certainty":** The paper demonstrates that the "curse of recursion" can be broken by a specific data handling strategy (accumulation), directly countering the idea that collapse is an unavoidable outcome of recursive training.
*   **Identifies mitigation strategies:** Data accumulation is presented as a key mitigation strategy. The theoretical framework provides a mathematical basis for *why* it works, linking it to the bounded nature of test error.
*   **Refines understanding of "distributional drift":** While acknowledging that performance can degrade, the paper shows that accumulation prevents the *unbounded* degradation characteristic of collapse. It implies that the loss of statistical information is managed rather than catastrophic when real and synthetic data are combined.
*   **Supports "manageable engineering challenge":** By showing that a practical data management technique (accumulation) prevents collapse, the paper frames the issue as an engineering challenge that can be overcome with appropriate methods, rather than a fundamental theoretical limitation.
*   **Nuances "anchor data":** The research implicitly supports the idea of maintaining "anchor" (real) data. The accumulation strategy ensures that the original real data remains part of the training set, preventing the model from solely relying on potentially degraded synthetic data. The theoretical analysis quantifies how the influence of new synthetic data diminishes over iterations in the accumulation setting, effectively preserving the integrity of the original data distribution's influence.
