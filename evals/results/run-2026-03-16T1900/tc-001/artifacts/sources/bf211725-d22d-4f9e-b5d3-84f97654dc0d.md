# How Bad is Training on Synthetic Data? A Statistical Analysis of Language Model Collapse

## Abstract
This paper investigates the phenomenon of "model collapse," where training on synthetic data generated by previous models leads to performance degradation. The authors propose a statistical model to analyze recursive training scenarios, concluding that collapse is unavoidable when training solely on synthetic data. However, they demonstrate that by mixing real and synthetic data, there's a maximal amount of synthetic data that can be used before collapse becomes inevitable. These theoretical findings are supported by empirical validation.

## Key Claims
*   Model collapse, characterized by performance deterioration due to recursive training on synthetic data, is a significant concern for future language models.
*   Training exclusively on synthetic data inevitably leads to model collapse, where the model forgets the original data distribution and produces limited or repetitive outputs.
*   When training on a mixture of real and synthetic data, it is possible to avoid model collapse up to a certain threshold of synthetic data.
*   The rate of collapse in the "fully synthetic" scenario is dependent on the number of generations, the size of the synthetic dataset used per generation, and the diversity of the initial distribution.
*   In the "partially synthetic" scenario, incorporating real data can significantly attenuate or avoid model collapse, with the amount of synthetic data needing to be considerably smaller than the original data.

## Contribution to Research Frame
This source directly addresses the core of the research frame by providing a rigorous statistical and empirical analysis of model collapse. It quantifies the mechanisms by which training on synthetic data degrades model performance, specifically focusing on the loss of information from the "tails" of the original distribution. The paper distinguishes between the inevitable collapse in fully synthetic training and the potentially avoidable collapse in partially synthetic training, offering theoretical bounds and empirical evidence for the critical ratio of real to synthetic data. This directly contributes to understanding the "distributional drift" and identifying "mitigation strategies" such as data mixing. The findings suggest that while collapse is a theoretically grounded phenomenon, its practical occurrence is highly dependent on the training regime, aligning with the frame's distinction between theoretical inevitability and empirical manageability.
