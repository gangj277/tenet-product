## Summary

This research investigates the phenomenon of "model collapse" in generative models, focusing on how repeatedly training on synthetic data can degrade model performance. The project aims to synthesize existing literature to ascertain whether model collapse is an unavoidable mathematical outcome or a problem that can be managed through engineering solutions. Key areas of focus include the relationship between real and synthetic data distributions, identifying thresholds for collapse, and exploring mitigation strategies like data mixing and architectural adjustments to preserve tail-end data and prevent convergence to a low-variance mean.

## Key Claims

*   Recursive training on synthetic data leads to a degradation of generative model performance.
*   Model collapse is characterized by a loss of diversity and tail-end representation in generated data.
*   The interplay between real and synthetic data distributions is crucial in understanding and preventing model collapse.
*   Specific thresholds and mitigation strategies, such as data mixing and architectural adjustments, can prevent or delay model collapse.
*   The research seeks to determine if model collapse is an inherent mathematical certainty or an engineering challenge.
