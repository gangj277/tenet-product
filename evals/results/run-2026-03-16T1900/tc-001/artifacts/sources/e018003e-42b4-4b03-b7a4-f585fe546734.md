# A Theoretical Basis for Model Collapse in Recursive Training

**Publication:** arXiv.org (Preprint)
**Author:** Vivek S. Borkar
**Date:** Not specified (Preprint)

## Summary

This paper provides a theoretical framework for understanding "model collapse" in recursive training, a phenomenon where generative models trained on their own synthetic data can degrade over time. The author distinguishes between two scenarios: "persistent excitation" (where fresh, external data is continuously injected) and training without such injection. In the former case, the system exhibits "degeneration," where the mean behavior is preserved but the samples become noisier. In the latter case, the model undergoes "total collapse," converging to a random Dirac measure, effectively losing all distributional information beyond the mean. The analysis utilizes advanced probability tools, including weak convergence of probability measures, martingales, and Markov chains on Polish spaces.

## Key Claims

*   Recursive training of generative models can lead to "model collapse," where the simulated probability distribution degrades.
*   The asymptotic behavior of recursive training differs based on whether external data is injected.
*   **Persistent excitation** (injection of fresh data) leads to "degeneration," preserving the mean but increasing noise.
*   Training **without continued injection of fresh data** leads to "total collapse," where the distribution converges to a random Dirac measure.
*   The analysis is performed in a general Polish space, accommodating function spaces.
*   Mathematical tools used include weak convergence of probability measures, discrete parameter martingales, and Markov chains on Polish spaces.
*   In the absence of persistent excitation, the measure-valued Markov chain converges to a random Dirac measure on the space of probability measures.

## Relevant Evidence/Quotes

*   "It is known that recursive training from generative models can lead to the so called 'collapse' of the simulated probability distribution."
*   "This note shows that one in fact gets two different asymptotic behaviours depending on whether an external source, howsoever minor, is also contributing samples."
*   "1. **Persistent excitation**: Injection of fresh data at every step over and above the recursive generation of data. This leads to a phenomenon called 'degeneration'."
*   "2. **Without continued injection of fresh data**: This leads to a collapse."
*   "In the former case, the measure-valued Markov chain on $P(S)$ degenerates into a random Dirac measure on $P(S)$."
*   "In the latter case, they asymptotically form a stationary Markov chain whose stationary distribution has its barycenter at the law of samples generated by the natural source."

## Contribution to/Challenge of Research Frame

This source directly addresses the core concept of "model collapse" by providing a rigorous mathematical foundation for its occurrence. It offers a crucial distinction between two fundamental scenarios (with and without external data injection), which directly informs the research frame's focus on "distributional drift" and the "interplay between real and synthetic data distributions."

Specifically:

*   **Supports:** The paper's distinction between "degeneration" (with external data) and "collapse" (without) provides a theoretical basis for the research frame's hypothesis that maintaining a "specific ratio of high-quality 'anchor' (real) data" is key to preventing collapse. The "degeneration" described aligns with the idea of preserving the mean while experiencing some form of degradation, potentially related to the loss of tail-end data. The "total collapse" scenario strongly supports the idea of "theoretical inevitability" in closed-loop systems without external input.
*   **Challenges:** The paper's focus is on the *theoretical inevitability* of collapse under specific conditions, rather than the *empirical manageability* or *mitigation strategies* mentioned in the research frame's summary. While it identifies the conditions for collapse, it doesn't delve into specific thresholds or architectural adjustments as mitigation techniques, which are central to the research frame's intent. The paper's "degeneration" might be interpreted as a form of "manageable" degradation, but the "collapse" is presented as a more fundamental outcome.
